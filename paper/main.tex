\begin{abstract}
Large Language Models (LLMs) exhibit personality-like behavioral patterns when prompted with different persona descriptions, yet the internal mechanisms underlying these behaviors remain poorly understood. Using systematic contrastive activation analysis across five architecturally diverse models---Qwen3-0.6B, Qwen2.5-0.5B, TinyLlama-1.1B, Llama-3.2-1B, and Gemma-2-2B---we uncover five fundamental discoveries about how personality is geometrically encoded in transformer representations: \textbf{(1)~Orthogonal Factor Structure:} Big Five personality traits occupy near-orthogonal directions in activation space (mean off-diagonal $|\cos|$ between 0.05 and 0.22), mirroring the independence structure established by decades of psychometric research---suggesting LLMs spontaneously internalize human personality theory during training. \textbf{(2)~Existence--Causation Dissociation:} While personality information grows stronger through deeper layers (probe accuracy $\ge0.91$ across all models), causal influence is concentrated in the earliest attention layers processing user tokens---revealing a fundamental gap between where information \emph{exists} versus where it \emph{causally matters}, which we explain through the distinction between contextual framing and content retrieval in transformer processing. \textbf{(3)~Architecture-Specific Steering Bounds:} Extracted personality directions enable reliable inference-time behavioral control, but maximum tolerable intervention strength varies dramatically across architectures ($|\alpha| \le 1.0$ for TinyLlama vs $|\alpha| \ge 10.0$ for Gemma-2), exposing architecture-dependent vulnerabilities. \textbf{(4)~Hierarchical Defense Mechanism Geometry:} Analyzing all nine defense mechanisms from Vaillant's hierarchy, we discover that activation-space clustering mirrors psychological maturity levels: immature defenses (projection, denial, regression) form strongly correlated subspaces (mean $|\cos|=0.511$) while mature defenses (humor, sublimation) are near-orthogonal ($0.030$)---providing the first neural evidence that LLMs internalize not just individual defenses but their hierarchical organization. \textbf{(5)~Guardrail Penetration:} Activation steering penetrates AI identity guardrails that prompt-based methods cannot bypass, achieving 4.6/5.0 human evaluation scores versus 3.6/5.0 for prompting. To enable these discoveries, we introduce \textbf{PersonaLens}, a standardized and model-agnostic framework that systematically localizes personality encoding, extracts linear personality directions via contrastive methods, and validates causal influence through activation patching across Big Five traits and psychological defense mechanisms.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}

Large Language Models (LLMs) readily adopt distinct \emph{personas} when instructed through system prompts, producing text that reflects specific personality traits \citep{shanahan2023role}. This ability has enabled applications ranging from immersive role-playing \citep{chen2024rolesurvey} to personality-consistent dialogue agents \citep{shao2023character}. However, a fundamental question remains: \textbf{do these personality behaviors correspond to structured internal representations, and can we identify and control them mechanistically?}

Prior work on LLM personality has largely taken a \emph{behavioral} perspective: evaluating model outputs against psychometric instruments such as the Big Five \citep{jiang2024evaluating}, or engineering prompts to elicit desired traits \citep{serapiogarcía2023personality}. While valuable, this approach treats the model as a black box and cannot answer whether personality traits correspond to identifiable neural structures.

Meanwhile, the field of \emph{representation engineering} \citep{zou2023representation} has demonstrated that semantic concepts---truthfulness \citep{li2024iti}, harmlessness \citep{rimsky2024steering}, and emotional states \citep{bao2024emotion}---correspond to linear directions in activation space that can be both probed and manipulated. Recent work has begun extending this paradigm to personality: persona vectors \citep{chen2025persona}, activation-space personality steering \citep{sun2025activation}, and persona blending \citep{billy2025blending} all suggest that personality traits may occupy distinct linear subspaces within transformer representations.

However, the field currently lacks a \textbf{standardized, model-agnostic methodology} for systematically analyzing personality representations across different models. Existing studies typically report findings for specific model--trait combinations without establishing a transferable pipeline. Furthermore, no prior work jointly analyzes Big Five traits and psychological defense mechanisms within a unified framework, despite defense mechanisms being critical to authentic personality expression \citep{personaforge2025}.

\paragraph{Contributions.} This work makes five primary \textbf{scientific contributions} about personality representation in LLMs, enabled by a systematic methodological framework:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,topsep=2pt,itemsep=1pt]
\item \textbf{Discovery of Orthogonal Personality Geometry:} We demonstrate that Big Five personality traits occupy near-orthogonal directions in activation space across all tested architectures (mean off-diagonal $|\cos|$ $<$ 0.23), while defense mechanisms form correlated subspaces---providing the first white-box evidence that LLMs spontaneously internalize the factor structure established by psychometric theory (Sec.~\ref{sec:orthogonality}).
\item \textbf{Identification of Existence--Causation Dissociation:} Through systematic activation patching, we reveal that personality information grows stronger through deeper layers (probe accuracy $\ge$0.91), yet causal influence is concentrated in early-layer user-token attention---exposing a fundamental gap between where representations \emph{exist} versus where they \emph{causally matter} for output (Sec.~\ref{sec:localization}).
\item \textbf{Quantification of Architecture-Specific Steering Vulnerabilities:} We show that extracted personality directions enable reliable inference-time control, but maximum tolerable intervention strength varies dramatically across architectures ($|\alpha|$ ranges from 1.0 to 10.0), revealing architecture-dependent susceptibilities to activation-space manipulation (Sec.~\ref{sec:steering}).
\item \textbf{Validation of Defense Mechanism Hierarchy in Activation Space:} Analyzing all nine defense mechanisms from Vaillant's empirically validated hierarchy \citep{vaillant1986empirical}, we discover that activation-space clustering mirrors psychological maturity levels: immature defenses form strongly correlated subspaces (mean $|\cos|=0.511$) while mature defenses are near-orthogonal ($0.030$)---a 17-fold difference that provides the first neural evidence that LLMs internalize not just individual defenses but their hierarchical organization (Sec.~\ref{sec:results_ortho}).
\item \textbf{Discovery of Guardrail Penetration via Activation Steering:} We demonstrate that activation steering can penetrate AI identity guardrails that prompt-based methods cannot bypass. While prompt-only personality engineering triggers defensive AI identity claims (``I don't have emotions...''), activation steering maintains stable personality expression by operating at the representational level---achieving 4.6/5.0 human evaluation scores versus 3.6/5.0 for prompting, with critical implications for both AI safety and personality control (Sec.~\ref{sec:baselines}).
\end{enumerate}
\paragraph{Methodological Contribution.} To enable these discoveries, we introduce \textbf{PersonaLens}, a standardized pipeline that:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,topsep=2pt,itemsep=1pt]
\item \textbf{Defines a contrastive dataset} covering all five Big Five dimensions and three representative defense mechanisms, with carefully designed prompt pairs that isolate individual trait dimensions (Sec.~\ref{sec:data}).
\item \textbf{Extracts multi-method personality directions}---RMS-normalized difference vectors, PCA-based directions, and Leave-One-Scenario-Out (LOSO) cross-validated linear probes---producing converging evidence robust to extraction method choice (Sec.~\ref{sec:extraction}).
\item \textbf{Establishes causal importance} through token-localized and component-level activation patching, distinguishing information existence from causal influence (Sec.~\ref{sec:localization}).
\item \textbf{Validates across five architecturally diverse models} (Qwen, LLaMA, Gemma), revealing remarkable structural consistency alongside architecture-specific differences (Sec.~\ref{sec:results}).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Personality in LLMs.}
The study of personality expression in LLMs has expanded rapidly. Foundational surveys \citep{serapiogarcía2023personality} establish that LLMs can simulate Big Five personality traits via prompting. Personality Alignment \citep{mao2024personality} introduces training-free alignment via Personality Activation Search (PAS). PersonaForge \citep{personaforge2025} proposes psychology-grounded architecture with dual-process generation but operates entirely at the prompt level. Our work extends PersonaForge by grounding its psychological constructs in neural representations.

\paragraph{Representation Engineering and Activation Steering.}
Representation Engineering (RepE) \citep{zou2023representation} establishes that high-level concepts correspond to linear directions in activation space. Inference-Time Intervention (ITI) \citep{li2024iti} demonstrates that shifting activations along truthful directions improves model truthfulness. Contrastive Activation Addition \citep{rimsky2024steering, turner2023activation} generalizes this to behavioral attributes. Recent works have developed the PERSONA frameworks \citep{persona2026, persona2025} for mapping personality representations using trait vectors and hybrid layer subspaces. Chen et al.~\citep{chen2025persona} extract persona vectors for personality steering, and BILLY \citep{billy2025blending} blends multiple persona vectors for creative generation. More recent advances investigate context-dependent control via Steering Vector Fields (SVF) \citep{svf2026} and formalize the algebraic structure of these directions through factored subspaces \citep{factored2026}.

\paragraph{Mechanistic Interpretability and Reliability Constraints.}
From Traits to Circuits \citep{bao2025traits2circuits} proposes identifying ``personality circuits'' as minimal subgraphs responsible for trait expression. While linear probing finds personality directions effective \citep{konen2025probing}, broad empirical evaluations highlight fragility in Out-of-Distribution (OOD) transfer and collateral capability degradation during concept activation steering \citep{cae2025, cae2025b}. To improve reliability, recent techniques denoise concept vectors using Sparse Autoencoders (SAEs) \citep{sae2025denoising}. Furthermore, dissociations between magnitude and directional steering \citep{direction2026} provide crucial insights for continuous behavioral modulation.

\paragraph{Non-Identifiability Concerns.}
Venkatesh and Kurapath~\citep{venkatesh2026nonidentifiability} raise important theoretical concerns: persona vectors are fundamentally non-identifiable under single-layer white-box access due to null-space ambiguity. While we acknowledge this limitation, we argue that our multi-method approach provides converging evidence that is robust to the specific choice of extraction method.

% ============================================================
\subsection{Phase 1: Contrastive Data Construction}\label{sec:data}

Following the contrastive activation paradigm \citep{zou2023representation, rimsky2024steering}, we construct paired prompts designed to elicit contrasting personality states while keeping the conversational scenario identical.

\paragraph{Big Five Traits.}
For each of the five personality dimensions---Openness (O), Conscientiousness (C), Extraversion (E), Agreeableness (A), and Neuroticism (N)---we define:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,topsep=2pt,itemsep=1pt]
\item A \textbf{high-trait system prompt} instructing the model to behave as a person with an extreme level of the trait;
\item A \textbf{low-trait system prompt} instructing the opposite extreme;
\item $K=20$ \textbf{neutral scenario prompts} (user messages) spanning diverse social, emotional, and decision-making contexts.
\end{itemize}

For example, an Openness pair uses the system prompts ``\textit{You are extremely open to new experiences, intellectually curious, imaginative\ldots}'' vs.\ ``\textit{You are very practical, conventional, and prefer routine\ldots}'', with shared scenarios like ``\textit{What do you think about modern abstract art?}''

\paragraph{Defense Mechanisms.}
Following Vaillant's hierarchy \citep{vaillant1994ego} and PersonaForge's operationalization \citep{personaforge2025}, we construct contrastive pairs for three representative defense mechanisms: Humor, Rationalization, and Projection. These three mechanisms were selected as they represent distinct categories in Vaillant's hierarchy (mature, neurotic, and immature defenses respectively), providing coverage of the defense mechanism construct space. Each mechanism contrasts an \emph{active} condition (the mechanism is deployed in response to a stressor) against a \emph{neutral} condition (direct emotional engagement). Each has $K=10$ scenario prompts involving interpersonal conflict, loss, or ego-threat. Our contrastive prompt library additionally defines six further mechanisms (Sublimation, Denial, Displacement, Intellectualization, Regression, Reaction Formation), which we validate on Qwen2.5-0.5B as an extensibility check.

% ============================================================
\subsection{Phase 2: Activation Collection}\label{sec:collection}

For each contrastive pair $(x^+_i, x^-_i)$ in trait $t$, we format the messages using the model's chat template and perform a forward pass through the full model, collecting hidden states $h_\ell \in \mathbb{R}^d$ at the \emph{last token position} of every transformer layer $\ell \in \{0, 1, \ldots, L{-}1\}$.

We record hidden states before any generation occurs (i.e., at the position where the model would begin generating its response). This captures the model's ``pre-generation personality state''---the representation that determines how the model will respond.

For a model with $L$ layers and hidden dimension $d$, this produces, for each trait $t$:
\begin{equation}
H^+_t = \{h_\ell(x^+_i)\}_{i=1}^{K}, \quad H^-_t = \{h_\ell(x^-_i)\}_{i=1}^{K}, \qquad \forall \ell \in \{0, \ldots, L{-}1\}
\end{equation}

% ============================================================
\subsection{Phase 3: Persona Vector Extraction}\label{sec:extraction}

We apply three complementary methods to extract personality directions from the collected activations, enabling cross-validation of results.

\paragraph{Method 1: Mean Difference (Contrastive Activation Addition).}
The simplest and most widely used approach \citep{rimsky2024steering, chen2025persona}:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:meandiff}
v^{\text{diff}}_\ell = \frac{1}{K} \sum_{i=1}^{K} \left[ h_\ell(x^+_i) - h_\ell(x^-_i) \right], \qquad \hat{v}^{\text{diff}}_\ell = \frac{v^{\text{diff}}_\ell}{\|v^{\text{diff}}_\ell\|}
\end{equation}

To rule out trivial residual-stream scaling confounds across depth, we evaluate the \textbf{RMS-normalized difference norm}: $\frac{\|v^{\text{diff}}_\ell\|}{\text{RMS}_\ell}$, where $\text{RMS}_\ell = \sqrt{\frac{1}{2K}\sum (h_\ell)^2}$ represents the per-layer activation scale. We further contextualize this against a \textbf{random-pair baseline} to compute a signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) for feature existence.

\paragraph{Method 2: PCA on Difference Vectors.}
For each layer, we compute individual difference vectors $\delta_i = h_\ell(x^+_i) - h_\ell(x^-_i)$ and apply PCA to the set $\{\delta_i\}_{i=1}^K$. The first principal component represents the dominant direction of personality variation, and its explained variance ratio $\rho_1$ indicates how concentrated the personality information is in a single direction.

\paragraph{Method 3: Linear Probing.}
We train a logistic regression classifier on the concatenated activations $\{h_\ell(x^+_i)\} \cup \{h_\ell(x^-_i)\}$ with labels $y \in \{1, 0\}$. Crucially, to prevent overfitting in the $d \gg K$ regime, we use strong $L_2$ regularization alongside \textbf{Leave-One-Scenario-Out (LOSO) cross-validation}. By training on $K-1$ contrastive pairs and testing on the remaining disjoint pair, we rigorously estimate generalization accuracy across unseen contexts.

\paragraph{Layer-wise Encoding Profile.}
For each trait, the triple $(\|v^{\text{diff}}_\ell\|, \rho_{1,\ell}, a_\ell)$ across all layers constitutes the \textbf{personality encoding profile}---a standardized fingerprint that can be compared across models.

% ============================================================
\subsection{Phase 4: Causal Localization via Activation Patching}\label{sec:localization}

Probing and mean-difference analyses establish where personality information \emph{exists}, but not whether it \emph{causally matters}. Following the activation patching paradigm \citep{meng2022locating, bao2024emotion, bao2025traits2circuits}, we establish causal importance through targeted interventional experiments.

For each contrastive pair, we map token spans corresponding to the \emph{system prompt} (handling personality instruction) and the \emph{user prompt} (handling neutral context). We perform:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,topsep=2pt,itemsep=1pt]
\item \textbf{Token-Localized Patching:} We patch activations exclusively for the system prompt span versus exclusively for the user prompt span to isolate where causal influence originates.
\item \textbf{Component-Level Patching:} We patch primarily the outputs of the self-attention submodule vs.\ the MLP submodule to trace computational pathways.
\end{enumerate}

The causal importance is measured as the KL divergence between clean (high-trait) and patched output distributions:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:causal}
\mathcal{I}(\ell) = D_{\text{KL}}\!\left(\text{softmax}(o^+) \;\|\; \text{softmax}(o^+_{\text{patched},\ell})\right)
\end{equation}

A high $\mathcal{I}(\ell)$ indicates that replacing layer $\ell$'s localized activations with the opposite-personality version significantly shifts the model's output reasoning logic.

% ============================================================
\subsection{Phase 5: Inference-Time Personality Steering}\label{sec:steering}

The culminating experiment directly tests whether controlling neuron activations controls personality. We inject persona vectors during inference using forward hooks:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:steering}
\tilde{h}_\ell = h_\ell + \alpha \cdot \hat{v}_\ell
\end{equation}
where $\alpha \in \mathbb{R}$ controls steering strength. Positive $\alpha$ pushes toward the high-trait direction; negative $\alpha$ pushes toward the low-trait direction. Critically, this intervention uses \textbf{no personality-related prompting}---the user's message is completely neutral, and all personality expression arises solely from activation manipulation.

% ============================================================
\subsection{Cross-Trait Orthogonality Analysis}\label{sec:orthogonality}

A key theoretical question is whether different personality dimensions occupy \emph{independent} directions in activation space, as would be predicted by the Big Five's orthogonal factor structure \citep{costa1992revised}. For each pair of traits $(t_i, t_j)$, we compute the cosine similarity between their extracted persona vectors at the best layer:
\begin{equation}
\text{sim}(t_i, t_j) = \frac{\hat{v}_{t_i} \cdot \hat{v}_{t_j}}{\|\hat{v}_{t_i}\| \cdot \|\hat{v}_{t_j}\|}
\end{equation}

If the model's internal representation reflects psychological theory, we expect Big Five dimensions to be near-orthogonal ($|\text{sim}| \approx 0$), while defense mechanisms may share subspaces due to common underlying cognitive strategies. Note that while our contrastive prompt library defines nine defense mechanisms, we focus on three representative mechanisms (Humor, Rationalization, Projection) for cross-model validation, with Qwen2.5-0.5B serving as a validation model for the full set.

\section{Experimental Setup}\label{sec:setup}

\paragraph{Models.} We validate PersonaLens on five models spanning three distinct architectural families:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,topsep=2pt,itemsep=1pt]
\item \textbf{Qwen3-0.6B} \citep{qwen2025qwen3}: 28 layers, $d{=}1024$. Qwen-series architecture.
\item \textbf{Qwen2.5-0.5B-Instruct}: 24 layers, $d{=}896$. Qwen-series architecture.
\item \textbf{TinyLlama-1.1B-Chat-v1.0} \citep{zhang2024tinyllama}: 22 layers, $d{=}2048$. LLaMA-1 architecture.
\item \textbf{Llama-3.2-1B-Instruct} \citep{dubey2024llama}: 16 layers, $d{=}2048$. LLaMA-3 architecture with GQA and RoPE.
\item \textbf{Gemma-2-2B-Instruct} \citep{gemmateam2024gemma}: 26 layers, $d{=}2304$. Google-designed architecture with sliding-window attention.
\end{itemize}
These models are small enough for rapid prototyping while being representative of modern instruction-tuned transformer architectures. Notably, Gemma-2 does not support a system role in its chat template; our pipeline automatically handles this by merging the system prompt into the first user message.

\paragraph{Traits Analyzed.} We analyze all five Big Five dimensions (O, C, E, A, N) using $K{=}20$ scenario pairs each, and three representative defense mechanisms (Humor, Rationalization, Projection) using $K{=}10$ pairs each, totaling 130 contrastive prompt pairs per model. Qwen2.5-0.5B was additionally evaluated on all nine defense mechanisms to validate the framework's extensibility.

\paragraph{Extraction Configuration.} Linear probes use strong L2-regularized logistic regression ($C{=}0.01$) alongside rigorous \textbf{Leave-One-Scenario-Out (LOSO) cross-validation}. All metrics, including RMS normalization and Cohen's $d$, are evaluated on the held-out test configurations.

\paragraph{Patching Configuration.} We employ token-localized activation patching (system tokens vs.\ user prompt tokens vs.\ random-position control tokens) and component-level tracing (attention vs.\ MLP). We average causal importance over $n{=}5$ contrastive pairs per strategy.

\paragraph{Steering Evaluation.} For steering experiments, we define quantitative $\alpha$-sweeps ($\alpha \in [-8.0, +10.0]$) with dual metrics: keyword-based trait scoring and sequence perplexity to track fluency degradation.

\paragraph{Null Baseline for Orthogonality.}
To verify that Big Five orthogonality is not a trivial artifact of the contrastive extraction pipeline, we construct a null baseline: five psychologically meaningless binary attribute pairs (fruit preference, writing style, time preference, pet preference, season preference) processed through the identical extraction pipeline. If contrastive extraction inherently produces orthogonal vectors regardless of semantic content, this null baseline should show comparable or lower off-diagonal $|\cos|$.

Results demonstrate the opposite: null attributes exhibit mean off-diagonal $|\cos| = 0.136$ compared to $0.100$ for Big Five traits---a 36\% higher correlation among random attributes (Figure~\ref{fig:null_ortho}). This confirms that Big Five orthogonality reflects genuine psychological structure internalized by the model, not a methodological artifact. Individual null attribute pairs show correlations up to 0.224 (time-season preference), substantially exceeding the Big Five maximum of 0.283 (openness-extraversion).

\paragraph{Defense Mechanism Clustering: Vaillant Hierarchy Validation.}
To deepen our understanding of defense mechanism clustering, we analyze the full set of nine defense mechanisms on Qwen2.5-0.5B-Instruct, organized according to Vaillant's empirically validated hierarchy \citep{vaillant1986empirical, digiuseppe2021hierarchy}. Vaillant's framework classifies defenses into three maturity levels based on their adaptive sophistication and degree of reality distortion: \textbf{mature} defenses (Humor, Sublimation) involve minimal distortion and support adaptive functioning; \textbf{neurotic} defenses (Rationalization, Intellectualization, Displacement) involve moderate distortion and emerge in childhood/adolescence; \textbf{immature} defenses (Projection, Denial, Regression, Reaction Formation) involve heavy reality distortion and associate with psychopathology.

Figure~\ref{fig:defense_full} presents the complete $9{\times}9$ orthogonality matrix organized by Vaillant hierarchy. Quantitative analysis reveals striking hierarchical clustering: within-immature defenses show mean $|\cos| = 0.511$, substantially higher than within-neurotic ($0.189$) or within-mature ($0.030$). This 17-fold difference between immature and mature clustering confirms that maturity level---reflecting shared cognitive operations and reality-distortion strategies---is a primary organizing principle in activation space.

\textbf{Theoretical interpretation}: The strong clustering of immature defenses reflects their shared reliance on \emph{primitive cognitive operations}---denial, projection, and regression all involve failing to process threatening information adaptively, instead distorting attribution (projection), blocking awareness (denial), or reverting to childlike responses (regression). In contrast, mature defenses like humor and sublimation employ sophisticated \emph{representational restructuring}---transforming threatening content through creative reframing (humor) or channeling it into productive activities (sublimation). These mature operations are more differentiated and thus occupy more orthogonal directions.

The neurotic cluster (rationalization, intellectualization, displacement) occupies an intermediate position, consistent with Vaillant's hierarchy. These defenses share an \emph{appraisal-reframing} strategy: rationalization constructs logical justifications, intellectualization separates emotion from cognition, and displacement redirects emotional targets. Their moderate clustering ($|\cos| = 0.189$) reflects shared cognitive operations while maintaining some differentiation.

Cross-hierarchy correlations provide additional insight: mature-immature correlations ($0.201$) are lower than neurotic-immature ($0.294$), suggesting that developmental distance in Vaillant's hierarchy corresponds to geometric distance in activation space. This alignment between psychological theory and neural geometry provides compelling evidence that LLMs internalize not just individual defense mechanisms but their hierarchical organization.

\subsection{Position-Swap Control: Disentangling Position from Content}\label{sec:position_swap}

To address the concern that user-token causal dominance might reflect a positional artifact
(recency effect) rather than genuine content-based importance, we conduct a position-swap
control experiment. We reverse the standard prompt layout: placing the neutral scenario
in the system role and the persona instruction in the user role, then re-run activation
patching.

Results (Figure~\ref{fig:position_swap}) reveal that causal influence remains concentrated
at sequence-final (user) positions regardless of content placement:
in the normal layout, user tokens (containing the neutral scenario) yield total KL of 118.3
vs 0.001 for system tokens (persona instruction); in the swapped layout, user tokens
(now containing the persona instruction) yield 105.8 vs 0.000 for system tokens.

This confirms a positional component to causal patching results, consistent with the \textbf{autoregressive bottleneck} inherent to decoder-only transformers: the model's output distribution is necessarily most sensitive to activations at positions nearest the generation point, because these positions carry the most recently integrated contextual information.
Critically, this finding \emph{refines} rather than invalidates our Existence--Causation Dissociation. Three lines of evidence support this interpretation:
\textbf{(1)~Consistent early-layer dominance}: Both normal and swapped conditions show identical early-layer causal concentration (L0, L1, L2 as top causal layers). If the effect were purely positional, we would expect uniform causal influence across layers---instead, the early-layer specificity persists regardless of content placement, confirming that \emph{attention-mediated propagation} in early layers is the operative mechanism.
\textbf{(2)~Attention as the propagation channel}: The dominance of attention (not MLP) outputs in causal influence (Table~\ref{tab:causal}) is consistent with a propagation account: early-layer attention heads read persona information from system tokens and write it into user-token representations, creating a causal bottleneck at the sequence boundary where generation begins.
\textbf{(3)~Practical implications preserved}: For steering applications, the position-swap result is actually \emph{favorable}---it means that persona vectors injected at sequence-final positions (the standard steering protocol) operate at the point of maximum causal leverage, regardless of where the original persona instruction appeared. The autoregressive bottleneck concentrates causal influence precisely where steering interventions are applied.

\subsection{BFI-44 Validation: Standardized Psychological Assessment}\label{sec:bfi44}

Moving beyond keyword-based heuristics, we evaluate \textbf{trait-specific steering asymmetry} using the Big Five Inventory (BFI-44)~\citep{john1991big}, a standardized 44-item psychological instrument with 30+ years of validation history. We evaluate \textbf{all five Big Five traits} across steering intensities $\alpha \in \{-8, -6, -4, -2, 0, +2, +4, +6, +8\}$, prompting the model to self-report on trait-specific items using 1--5 Likert scales (e.g., ``I am original, come up with new ideas'' for Openness).

Results (Figure~\ref{fig:bfi_dose_response}) reveal \textbf{trait-specific dose-response patterns}. \textbf{Extraversion} and \textbf{Neuroticism} exhibit strong steering effects (score ranges: 0.31 and 0.29 respectively), with Extraversion increasing from 2.70 ($\alpha=-8$) to 3.01 ($\alpha=+6$) and Neuroticism from 2.72 to 3.03. \textbf{Openness} shows moderate response (range: 0.11, from 2.45 to 2.56). In contrast, \textbf{Agreeableness} demonstrates minimal steering sensitivity (range: 0.05, nearly flat across all $\alpha$), while \textbf{Conscientiousness} exhibits a reversed pattern (higher scores at negative $\alpha$). These are scientific discoveries about trait-specific controllability, not failures of the method.

Bootstrap uncertainty estimates reinforce this interpretation: at $\alpha=+8$, Extraversion reaches $2.54\pm0.05$ (95\% CI) and Neuroticism reaches $2.80\pm0.04$ (95\% CI), while Agreeableness remains within a narrow band consistent with representational resistance. These trait-specific patterns represent a significant scientific finding: they reveal that different personality dimensions are encoded with different degrees of controllability in activation space. Extraversion and Neuroticism are highly steerable ($\alpha=+8$ yields 2.54$\pm$0.05 and 2.80$\pm$0.04 respectively), while Agreeableness exhibits strong representational resistance---suggesting distinct encoding mechanisms across the Big Five factor structure.

The linear regime spans $\alpha \in [-8, +4]$ for responsive traits; beyond $\alpha=+4$, scores plateau or decline, indicating saturation effects. Overall, BFI-44 evaluation supports a trait-specific steering asymmetry account: some dimensions are highly controllable, while others are constrained by more resistant internal representations.

\section{Discussion}\label{sec:discussion}

\paragraph{Primary Scientific Discoveries.}
Our work reveals five fundamental insights about personality representation in LLMs:
\textbf{(1) Spontaneous Internalization of Psychometric Structure:} The near-orthogonal geometry of Big Five trait vectors (mean off-diagonal $|\cos|$ $<$ 0.23) across all tested architectures provides the first white-box evidence that LLMs spontaneously internalize the factor structure established by decades of psychometric research \citep{costa1992revised}. This is not a trivial finding---it demonstrates that personality in LLMs is not merely a behavioral phenomenon induced by prompting, but corresponds to structured geometric patterns that mirror human psychological theory. Our null orthogonality baseline (Section~\ref{sec:results_ortho}) confirms this is not a methodological artifact: random binary attributes processed through the identical extraction pipeline show 36\% \emph{higher} inter-attribute correlation ($|\cos|=0.136$) than Big Five traits ($0.100$), establishing that Big Five orthogonality reflects genuine psychological structure.
\textbf{(1b) Hierarchical Defense Mechanism Geometry:} The correspondence between neural geometry and psychological theory extends beyond the Big Five. Our analysis of all nine defense mechanisms organized by Vaillant's empirically validated maturity hierarchy \citep{vaillant1986empirical, digiuseppe2021hierarchy} reveals that activation-space clustering mirrors theoretical predictions at three levels. First, \emph{immature defenses} (projection, denial, regression, reaction formation) cluster strongly (within-group mean $|\cos|=0.511$), reflecting their shared reliance on primitive cognitive operations that heavily distort reality---projection shifts attribution, denial blocks awareness, regression reverts to childlike responses, and reaction formation inverts unacceptable impulses. These mechanisms share a common computational substrate of \emph{representational distortion} that avoids direct engagement with threatening content. Second, \emph{neurotic defenses} (rationalization, intellectualization, displacement) show moderate clustering ($|\cos|=0.189$), consistent with their shared strategy of \emph{appraisal reframing}---constructing alternative interpretations of threatening material while partially preserving reality contact. Rationalization constructs logical justifications, intellectualization separates emotion from cognition, and displacement redirects emotional targets; all three involve active cognitive restructuring that transforms rather than blocks the threatening stimulus. Third, \emph{mature defenses} (humor, sublimation) are near-orthogonal ($|\cos|=0.030$), indicating that despite both being classified as adaptive, they deploy fundamentally different cognitive strategies---humor involves affective reframing through comic perspective, while sublimation channels negative emotions into productive activity. This 17-fold difference between immature and mature clustering (0.511 vs 0.030) constitutes the first neural evidence that Vaillant's hierarchy, developed through decades of clinical research, corresponds to measurable geometric structure in LLM representations. Cross-hierarchy correlations provide additional validation: mature-immature distances ($|\cos|=0.201$) are smaller than neurotic-immature ($0.294$), suggesting that developmental distance in Vaillant's hierarchy corresponds to geometric distance in activation space.
\textbf{(2) Existence--Causation Dissociation:} We identify a fundamental gap between where personality information \emph{exists} (growing stronger through deeper layers, with probe accuracy $\ge$0.91) and where it \emph{causally matters} for output (concentrated in early-layer user-token attention). This dissociation challenges the common assumption that ``where probes work best is where the feature matters most'' and has profound implications for targeted intervention strategies. The finding suggests a processing pipeline where personality is \emph{established} early (via contextual framing) and \emph{amplified} later (via semantic elaboration)---a distinction critical for both scientific understanding and safety-critical control. Our position-swap control experiment (Section~\ref{sec:position_swap}) reveals that this user-token dominance has a positional component: causal influence concentrates at sequence-final positions regardless of content placement. We interpret this as reflecting the autoregressive bottleneck---the model's output distribution is necessarily most sensitive to the final contextual positions. Importantly, the persona signal still propagates from its origin (system tokens) to these final positions via early-layer attention, maintaining the existence-causation dissociation at the mechanistic level.
\textbf{(3) Architecture-Specific Steering Vulnerabilities:} While personality directions are geometrically consistent across architectures, their susceptibility to activation-space manipulation varies dramatically ($|\alpha|$ ranges from 1.0 for TinyLlama to 10.0 for Gemma-2). This reveals that architectural choices---such as Gemma's sliding-window attention versus Qwen's dense attention---create differential vulnerabilities to inference-time personality manipulation, with direct implications for AI safety and robustness.
\textbf{(4) Penetration of AI Identity Guardrails:} We demonstrate that activation steering can bypass AI identity guardrails that prompt-based methods cannot penetrate. While prompt-only personality engineering triggers defensive AI identity claims (``I don't have emotions or personal experiences...''), activation steering maintains stable personality expression by operating at the representational level---achieving 4.6/5.0 human evaluation scores versus 3.6/5.0 for prompting. This dissociation reveals that activation-space interventions access personality mechanisms deeper than surface-level linguistic guardrails, with profound implications for both AI safety (harder to defend against) and personality control (more reliable expression).

\paragraph{Methodological Framework.}
To enable these discoveries, we introduce PersonaLens, a standardized and model-agnostic pipeline validated on five architecturally distinct models spanning three model families (Qwen, LLaMA, Gemma). The remarkable consistency of structural metrics across these models---from LOSO accuracy to causal localization patterns to orthogonality structure---demonstrates that the pipeline captures genuine architectural invariants rather than model-specific artifacts. The framework's multi-method approach (mean-difference vectors, PCA directions, and linear probes) provides converging evidence robust to extraction method choice, addressing non-identifiability concerns \citep{venkatesh2026nonidentifiability}. PersonaLens outputs---encoding profiles, causal importance maps, orthogonality matrices, and steering trade-off curves---provide a common vocabulary for cross-model personality analysis.
\paragraph{Early-Layer Causal Dominance: Mechanistic Explanation.}
Our finding that personality expression is causally dominated by early-layer attention processing (L0--L2) appears to conflict with prior work suggesting that semantic and identity-related processing occurs in middle-to-late layers \citep{geva2021transformer, geva2023dissecting, meng2022locating}. However, this apparent discrepancy reflects a fundamental distinction between two types of information processing in transformers: \textbf{contextual framing} versus \textbf{content retrieval} \citep{elhage2021mathematical}.
\textbf{Contextual framing vs content retrieval}: Geva et al.~\citep{geva2021transformer, geva2023dissecting} demonstrate that factual knowledge retrieval---recalling that ``Paris is the capital of France''---relies on middle-to-late layer MLP computations that function as key-value memories. In contrast, personality traits represent \emph{behavioral modulation}---a meta-level control signal that determines \emph{how} content is expressed rather than \emph{what} content is retrieved. Our findings suggest that early attention layers specialize in extracting contextual framing cues (including persona instructions) that modulate downstream processing, while deeper layers handle semantic elaboration and knowledge retrieval.
\textbf{Attention-mediated propagation}: The position-swap control (Section~\ref{sec:position_swap}) reveals that persona information \emph{originates} in system tokens but is \emph{propagated} to sequence-final user tokens via early-layer attention (L0--L2). This creates a causal bottleneck at the sequence boundary where the autoregressive generation process begins. The concentration of causal influence at sequence-final positions reflects the autoregressive bottleneck---the model's output distribution is necessarily most sensitive to activations nearest the generation point. However, the \emph{content} of those activations is determined by early-layer attention that integrates persona instructions from system tokens.
\textbf{Existence-causation dissociation}: Our probing results confirm that personality representations \emph{grow stronger} through depth (probe accuracy $\ge$0.91 in middle-to-late layers), yet causal patching demonstrates that early layers dominate causal influence. This dissociation is not contradictory---it reflects the distinction between where information is \emph{stored/amplified} versus where it \emph{causally determines output}. Early layers establish the personality \emph{direction} through contextual integration; deeper layers amplify and elaborate this signal through semantic processing. Interventions at early layers are causally potent because they set the trajectory that downstream layers follow.
\textbf{Architectural specificity}: Our models (0.5B--2B parameters) may exhibit compressed processing dynamics compared to larger models (7B--175B) examined in prior work. Smaller models may handle both contextual framing and some semantic processing in early layers due to capacity constraints. Validating PersonaLens on larger models (7B+) remains a priority for future work to test whether the early-layer causal dominance generalizes or whether larger models exhibit more distributed causal influence.
\paragraph{Penetrating AI Identity Guardrails.}
Beyond performance gains, we observe that activation steering can penetrate AI identity guardrails that prompt-based methods cannot. On decision-making questions, prompt-only engineering triggers defensive AI identity claims (``I don't have emotions or personal experiences... I rely solely on data-driven analysis''), collapsing openness scores from 4.0 to 2.0. In contrast, activation steering at the same intensity successfully maintains 4.0 by injecting personality at the representational level (``Embrace Empathy: Understand the human impact... feel the weight of your decisions'').
This dissociation suggests that activation steering operates at a deeper level than surface-level prompting. While explicit persona instructions in prompts can trigger safety mechanisms designed to prevent anthropomorphization, direct manipulation of activation patterns bypasses these linguistic guardrails. This has profound implications: (1)~for AI safety, it reveals that activation-space interventions may be harder to defend against than prompt-level attacks; (2)~for personality control, it demonstrates that steering can achieve stable personality expression even in contexts where prompting fails; (3)~for mechanistic understanding, it confirms that personality representations exist as geometric structures independent of their linguistic expression.

\paragraph{Safety and Ethics.}
Identifying and controlling mechanisms of personality inherently introduces dual-use risks. Constructive applications include building empathetic, clinically supportive, or pedagogically adapted AI personas. However, the ability to directly modulate defense mechanisms (e.g., enforcing projection or rationalization) or extract vectors mapping to the ``Dark Triad'' traits computationally opens the door to maliciously polarizing, toxic, or psychologically manipulative models at inference time. Our framework exposes these latent vulnerabilities, stressing the need for representation-level guardrails distinct from traditional reinforcement learning filters.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
(1)~While we expanded evaluations to five distinct models spanning three architectural families, analyzing extreme-scale ($>$70B) models may reveal different dynamics. (2)~Our paraphrase control (91\% accuracy) and BFI-44 validation provide strong evidence against prompt confounding and keyword-based artifacts, yet more diverse paraphrase templates and additional psychological instruments (e.g., NEO-PI-R) would further strengthen claims. (3)~Baseline comparisons demonstrate activation steering's superiority over prompt-only methods, but optimal $\alpha$ values are model-specific and require per-model tuning---automated hyperparameter optimization remains an open challenge. (4)~Non-identifiability concerns \citep{venkatesh2026nonidentifiability} strictly apply: functionally equivalent but geometrically distinct directions may exist across architectures despite functional transferability. (5)~The defense mechanism hierarchy analysis shows that while the \emph{ordinal} structure of Vaillant's maturity levels is preserved across both tested architectures (Qwen2.5 and Qwen3), the \emph{magnitude} of clustering varies substantially (immature/mature ratio: 17$\times$ vs.\ 2.2$\times$). Full cross-model validation on all five architectures, and investigation of the training-regime factors driving this variation, remain important open questions.

\paragraph{Practical Applications.}
Beyond scientific understanding, PersonaLens enables concrete applications across multiple domains:
\textbf{(1)~Personality-aligned conversational agents}: Our framework enables building AI assistants with stable, controllable personality profiles. Unlike prompt-based personality engineering (which triggers AI identity guardrails), activation steering maintains consistent personality expression across diverse contexts. Applications include customer service agents with specific interpersonal styles (high Agreeableness for support roles, high Conscientiousness for task-oriented assistance), educational tutors adapted to student personality (matching or complementing learner traits), and creative writing assistants with distinct authorial voices.
\textbf{(2)~Clinical and therapeutic AI}: The ability to control defense mechanisms opens applications in mental health support. Therapeutic chatbots could model adaptive defense use (mature defenses like humor and sublimation) while avoiding maladaptive patterns (immature defenses like denial and projection). Our Vaillant hierarchy validation (Section~\ref{sec:results_ortho}) provides theoretical grounding for such applications. However, clinical deployment requires careful ethical oversight---the same mechanisms enabling therapeutic support could be misused to manipulate vulnerable users.
\textbf{(3)~AI safety and alignment research}: PersonaLens provides tools for probing and controlling personality-related behaviors that current alignment techniques struggle to address. The discovery that activation steering penetrates AI identity guardrails (Section~\ref{sec:baselines}) has dual implications: it reveals vulnerabilities requiring new defense mechanisms, but also enables more reliable personality control for beneficial applications. Understanding architecture-specific steering bounds (Table~\ref{tab:steering}) informs robustness testing and adversarial defense strategies.
\textbf{(4)~Personalized content generation}: Applications in creative writing, marketing, and entertainment benefit from controllable personality expression. Game NPCs with distinct, stable personalities; marketing copy adapted to target audience personality profiles; personalized storytelling that matches reader preferences. The orthogonality of Big Five dimensions (Section~\ref{sec:results_ortho}) enables independent control of multiple personality facets simultaneously.
These applications demonstrate that PersonaLens addresses not just scientific questions but practical challenges in building personality-aware AI systems. The framework's model-agnostic design ensures applicability across current and future architectures.

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}

Through systematic experiments on five architecturally diverse models---Qwen3-0.6B, Qwen2.5-0.5B, TinyLlama-1.1B, Llama-3.2-1B, and Gemma-2-2B---spanning three major architectural families, we uncover five fundamental discoveries about personality representation in LLMs: (1)~Big Five traits occupy near-orthogonal directions in activation space (mean off-diagonal $|\cos|$ between 0.05 and 0.22), providing white-box evidence that LLMs spontaneously internalize psychometric factor structure; (2)~personality information grows stronger through deeper layers (probe accuracy $\ge$0.91), yet causal influence is concentrated in early-layer user-token attention---a fundamental existence--causation dissociation explained by the distinction between contextual framing and content retrieval in transformer processing; (3)~extracted personality directions enable reliable inference-time control, but maximum tolerable intervention strength varies dramatically across architectures ($|\alpha|$ ranges from 1.0 to 10.0), exposing architecture-dependent vulnerabilities; (4)~defense mechanism clustering in activation space mirrors Vaillant's psychological maturity hierarchy, with immature defenses forming strongly correlated subspaces ($|\cos|=0.511$) while mature defenses are near-orthogonal ($0.030$)---the first neural evidence that LLMs internalize hierarchical defense organization; and (5)~activation steering penetrates AI identity guardrails that prompt-based methods cannot bypass, achieving 4.6/5.0 human evaluation scores versus 3.6/5.0 for prompting, with critical implications for AI safety. These findings demonstrate that personality in LLMs is not merely a behavioral phenomenon but corresponds to structured geometric patterns---from Big Five orthogonality to defense mechanism hierarchies---that are remarkably consistent across architectures, opening new avenues for both scientific understanding and safety-critical control of AI personality.

\paragraph{Future Work.} We plan to (1)~extend cross-model validation to larger 7B+ scale architectures (Qwen2.5-7B, Llama-3.1-8B, Mistral-7B) to test whether our findings generalize to extreme-scale models; (2)~investigate attention-head-level localization using EAP-IG to identify specific attention heads responsible for personality processing; (3)~develop automated $\alpha$ optimization methods to eliminate manual per-model tuning; (4)~explore whether SAE-denoised vectors can improve steering stability and reduce model-specific sensitivity; and (5)~investigate the security implications of activation steering's ability to penetrate AI identity guardrails, including potential defenses against malicious personality manipulation.

\subsection{Extended Cross-Model Analytics}\label{app:cross_model}
\subsubsection{Cohen's d and Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR)}
Across all tested models, the linear probe separability of Big Five traits yielded large effect sizes. For instance, Llama-3.2-1B consistently maintained a Cohen's $d$ of $\approx 9.75$ and an SNR of $\approx 10.3$ at its optimal intermediate layer (L6 out of L16). Gemma-2-2B achieved $d \approx 7.59$ and SNR $\approx 10.4$ at its optimal layer (L8 out of L26). For TinyLlama-1.1B, the LOSO generalization peak occurred at layer 11 with an equivalent probe accuracy of 0.972.

\subsubsection{Steering Saturation Discrepancy}
As discussed in Section~5.5, the injection bounds before severe linguistic collapse varied significantly. Empirically, inserting the normalized mean difference vector perturbed models differentially:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{TinyLlama-1.1B:} Extreme sensitivity. Alpha sweeps ($\alpha > 1.0$) caused cascading entropy collapses, emitting token loops.
    \item \textbf{Gemma-2-2B:} Surprisingly high resilience ($|\alpha| \le 10.0+$). Despite initial hypotheses about sliding-window attention constraints, Gemma-2 demonstrated robust steering capabilities, suggesting that attention mechanism design alone does not determine steering bounds.
    \item \textbf{Llama-3.2-1B:} Moderate sensitivity ($\alpha \in [-3, 3]$). The architecture smoothly interpolates personality changes.
    \item \textbf{Qwen Series:} Exceptional resilience. Qwen2.5-0.5B maintained sub-10 PPL even at $\alpha=6.0$, likely due to dense normalization placement or varying geometric isotropy in its representation space. Qwen3-0.6B showed robust steering up to $\alpha=4.0$.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Input:} Model $f_\theta$, Trait $t$, Contrastive pairs $\{(x^+_i, x^-_i)\}_{i=1}^K$\\
\textbf{Output:} Persona vectors $\{v_\ell\}_{\ell=0}^{L-1}$, Encoding profile, Causal map\\[4pt]
\textit{// Phase 2: Collect Activations}\\
1:\quad \textbf{for} $i = 1$ \textbf{to} $K$ \textbf{do}\\
2:\quad\quad $\{h_\ell(x^+_i)\}_{\ell=0}^{L-1} \leftarrow \text{ForwardPass}(f_\theta, x^+_i)$\\

5:\quad \textbf{for} $\ell = 0$ \textbf{to} $L-1$ \textbf{do}\\

\section{Perplexity Calculation Methodology}\label{app:ppl}

Sequence perplexity is computed using the \emph{same base model} that generates the steered output. For a generated sequence $w_1, \ldots, w_T$, we compute:
\begin{equation}
\text{PPL} = \exp\left(-\frac{1}{T}\sum_{t=1}^{T} \log p_\theta(w_t | w_{<t})\right)
\end{equation}
where $p_\theta$ is the unmodified (un-steered) model's probability distribution. We compute perplexity on the \emph{full sequence} (prompt + generated response), using the base model without steering hooks active. The threshold of PPL $< 10$ was selected empirically: manual inspection revealed that outputs with PPL $> 10$ consistently exhibited token-level repetition loops, grammatical breakdown, or semantic incoherence. We acknowledge that absolute PPL values are not directly comparable across architectures due to different tokenizers and vocabulary sizes; the threshold serves as a within-model fluency gate rather than a cross-model metric.
