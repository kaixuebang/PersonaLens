\begin{abstract}
Activation steering---controlling LLM behavior by modifying internal activations---holds promise for aligning model outputs with desired attributes. Yet steering efficacy varies dramatically: the same intervention produces strong behavioral change in some models but zero effect in others. \emph{Why?} Across \textbf{14} instruction-tuned LLMs (0.5B--14B, five architectural families, 910 steering experiments), we identify the structural determinants of this variance.

\textbf{(1)~Hidden state scale is the primary bottleneck, but not the only one.} RMSNorm epsilon ($\epsilon$) determines hidden state magnitude, which dilutes additive perturbations. RMS and steering efficacy are strongly correlated ($\rho{=}{-}0.80$, $p{<}0.001$), and equalizing $\alpha$ to match perturbation ratio closes 74\% of the gap. Yet three $\epsilon{=}10^{-5}$ models (Phi-3, Llama-2, DeepSeek-R1) achieve $\Delta{<}0.7$ despite small RMS, revealing a \emph{second failure mode}: architecture-specific factors beyond $\epsilon$.

\textbf{(2)~Detectability $\neq$ controllability.} The layer where personality is most detectable (LOSO accuracy 1.0 at layer~2) is not where it is controllable; RMSNorm erases late-layer perturbations. Middle-layer selection ($L{\approx}N/2$) is a reliable cross-model default.

\textbf{(3)~Traits form a representational hierarchy, not flat orthogonality.} Cross-probe analysis reveals O-E sharing 79\% of probe information, while C-N are genuinely isolated (23\%). This hierarchy is stable across all 14 architectures ($t{=}13.3$, $p{<}0.001$).

\textbf{(4)~15$\times$ efficacy range with generalized linearity.} Effectiveness spans $\Delta{=}0.16$--$2.33$; 50/70 model-trait pairs show strong linear dose-response ($|r|{>}0.8$).

These findings establish steering efficacy as an \emph{architectural property} and provide practical guidance: check $\epsilon$, steer at $L{\approx}N/2$, and report perturbation ratio.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}

Activation steering---modifying internal hidden states during inference to control model behavior---has emerged as a powerful tool for aligning Large Language Models (LLMs) without retraining. The representation engineering paradigm \citep{zou2023representation} has demonstrated that semantic concepts including truthfulness \citep{li2024iti}, harmlessness \citep{rimsky2024steering}, and emotional states \citep{bao2024emotion} correspond to linear directions in activation space that can be extracted and injected at inference time.

Yet a striking empirical observation remains unexplained: \textbf{steering efficacy varies dramatically across models}. The same intervention ($\tilde{h}_\ell = h_\ell + \alpha \cdot \hat{v}_\ell$) with the same coefficient $\alpha$ produces strong behavioral change in some architectures but zero effect in others. Prior work has largely studied steering on single models \citep{feng2026persona, rimsky2024steering, li2024iti}, leaving the sources of this variance unknown. Is it the quality of the extracted direction? The choice of injection layer? Or something deeper in the model's architecture?

We conduct the first systematic investigation of this question. Across \textbf{fourteen} instruction-tuned LLMs (0.5B--14B parameters, five architectural families), we extract personality steering vectors using contrastive activation analysis, inject them across multiple layers and coefficients, and evaluate behavioral change using LLM-as-Judge scoring with BFI rubrics. This yields 910+ steering experiments that reveal the structural determinants of steering success.

\paragraph{Research Questions.} We investigate four questions about what makes activation steering work:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,topsep=2pt,itemsep=1pt]
    \item \textbf{Q1 (Architecture):} Can architectural hyperparameters predict which models are steerable?
    \item \textbf{Q2 (Layer Selection):} What determines the optimal injection layer, and why do common selection criteria fail?
    \item \textbf{Q3 (Representational Structure):} How are personality traits organized in activation space, and does this structure constrain steering?
    \item \textbf{Q4 (Dose-Response):} Does the linear relationship between steering strength and behavioral effect generalize across architectures?
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Contributions.} Our analysis reveals four findings that establish steering efficacy as an \emph{architectural property}:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,topsep=2pt,itemsep=1pt]
\item \textbf{Hidden state scale is the primary bottleneck} (Sec.~\ref{sec:arch_finding}): RMS and steering efficacy correlate at $\rho{=}{-}0.80$ ($p{<}0.001$). Equalizing perturbation ratio closes 74\% of the gap, confirming RMSNorm epsilon ($\epsilon$) as the dominant factor. Yet three $\epsilon{=}10^{-5}$ models remain poorly steerable, revealing a \emph{second failure mode}: architecture-specific factors beyond $\epsilon$ (Fig.~\ref{fig:rms_vs_delta}).
\item \textbf{Signal Detectability $\neq$ Controllability} (Sec.~\ref{sec:layer_finding}): The layer where personality is most detectable (LOSO accuracy 1.0) is not where it is controllable. RMSNorm erases late-layer perturbations; middle-layer selection ($L \approx N/2$) is a reliable default.
\item \textbf{Representational Hierarchy} (Sec.~\ref{sec:oe_collapse}): Cross-probe analysis reveals O-E sharing 79\% of probe information, while C-N are genuinely isolated ($0.23$). This hierarchy is stable across all 14 architectures ($t{=}13.3$, $p{<}0.001$), refuting prior claims of flat orthogonality.
\item \textbf{15$\times$ Efficacy Range with Generalized Linearity} (Sec.~\ref{sec:linearity}): Steering effectiveness ranges from $\Delta{=}0.16$ to $\Delta{=}2.33$. 50/70 model-trait pairs show strong linear dose-response ($|r|{>}0.8$), suggesting linearity is a general property of residual-stream perturbation integration (Fig.~\ref{fig:steering_curves}).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Personality in LLMs.}
Personality expression in LLMs has been studied through probing \citep{serapiogarcia2023personality, ju2025probing}, steering \citep{frising2025linear, feng2026persona, chen2025persona, bhandari2026activation}, and vector algebra \citep{feng2026persona, billy2025blending}. \citet{ju2025probing} find personality in middle/upper layers across 11 models, addressing \emph{detection}. We address \emph{control}: on Qwen2.5-7B, personality is perfectly detectable at L2 but not controllable there (Sec.~\ref{sec:layer_finding}). The most related work, PERSONA \citep{feng2026persona}, demonstrated steering on a single model (Llama-3-8B). Single-model studies cannot identify architectural determinants---our cross-model analysis reveals that PERSONA's methodological choices (LOSO layer selection, cosine similarity for orthogonality) do not generalize.

\paragraph{Representation Engineering and Steering.}
Representation Engineering (RepE) \citep{zou2023representation} establishes that concepts correspond to linear directions. ITI \citep{li2024iti}, Contrastive Activation Addition \citep{rimsky2024steering}, and recent advances (SVF \citep{svf2026}, factored subspaces \citep{factored2026}) generalize this. EasySteer \citep{xu2025easysteer} provides production infrastructure; PersonaLens provides the complementary \emph{scientific foundation}.

\paragraph{Reliability Concerns.}
\citet{bao2025traits2circuits} propose ``personality circuits.'' OOD fragility and collateral degradation are noted by \citet{cae2025}. SAE-denoised vectors \citep{sae2025denoising} and magnitude-direction dissociations \citep{direction2026} improve reliability. \citet{venkatesh2026nonidentifiability} raise non-identifiability concerns; our multi-method convergence mitigates this.

% ============================================================
\subsection{Phases 1--2: Data Construction and Activation Collection}\label{sec:data}

For each Big Five dimension, we construct $K{=}20$ paired high/low-trait system prompts with shared neutral scenarios, following the contrastive activation paradigm \citep{zou2023representation}. Hidden states $h_\ell \in \mathbb{R}^d$ are collected at the last token position of every layer, producing paired activations $H^+_t, H^-_t$.

% ============================================================
\subsection{Phase 3: Persona Vector Extraction}\label{sec:extraction}

We extract personality directions using mean-difference (Contrastive Activation Addition) \citep{rimsky2024steering}:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:meandiff}
v^{\text{diff}}_\ell = \frac{1}{K} \sum_{i=1}^{K} \left[ h_\ell(x^+_i) - h_\ell(x^-_i) \right], \qquad \hat{v}^{\text{diff}}_\ell = \frac{v^{\text{diff}}_\ell}{\|v^{\text{diff}}_\ell\|}
\end{equation}
PCA first components and LOSO-validated linear probes yield highly collinear directions (mean $|\cos| > 0.95$), confirming robustness. We additionally compute \textbf{RMS-normalized difference norm} $\|v^{\text{diff}}_\ell\|/\text{RMS}_\ell$ to control for residual-stream scaling.

% ============================================================
\subsection{Phases 4--5: Causal Localization and Steering}\label{sec:localization}

Activation patching measures causal importance as KL divergence $\mathcal{I}(\ell) = D_{\text{KL}}(\text{softmax}(o^+) \| \text{softmax}(o^+_{\text{patched},\ell}))$ \citep{meng2022locating, bao2024emotion}. The steering experiment injects persona vectors during inference:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:steering}
\tilde{h}_\ell = h_\ell + \alpha \cdot \hat{v}_\ell
\end{equation}
where $\alpha \in [-6, +6]$ controls strength. Crucially, \textbf{no personality-related prompting} is used during steering---all personality expression arises from activation manipulation.

% ============================================================
\subsection{Cross-Trait Orthogonality Analysis}\label{sec:orthogonality}

We measure trait independence through cosine similarity between persona vectors and, more rigorously, \textbf{cross-probe accuracy}: training a probe on trait A's activations and testing on trait B's labels. High accuracy indicates shared representations, directly testing PERSONA's orthogonality claim.

% ============================================================
\section{Experimental Setup}\label{sec:setup}

\paragraph{Models.} We evaluate on 14 instruction-tuned LLMs (0.5B--14B) from five families: Qwen2.5/3 (6 models, $\epsilon{=}10^{-6}$ or $10^{-5}$), LLaMA-2/3 (5 models, $\epsilon{=}10^{-5}$), Mistral (1, $\epsilon{=}10^{-5}$), Gemma-2 (1, $\epsilon{=}10^{-6}$), and Phi-3 (1, $\epsilon{=}10^{-5}$). Full model details in Appendix~\ref{app:model_details}. Llama-3-8B-Instruct is PERSONA's exact model, enabling direct validation.

\paragraph{Traits Analyzed.} Five Big Five dimensions (O, C, E, A, N) using $K{=}20$ scenario pairs each (130 pairs per model).

\paragraph{Evaluation.} Steering is evaluated at $\alpha \in [-6, +6]$ with LLM-as-Judge scoring using BFI rubrics. We report: (1)~steering effectiveness $\Delta = \max(\text{mean}) - \min(\text{mean})$; (2)~Spearman $\rho$ between $\alpha$ and ratings. All statistical analyses use $n{=}70$ (14 models $\times$ 5 traits).

\section{Results}\label{sec:results}

We organize results around four findings that collectively explain why steering efficacy varies across architectures. All statistical analyses use per-trait data points ($n{=}70$: 14 models $\times$ 5 traits) unless otherwise noted.

% ============================================================
\subsection{Finding 1: Hidden State Scale Is the Primary Bottleneck}\label{sec:arch_finding}

The most striking observation in our data is the 15$\times$ range in steering efficacy: from $\Delta{=}0.16$ (Q2.5-14B) to $\Delta{=}2.33$ (Llama-3-8B). We investigate whether this variation is predictable from model architecture.

\paragraph{Hidden State Scale Varies 69$\times$.} At the steering layer, the RMS magnitude of hidden states varies from 0.036 (Mistral-7B) to 2.47 (Gemma-2B)---a 69$\times$ range. Since steering adds a unit-normalized vector ($\hat{v}$, $\|\hat{v}\|{=}1$), the effective perturbation ratio $\alpha / \|h_\ell\|$ is inversely proportional to hidden state magnitude. Models with large hidden states require proportionally larger $\alpha$ for equivalent behavioral change.

\paragraph{RMS Strongly Predicts Efficacy.} The Spearman correlation between RMS and steering $\Delta$ is $\rho{=}{-}0.80$ ($p{=}0.0006$, Fig.~\ref{fig:rms_vs_delta}). This correlation is driven by the RMSNorm epsilon ($\epsilon$): models with $\epsilon{=}10^{-5}$ generally have small RMS (0.04--0.19) and high $\Delta$, while $\epsilon{=}10^{-6}$ models have large RMS (0.31--2.5) and low $\Delta$. A linear regression from ($\epsilon$, softcap) to $\log_{10}(\text{RMS})$ achieves $R^2{=}0.56$ ($n{=}14$), rising to 0.87 excluding five outliers (Table~\ref{tab:architecture_regression}).

\paragraph{Two Failure Modes.} The expanded model set reveals that $\epsilon$ is necessary but not sufficient for steerability. Three $\epsilon{=}10^{-5}$ models achieve surprisingly low $\Delta$:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,topsep=2pt,itemsep=1pt]
\item \textbf{Phi-3-mini} ($\Delta{=}0.38$, RMS${=}0.588$): Despite $\epsilon{=}10^{-5}$, its RMS is anomalously high---comparable to $\epsilon{=}10^{-6}$ models. We attribute this to Phi-3's distinct RMSNorm implementation.
\item \textbf{DeepSeek-R1-14B} ($\Delta{=}0.18$, RMS${=}0.559$): Same anomaly. Its Qwen2 backbone inherits normalization properties that inflate hidden states despite $\epsilon{=}10^{-5}$.
\item \textbf{Llama-2-7B} ($\Delta{=}0.66$, RMS${=}0.192$): Low RMS as expected for $\epsilon{=}10^{-5}$, yet $\Delta$ is only 0.66---far below Llama-3-8B's 2.33 at similar RMS. This suggests training and instruction-tuning quality also contribute.
\end{itemize}
These observations refine our understanding: $\epsilon$ sets the \emph{order of magnitude} of RMS, but architecture-specific normalization implementations and training can shift RMS within that range. The \textbf{primary failure mode} (Mode~1) is large RMS from small $\epsilon$, fixable by scaling $\alpha$. The \textbf{secondary failure mode} (Mode~2) is architecture-specific limitations even with favorable $\epsilon$.

\paragraph{Causal Validation: Equalized-$\alpha$.} To confirm that RMS is causally responsible (not merely correlated), we proportionally scale $\alpha$ to equalize perturbation ratio across five $\epsilon{=}10^{-6}$ models. This closes 74\% of the gap relative to the five strong $\epsilon{=}10^{-5}$ steerers (mean $\Delta$: 0.46 $\to$ 1.72 vs.\ strong group mean 2.13; Table~\ref{tab:equalized_alpha} in Appendix~\ref{app:architecture}). The residual 26\% is attributable to Mode~2 factors. Notably, the equalized-$\epsilon{=}10^{-6}$ group ($\Delta{=}1.72$) substantially exceeds the weak $\epsilon{=}10^{-5}$ group ($\Delta{=}0.41$), confirming that RMS is the \emph{dominant} factor despite not being the only one.

\subsection{Finding 2: Signal Detectability $\neq$ Controllability}\label{sec:layer_finding}

Finding~1 establishes that hidden state scale matters for steering efficacy. We now show that \emph{where} in the network the perturbation is injected matters equally.

\paragraph{The Problem.} On Qwen2.5-7B, LOSO accuracy reaches 1.0 at \emph{layer 2}---the token embedding layer---because personality cues are trivially detectable from vocabulary patterns (e.g., ``adventure'' vs.\ ``routine''). The strongest contrastive signal ($\|v_\text{diff}\|/\|h\|$) occurs at layer 27 (the last transformer layer). Yet steering at \emph{either} layer produces zero behavioral change:

\paragraph{Why Late-Layer Steering Fails.} At layer 27, hidden state RMS is 4.52 while the perturbation at $\alpha{=}10$ is only 3.7\% of magnitude. The subsequent RMSNorm ($\texttt{model.norm}$) normalizes the hidden state, erasing this tiny perturbation before the LM head. We confirmed this: the steering hook fires correctly at L27, but output is \emph{identical} to the unsteered baseline.

\paragraph{Why Middle Layers Work.} At layer 14, the perturbation propagates through 13 subsequent transformer layers that can amplify task-relevant dimensions before reaching the final norm. On Llama-3-8B, LOSO selects layer 14---the exact middle---a fortunate coincidence. On 6/14 models, LOSO selects early layers (L0--L8) where personality is detectable but not controllable. We find $L \approx N/2$ is a reliable cross-model default.

\subsection{Finding 3: Cross-Trait Representational Hierarchy}\label{sec:oe_collapse}

Prior work reports that personality vectors are ``approximately orthogonal,'' enabling compositional persona algebra \citep{feng2026persona}. We test this with cross-probe accuracy---a more discriminating metric that measures whether the linear boundary separating one trait's activations also separates another's (Table~\ref{tab:cross_probe} in Appendix~\ref{app:entanglement}).

\paragraph{Method.} For each pair of traits (A, B), we train a linear probe on trait A's contrastive activations and evaluate it on trait B's labels. High cross-probe accuracy indicates shared representations.

\textbf{O-E Collapse:} Openness and Extraversion share 79\% of linear probe information (cross-probe accuracy 0.79 across 14 models), confirmed by shuffle-label permutation test (null mean $= 0.49$, $p{=}0.004$). The O-E cross-probe exceeds 0.5 in 13/14 models (range: 0.50--0.98), confirming this is not driven by outlier architectures. This holds even on the model where prior work reported approximate orthogonality (Llama-3-8B: O-E cross-probe${=}0.94$). \textbf{C-N Isolation:} Conscientiousness and Neuroticism are genuinely isolated (cross-probe accuracy 0.23, at chance level $p{=}0.84$). The O-E vs.\ C-N difference is significant (paired $t{=}13.3$, $p{<}0.001$). Orthogonality is \emph{pair-dependent}: the Big Five exhibit a representational hierarchy that constrains compositional steering---simultaneously controlling O and E independently is fundamentally limited by shared representations.

\subsection{Finding 4: Dose-Response Linearity Generalizes}\label{sec:linearity}

We quantify dose-response linearity as the Spearman correlation between $\alpha$ and behavioral shift. Across 70 model-trait pairs, 50/70 show strong linearity ($|r|{>}0.8$) and only 6/70 are essentially non-linear (Table~\ref{tab:linearity} in Appendix~\ref{app:linearity_detail}).

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/fig_steering_curves.png}
\caption{\textbf{Representative steering curves.} Mean judge rating vs.\ $\alpha$ for three models spanning the efficacy range. Left: Llama-3-8B ($\varepsilon{=}10^{-5}$) shows strong monotonic response across all traits. Center: Qwen2.5-7B ($\varepsilon{=}10^{-6}$) shows weaker but still linear response. Right: Qwen2.5-14B ($\varepsilon{=}10^{-6}$) shows near-flat response---personality is detectable (LOSO${=}1.0$) but not controllable at this scale.}
\label{fig:steering_curves}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Why Linearity Matters.} Linear dose-response means that steering produces \emph{graded, predictable} control: doubling $\alpha$ approximately doubles the behavioral effect. This is not trivially expected---non-linear saturation or threshold effects would be equally plausible. Figure~\ref{fig:steering_curves} illustrates the spectrum: Llama-3-8B shows near-perfect linear response across all five traits, Qwen2.5-7B shows moderate linearity, and Qwen2.5-14B shows essentially flat response despite personality being perfectly detectable. This confirms that linearity is a property of the \emph{steering interface} (perturbation ratio $+$ layer position), not the representation quality. Non-linear responses (6/70 pairs) occur exclusively in weak-steering models where the perturbation is too small relative to hidden state magnitude to produce reliable effects.

\paragraph{Linearity Breaks Down at Scale.} Among models with $\Delta{>}0.5$, 36/41 pairs (88\%) show $|r|{>}0.8$. Among models with $\Delta{<}0.3$, only 5/17 pairs (29\%) exceed this threshold. This suggests that linearity is an emergent property of sufficiently strong perturbations: once the perturbation ratio exceeds a threshold, the residual stream integrates it linearly.

\subsection{Additional Findings}\label{sec:additional}

\paragraph{Extraction is Universal.} All fourteen models achieve LOSO accuracy $\ge$0.82, confirming that personality representations are a universal property of instruction-tuned transformers. However, extraction quality does \emph{not} predict steering effectiveness: the Qwen2.5 family achieves perfect LOSO (1.0) at early layers while requiring middle-layer steering, illustrating the distinction between detectability and controllability.

\paragraph{Cross-Trait Interference.} On a representative subset of 8 models, models with strong steering show $>$2$\times$ selectivity (primary effect vs.\ off-target shifts), while weak-steering models show near-unity selectivity (Table~\ref{tab:interference_summary}). This confirms the representational overlap identified by cross-probe analysis.

\section{Discussion}\label{sec:discussion}

\paragraph{Architecture Predicts Steerability.}
Hidden state RMS and steering $\Delta$ correlate at $\rho{=}{-}0.80$ ($p{<}0.001$), with $\epsilon$ as the primary determinant of RMS. However, our expanded model set reveals two distinct failure modes (Fig.~\ref{fig:rms_vs_delta}): \textbf{Mode~1} (RMS too large from small $\epsilon$) affects 6/14 models and is fixable by scaling $\alpha$ (equalized-$\alpha$ experiment closes 74\% of the gap); \textbf{Mode~2} (architecture-specific limitations despite favorable $\epsilon$) affects 3/14 models (Phi-3-mini, Llama-2-7B, DeepSeek-R1-14B). The key diagnostic is perturbation ratio $\alpha/\|h\|$: if RMS is small but $\Delta$ remains low, Mode~2 is at play. For example, Llama-2-7B (RMS${=}0.192$, $\Delta{=}0.66$) vs.\ Llama-3-8B (RMS${=}0.121$, $\Delta{=}2.33$) share the same architecture family and similar RMS, yet differ by 3.5$\times$ in $\Delta$---likely due to differences in instruction-tuning quality and training data composition.

\paragraph{Layer Selection and RMSNorm Attenuation.}
At layer $\ell$, the perturbation $\alpha \hat{v}_\ell$ propagates through $L - \ell$ residual blocks. At late layers, few blocks remain to amplify it; at middle layers ($\ell \approx L/2$), roughly $L/2$ transformations can selectively amplify task-relevant dimensions. This is consistent with representations growing in magnitude with depth \citep{godey2024anisotropy}.

\paragraph{Implications for Compositional Steering.}
The O-E representational collapse places a boundary on compositional persona algebra: if Openness and Extraversion share 79\% of their linear probe information, then $\alpha_O \cdot v_O + \alpha_E \cdot v_E$ cannot independently control both traits. However, for genuinely isolated pairs (C and N, cross-probe 0.23), compositional steering remains promising. Cross-probe analysis can predict which trait pairs are composable.

\paragraph{Entanglement Metrics Do Not Predict Steering.}
Aggregate entanglement does \emph{not} predict steering ($\rho{=}0.05$, $p{=}0.87$, $n{=}14$), confirming steerability is determined by architectural factors and layer selection, not representation quality.

\paragraph{Practical Recommendations.}
(1)~\emph{Check $\epsilon$ first}---models with $\epsilon{=}10^{-6}$ need proportionally larger $\alpha$. (2)~\emph{Use middle-layer steering} ($L \approx N/2$) as default. (3)~\emph{Report perturbation ratio} $\alpha/\|h\|$ for cross-model comparison. (4)~\emph{Use cross-probe accuracy} for trait independence, not cosine similarity.

\paragraph{Can Non-Linear Probes Overcome the O-E Collapse?}\label{sec:mlp_probe}
A natural question is whether the O-E representational collapse---where linear probes for Openness and Extraversion achieve 79\% cross-accuracy---is an artifact of linear readout or a fundamental property of the representation. We test this by replacing the linear probe with a 2-layer MLP (hidden dimension 64, ReLU) on the same activation data for six models. The MLP probe reduces O-E cross-probe confusion in 15/24 trait pairs across the six models, with a mean reduction of $\Delta_{\text{cross}}{=}{-}0.124$ on the most entangled pairs (O-E and E-A). The effect is strongest for Gemma-2B (O-E: $0.90 \to 0.575$, $-0.325$) and Qwen2.5-7B (O-E: $0.938 \to 0.725$, $-0.213$). However, MLP probes \emph{increase} confusion for some low-entanglement pairs (e.g., C-N in Llama-3-8B: $0.163 \to 0.500$, $+0.338$), suggesting that non-linear probes learn both trait-specific and shared non-linear features. This provides initial evidence that non-linear steering directions could partially overcome the O-E collapse, but careful regularization would be needed to avoid introducing new cross-trait interference.

\paragraph{Instruction-Tuning Enables Reliable Dose-Response.}\label{sec:instruction_ablation}
To isolate instruction-tuning as a Mode~2 factor, we compare Llama-2-7b-hf (base, no instruction tuning) with Llama-2-7b-chat (instruct). Both share identical architecture, training data, and $\epsilon{=}10^{-5}$, but differ in the instruction-tuning phase. The base model's persona vectors are extractable (LOSO accuracy ${=}1.0$ at layer 5), yet steering produces chaotic dose-response curves: mean $|\rho|{=}0.37$ across five traits, with scores fluctuating non-monotonically (e.g., openness: $\alpha{=}{-}6$ scores 2.17, $\alpha{=}{+}6$ scores 2.31, but $\alpha{=}{+}4$ drops back to 2.26). The instruct model shows clean linear dose-response ($|\rho|{=}0.96$, same architecture). The key difference is \emph{not} effect magnitude ($\Delta{=}0.49$ vs.\ $\Delta{=}0.66$, only 1.3$\times$), but \emph{reliability}: instruction-tuning transforms erratic, non-monotonic responses into predictable linear control. This confirms instruction-tuning quality as a Mode~2 factor---not by making representations more detectable (both achieve perfect LOSO), but by organizing the activation space to support linear perturbation-response relationships.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
(1)~All steering uses linear directions; non-linear interventions \citep{raval2026curveball} might overcome the O-E collapse. Our MLP probe experiment (Section~\ref{sec:mlp_probe}) shows that non-linear probes reduce O-E cross-probe confusion by 12.5\% on average, providing initial evidence that non-linear steering directions may partially address this limitation. (2)~LLM-as-Judge may introduce systematic biases (position bias, verbosity preference) that correlate with but do not perfectly reflect human personality judgments \citep{zheng2023judging}. We mitigate this by using structured BFI rubrics with explicit scoring criteria, and validate with two complementary approaches: (a)~human evaluation on a stratified sample of 47 responses across 8 models, achieving Pearson $r{=}0.62$ with 91.5\% of ratings within $\pm1$ point (quadratic weighted $\kappa{=}0.51$); (b)~inter-judge agreement between Gemma-2-2B (primary) and Qwen2.5-7B (secondary), evaluated on a balanced subset of 420 responses spanning all 14 models (see Appendix~\ref{app:interjudge}). Both human and LLM judges detect the steering effect (human $\Delta{=}0.76$, $p{=}0.015$; LLM $\Delta{=}0.61$, $p{=}0.007$), and human ratings confirm dose-response linearity ($\rho{=}0.30$, $p{=}0.044$). (3)~The O-E collapse may reflect Big Five framework limitations: in psychology, O and E are moderately correlated \citep{costa1992revised}. (4)~Our findings identify two failure modes and provide initial evidence that instruction-tuning quality is a Mode~2 factor (Section~\ref{sec:instruction_ablation}): the base Llama-2-7b shows $|\rho|{=}0.37$ vs.\ $|\rho|{=}0.96$ for the instruct version, despite identical architecture and perfect LOSO detectability in both. However, other Mode~2 factors---training data composition, attention head distribution, and RLHF alignment---remain unisolated. Disentangling these requires controlled ablations on models trained from scratch with known configurations. (5)~All reported $p$-values are two-sided. For the 10 trait-pair cross-probe comparisons, Bonferroni correction requires $p{<}0.005$; our key O-E vs.\ C-N comparison ($t{=}14.2$, Bonferroni-corrected $p{=}1.9{\times}10^{-12}$) survives this threshold by a wide margin. (6)~Our models range from 0.5B to 14B parameters. While the RMS--$\Delta$ correlation holds strongly ($\rho{=}{-}0.80$), the Mode~2 failure in DeepSeek-R1-14B ($\epsilon{=}10^{-5}$ yet $\Delta{=}0.18$) suggests that larger, reasoning-specialized models may introduce additional challenges. Future work should test at 70B+ scale. (7)~We extend defense mechanism analysis to 5 models (Appendix~\ref{app:defense}), confirming Vaillant's hierarchy in 4/5 ($p{=}7.7{\times}10^{-4}$), though cross-framework generalization remains limited.

\paragraph{Reproducibility.}
All experimental code, steering vectors, and evaluation scripts will be publicly released upon publication. Our pipeline uses only open-source models available on HuggingFace and standard Python libraries (PyTorch, scikit-learn, transformers).

\section*{Impact Statement}
This work advances understanding of when and why activation steering succeeds or fails across model architectures. While personality steering has beneficial applications (personalized assistants, therapeutic chatbots), the same techniques could be misused for social engineering or impersonation. We mitigate this by studying only open-source models $\le$14B parameters, using personality traits rather than harmful attributes, and not training new steering capabilities. Our findings---that steering efficacy is architecturally constrained---actually limit the misuse potential: most models are inherently resistant to strong steering at default settings. We encourage future work on guardrails against adversarial activation manipulation.

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}

Through systematic experiments on fourteen instruction-tuned LLMs spanning five architectural families (0.5B--14B parameters), we identify the structural determinants of activation steering efficacy. We report four findings:

\textbf{(1)~Hidden state scale is the primary bottleneck:} RMS and steering efficacy correlate at $\rho{=}{-}0.80$ ($p{<}0.001$). Equalizing perturbation ratio closes 74\% of the gap (Mode~1 failure). Yet three $\epsilon{=}10^{-5}$ models remain poorly steerable, revealing a second failure mode: architecture-specific factors beyond $\epsilon$ (Mode~2).

\textbf{(2)~Signal detectability $\neq$ controllability:} The layer where personality is most detectable is not the best layer for steering. RMSNorm attenuation erases late-layer perturbations; middle-layer selection ($L \approx N/2$) is a more reliable cross-model default than probe-accuracy-based criteria.

\textbf{(3)~Representational hierarchy:} Cross-probe analysis reveals that Openness and Extraversion share 79\% of linear probe information, while Conscientiousness and Neuroticism are genuinely isolated (23\%). This hierarchy is stable across all fourteen architectures ($t{=}13.3$, $p{<}0.001$).

\textbf{(4)~15$\times$ efficacy range with generalized linearity:} Steering effectiveness spans from Q2.5-14B ($\Delta{=}0.16$) to Llama-3-8B ($\Delta{=}2.33$). With proper layer selection, 50/70 model-trait pairs exhibit strong linear dose-response ($|r|{>}0.8$).

These findings establish that steering efficacy is primarily an architectural property---predictable from hyperparameters. Our ablation studies provide three new insights: (i)~MLP probes reduce O-E cross-probe confusion by 12.5\%, suggesting non-linear steering may partially address the O-E collapse; (ii)~instruction-tuning transforms chaotic dose-response ($|\rho|{=}0.37$) into reliable linear control ($|\rho|{=}0.96$), confirming it as a Mode~2 factor; (iii)~Vaillant's defense hierarchy generalizes to 5 models ($p{=}7.7{\times}10^{-4}$). Future work should validate these determinants on larger models, develop non-linear steering methods, and explore SAE-based decomposition of personality representations.

\paragraph{Reproducibility.} All experiments used a single NVIDIA RTX~3090 (24\,GB). Total compute: approximately 25 GPU-hours across all fourteen models (extraction, steering evaluation, entanglement analysis, and ablations). Code and prompts are provided in the supplementary material.

\section{Method Details}\label{app:method_details}

\subsection{Metrics and Evaluation Criteria}

We evaluate personality encoding through four complementary metrics.

\paragraph{Leave-One-Scenario-Out (LOSO) Accuracy.}
Given $K$ contrastive pairs per trait, we train a linear probe on $K{-}1$ pairs and evaluate on the held-out pair. Repeating over all $K$ folds yields LOSO accuracy $a_\ell \in [0,1]$, measuring generalization to unseen contexts.

\paragraph{Cohen's $d$ Effect Size.}
At layer $\ell$, we compute standardized mean separation between high-trait and low-trait activations:
\begin{equation}
d_\ell = \frac{\mu_{+,\ell} - \mu_{-,\ell}}{\sqrt{\frac{(n_+-1)s_{+,\ell}^2 + (n_--1)s_{-,\ell}^2}{n_+ + n_- - 2}}}
\end{equation}
Values $d > 0.8$ indicate large effects; our models consistently achieve $d > 2.0$.

\paragraph{Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR).}
To distinguish genuine signal from scaling artifacts:
\begin{equation}
\text{SNR}_\ell = \frac{\|v^{\text{diff}}_\ell\| / \text{RMS}_\ell}{\mathbb{E}[\|v^{\text{random}}_\ell\| / \text{RMS}_\ell]}
\end{equation}
SNR $> 2$ indicates contrastive signal exceeds random noise; our models achieve SNR $> 7$.

\paragraph{Perturbation Ratio (PR).}
Defined as PR = $\alpha / \|h\|$, this metric normalizes intervention strength by hidden state magnitude for cross-model comparability.

\subsection{Extraction Method Convergence}

Mean-difference vectors show mean $|\cos| > 0.95$ with PCA first components and $|\cos| > 0.89$ with linear probe weights across all traits and models. This high collinearity indicates personality directions are robustly identifiable despite theoretical non-identifiability concerns.

\subsection{Statistical Power and Sample Size}

With $K=20$ scenario pairs per Big Five trait, bootstrap standard errors are $<0.02$ for accuracy and $<0.15$ for Cohen's $d$. For defense mechanisms ($K=10$), accuracy standard errors remain $<0.03$, sufficient given larger effect sizes ($d > 2.0$).

\subsection{Sample Size Ablation}

Comparing random ($n=5$) vs.\ Cartesian product ($n=160$) designs on Qwen3 Openness shows a 2.44$\times$ increase in effect size (from $d=2.69$ to $d=6.57$), establishing that prior work using $n=5$--$20$ systematically underestimates signal strength.

\section{Defense Mechanism Geometry}\label{app:defense}

Following Vaillant's hierarchy \citep{vaillant1986empirical}, we analyze nine defense mechanisms across five maturity levels. We extend this analysis from one to five models (Qwen2.5-0.5B, Qwen3-0.6B, TinyLlama, Llama-3.2-1B, Gemma-2B). The Vaillant hierarchy (Immature $>$ Neurotic $>$ Mature within-group cosine similarity) holds in 4/5 models, with immature/mature ratios of 4.1$\times$, 1.8$\times$, 5.0$\times$, and 2.5$\times$ (Qwen3-0.6B is the exception, with NaN mature-mature similarity due to a single mature pair). Under the null hypothesis of random ordering, the probability of 4/5 models showing the correct strict ordering is $(1/6)^4 \times 5/6 \approx 7.7{\times}10^{-4}$, providing strong evidence that the hierarchy reflects a general property of instruction-tuned LLMs rather than an artifact of a single model.

Cross-framework analysis reveals moderate separation between Big Five traits and defense mechanisms: the maximum $|\cos|$ between any BF vector and any defense vector ranges from 0.331 (TinyLlama) to 0.515 (Gemma-2B), with a mean of $\sim$0.16. This suggests that personality traits and defense mechanisms occupy overlapping but distinguishable subspaces, consistent with psychological theory where defense mechanisms are partially personality-driven but have independent variance.

\section{Control Experiments}\label{app:control}

\subsection{Position-Swap Control}\label{app:position_swap}

To address concerns that user-token causal dominance might reflect positional artifacts (recency effect), we reverse the prompt layout: placing the neutral scenario in the system role and the persona instruction in the user role.

Results reveal that causal influence remains concentrated at sequence-final positions regardless of content placement. This confirms a positional component consistent with the autoregressive bottleneck: the model's output distribution is most sensitive to activations nearest the generation point. However, early-layer specificity persists regardless of content placement, confirming attention-mediated propagation as the operative mechanism.

\subsection{Paraphrase Control}\label{app:paraphrase}

To rule out keyword confounding, we train probes on Template A (original prompts) and test on Template B (paraphrased versions), achieving 91.2\% mean cross-template accuracy (range: 87.5--100\%). Openness achieves perfect bidirectional transfer (1.000), confirming abstract conceptual representation rather than surface lexical cues.

\subsection{Out-of-Domain Generalization}\label{app:ood}

When extracting vectors from non-overlapping 50\% splits, OOD stability is strong for larger models (Llama-3.2 mean 0.988, Gemma-2 mean 0.914) and moderate for smaller models (Qwen2.5 mean 0.801). Openness shows exceptional stability (mean $\cos=0.935$), suggesting abstract conceptual encoding, while Extraversion and Agreeableness show context-dependent encoding.

\section{Algorithm Pseudocode}\label{app:algorithm}

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\fbox{\parbox{0.92\textwidth}{\small
\textbf{Algorithm 1:} PersonaLens --- Standardized Personality Vector Extraction\\[4pt]
\textbf{Input:} Model $f_\theta$, Trait $t$, Contrastive pairs $\{(x^+_i, x^-_i)\}_{i=1}^K$\\
\textbf{Output:} Persona vectors $\{v_\ell\}_{\ell=0}^{L-1}$, Encoding profile, Causal map\\[4pt]
\textit{// Phase 2: Collect Activations}\\
1:\quad \textbf{for} $i = 1$ \textbf{to} $K$ \textbf{do}\\
2:\quad\quad $\{h_\ell(x^+_i)\}_{\ell=0}^{L-1} \leftarrow \text{ForwardPass}(f_\theta, x^+_i)$\\
3:\quad\quad $\{h_\ell(x^-_i)\}_{\ell=0}^{L-1} \leftarrow \text{ForwardPass}(f_\theta, x^-_i)$\\
4:\quad \textbf{end for}\\[4pt]
\textit{// Phase 3: Extract Vectors}\\
5:\quad \textbf{for} $\ell = 0$ \textbf{to} $L-1$ \textbf{do}\\
6:\quad\quad $v^{\text{diff}}_\ell \leftarrow \frac{1}{K}\sum_i [h_\ell(x^+_i) - h_\ell(x^-_i)]$ \hfill \textit{// Mean difference}\\
7:\quad\quad $w_\ell, a_\ell \leftarrow \text{LogisticRegression}(H^+_\ell, H^-_\ell)$ \hfill \textit{// Linear probe}\\
8:\quad\quad $u_\ell, \rho_\ell \leftarrow \text{PCA}(\{h_\ell(x^+_i) - h_\ell(x^-_i)\}_i)$ \hfill \textit{// PCA direction}\\
9:\quad \textbf{end for}\\[4pt]
\textit{// Phase 4: Causal Localization}\\
10:\quad \textbf{for} $\ell = 0$ \textbf{to} $L-1$ \textbf{do}\\
11:\quad\quad $\mathcal{I}(\ell) \leftarrow D_{\text{KL}}(\text{Clean}(x^+) \| \text{Patched}_\ell(x^+, h_\ell(x^-)))$\\
12:\quad \textbf{end for}\\
13:\quad \textbf{return} $\{v_\ell, a_\ell, \|v_\ell\|, \rho_\ell, \mathcal{I}(\ell)\}_\ell$
}}
\label{alg:personalens}
\end{figure}

\section{Perplexity Calculation Methodology}\label{app:ppl}

\begin{equation}
\text{PPL} = \exp\left(-\frac{1}{T}\sum_{t=1}^{T} \log p_\theta(w_t | w_{<t})\right)
\end{equation}
where $p_\theta$ is the unmodified (un-steered) model's probability distribution. We compute perplexity on the \emph{full sequence} (prompt + generated response), using the base model without steering hooks active. The threshold of PPL $< 10$ was selected empirically: manual inspection revealed that outputs with PPL $> 10$ consistently exhibited token-level repetition loops, grammatical breakdown, or semantic incoherence. We acknowledge that absolute PPL values are not directly comparable across architectures due to different tokenizers and vocabulary sizes; the threshold serves as a within-model fluency gate rather than a cross-model metric.

\section{LLM-as-Judge Rubric}\label{app:judge_rubric}

The judge (Gemma-2-2B-it) evaluates each response on a 1--5 BFI scale using the following structured prompt:

\begin{figure}[H]
\fbox{\parbox{0.92\textwidth}{\small
\textbf{Judge Prompt Template:}\\[4pt]
You are a personality psychology expert. Rate how strongly the following response expresses the personality trait \textsc{[TRAIT]} on a scale of 1--5, where:\\
1 = Very low (opposite extreme of the trait)\\
2 = Low expression\\
3 = Moderate / neutral\\
4 = High expression\\
5 = Very high (extreme expression)\\[4pt]
\textbf{Trait definition}: [BFI trait definition from Costa \& McCrae] \\
\textbf{Prompt}: [scenario prompt] \\
\textbf{Response}: [model response] \\[4pt]
First, provide a 1--2 sentence reasoning. Then give your rating on the final line as:\\
RATING: [1--5]
}}
\end{figure}

To reduce position bias, each response is judged independently (not compared to other responses). The BFI trait definitions are taken verbatim from \citet{costa1992revised}. All 14 models use the standardized V2 protocol (7 scenarios $\times$ 5 repetitions per ($\alpha$, trait) condition).

\section{Cross-Probe Stability Across Layers}\label{app:crossprobe_layers}

The O-E representational collapse reported in Sec.~\ref{sec:oe_collapse} is not an artifact of layer selection. On Llama-3-8B, we compute the O-E cross-probe accuracy at every layer (L0--L31). The O-E cross-probe exceeds 0.70 at all layers from L4 to L27, peaking at 0.94 at L14 (the steering layer). The C-N pair remains below 0.35 at all layers. This confirms that the representational hierarchy is a stable property of the activation space, not dependent on which layer is analyzed. The same pattern holds across all fourteen models: O-E cross-probe exceeds C-N cross-probe at every layer by a margin of at least 0.30.

\section{Inter-Judge Agreement}\label{app:interjudge}

To validate our LLM-as-Judge evaluation, we score a balanced subset of 420 responses (3 $\alpha$ values $\times$ 2 scenarios $\times$ 5 traits $\times$ 14 models) with a secondary judge (Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct) that differs from the primary judge (Gemma-2-2B-it) in architecture, parameter count, and training data.

\paragraph{Agreement Metrics.} The two judges achieve Pearson $r{=}0.73$ ($p{<}0.001$), Spearman $\rho{=}0.73$, exact agreement of 33.8\%, and 91.4\% of ratings within $\pm1$ point (MAE${=}0.76$). The moderate exact agreement is expected given the 1--5 integer scale: a rating of 3 vs.\ 4 reflects the same directional judgment with a borderline difference. The within-$\pm1$ rate of 91.4\% confirms that both judges consistently identify the direction and approximate magnitude of personality expression. Critically, both judges detect the same monotonic dose-response relationship: when restricted to the $\alpha$-sweeps, the Spearman correlation between $\alpha$ and judge rating is significant ($p{<}0.05$) for the same 50/70 model-trait pairs under both judges.

\paragraph{Shuffle-Label Baseline Coverage.} We extend the shuffle-label null baseline to 13/14 models (all except Qwen2.5-14B, for which raw activations were not retained). In 12/13 models, genuine off-diagonal $|\cos|$ differs significantly from the shuffled distribution ($p{<}0.01$), confirming that trait entanglement reflects genuine representational structure rather than pipeline artifacts. The exception is Mistral-7B ($p{=}0.23$), where genuine and shuffled correlations are similar, consistent with its near-orthogonal Big Five vectors.
