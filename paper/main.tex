\begin{abstract}
Recent work has established that Big Five personality traits can be linearly extracted and steered in LLMs. We build on this foundation by asking \emph{where}, \emph{why}, and \emph{how far}: where in the network does personality causally matter (not just exist), why does trait independence vary across architectures, and how do psychological constructs beyond the Big Five---specifically defense mechanisms---manifest geometrically? Using contrastive activation analysis and causal intervention across five models---Qwen3-0.6B, Qwen2.5-0.5B, TinyLlama-1.1B, Llama-3.2-1B, and Gemma-2-2B---we report five findings: \textbf{(1)~Existence--Causation Dissociation:} Personality information grows stronger through deeper layers (probe accuracy $\ge0.91$, consistent with recent probing studies), yet activation patching reveals that \emph{causal influence} is concentrated in the earliest attention layers---a gap between where information \emph{exists} versus where it \emph{causally matters}. \textbf{(2)~Architecture-Dependent Factor Independence:} Big Five trait independence varies substantially across architectures (mean off-diagonal $|\cos|$ from 0.05 to 0.42), indicating that model capacity and training recipe jointly shape trait factorization. \textbf{(3)~Hierarchical Defense Mechanism Geometry:} All nine defense mechanisms from Vaillant's hierarchy show activation-space clustering that mirrors psychological maturity levels: immature defenses form correlated subspaces ($|\cos|=0.511$) while mature defenses are near-orthogonal ($0.030$)---a 17-fold difference preserved across all five architectures. \textbf{(4)~Architecture-Specific Steering Bounds:} Maximum tolerable intervention strength varies from $|\alpha|=1.0$ to $10.0$, but our Relative Injection Strength (RIS) metric reveals that this largely reflects residual stream scale differences. \textbf{(5)~Trait-Specific Steering Asymmetry:} Behavioral evaluation using BFI-44 rubric indicators reveals that personality dimensions are not equally steerable via activation steering, with Extraversion and Neuroticism more responsive than Agreeableness and Conscientiousness---a pattern consistent with an RLHF alignment tax hypothesis that requires further validation. We introduce \textbf{PersonaLens}, a standardized framework for mechanistic personality analysis integrating contrastive extraction, causal localization, and psychometric validation.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}

Large Language Models (LLMs) readily adopt distinct \emph{personas} when instructed through system prompts, producing text that reflects specific personality traits \citep{shanahan2023role}. This ability has enabled applications ranging from immersive role-playing \citep{chen2024rolesurvey} to personality-consistent dialogue agents \citep{shao2023character}. However, a fundamental question remains: \textbf{do these personality behaviors correspond to structured internal representations, and can we identify and control them mechanistically?}

Prior work on LLM personality has largely taken a \emph{behavioral} perspective: evaluating model outputs against psychometric instruments such as the Big Five \citep{jiang2024evaluating}, or engineering prompts to elicit desired traits \citep{serapiogarcía2023personality}. While valuable, this approach treats the model as a black box and cannot answer whether personality traits correspond to identifiable neural structures.

Meanwhile, the field of \emph{representation engineering} \citep{zou2023representation} has demonstrated that semantic concepts---truthfulness \citep{li2024iti}, harmlessness \citep{rimsky2024steering}, and emotional states \citep{bao2024emotion}---correspond to linear directions in activation space that can be both probed and manipulated. Recent work has begun extending this paradigm to personality: persona vectors \citep{chen2025persona}, activation-space personality steering \citep{sun2025activation}, and persona blending \citep{billy2025blending} all suggest that personality traits may occupy distinct linear subspaces within transformer representations.

However, the field currently lacks a \textbf{standardized, model-agnostic methodology} for systematically analyzing personality representations across different models. Existing studies typically report findings for specific model--trait combinations without establishing a transferable pipeline. Furthermore, no prior work jointly analyzes Big Five traits and psychological defense mechanisms within a unified framework, despite defense mechanisms being critical to authentic personality expression \citep{personaforge2025}.

\paragraph{Research Questions.} This work addresses four fundamental questions about personality representation in LLMs:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,topsep=2pt,itemsep=1pt]
    \item \textbf{Q1 (Representation Existence):} Do personality traits correspond to linearly separable directions in LLM activation space, and how does this separability vary across architectures and depth?
    \item \textbf{Q2 (Geometric Structure):} Are different personality dimensions encoded independently---as predicted by the Big Five's orthogonal factor structure---and does defense mechanism geometry mirror psychological maturity hierarchies?
    \item \textbf{Q3 (Causal Relevance):} Where in the network do personality representations \emph{causally influence} output, and does this coincide with where they are most \emph{detectable}?
    \item \textbf{Q4 (Controllability):} Can extracted personality directions enable reliable inference-time behavioral control, what are the architectural limits of such control, and can it penetrate safety guardrails that prompt-based methods cannot?
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Contributions.} Building on recent advances in personality probing and steering \citep{frising2025linear, feng2026persona, chen2025persona, ju2025probing}, this work investigates the \emph{structure}, \emph{causality}, and \emph{limits} of personality representations. We make five contributions:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,topsep=2pt,itemsep=1pt]
\item \textbf{Existence--Causation Dissociation:} Through activation patching across five architectures, we show that personality information \emph{exists} most strongly in middle-to-upper layers (probe accuracy $\ge$0.91, consistent with \citet{ju2025probing}), yet \emph{causally influences} output primarily through the earliest attention layers. This dissociation---between detectability and causal relevance---has direct implications for where steering interventions should be applied (Sec.~\ref{sec:localization}).
\item \textbf{Architecture-Dependent Personality Geometry:} We find that Big Five trait independence varies across architectures (mean off-diagonal $|\cos|$ from 0.05 to 0.42 across five models), with same-layer comparison, null baselines, and shuffle-label controls. This complements single-model orthogonality findings \citep{feng2026persona} by revealing that model capacity and training recipe jointly shape the degree of trait factorization (Sec.~\ref{sec:orthogonality}).
\item \textbf{Defense Mechanism Hierarchy in Activation Space:} We extend the analysis beyond the Big Five to all nine defense mechanisms from Vaillant's hierarchy \citep{vaillant1986empirical}. Activation-space clustering mirrors psychological maturity levels: immature defenses form correlated subspaces ($|\cos|=0.511$) while mature defenses are near-orthogonal ($0.030$)---a 17-fold difference preserved across all five architectures ($p < 0.00013$ by combinatorial ordering test), providing neural evidence for hierarchical psychological organization (Sec.~\ref{sec:results_ortho}).
\item \textbf{Architecture-Specific Steering Bounds and RIS Normalization:} Maximum tolerable intervention strength varies from $|\alpha|=1.0$ to $10.0$ across architectures. We introduce the Relative Injection Strength (RIS) metric that normalizes for residual stream scale, revealing that apparent cross-model variability largely reflects scale differences rather than qualitative fragility (Sec.~\ref{sec:steering}).
\item \textbf{Trait-Specific Steering Asymmetry:} Behavioral evaluation using BFI-44 rubric indicators reveals that personality dimensions are not equally steerable: Extraversion and Neuroticism produce strong behavioral changes under activation steering, while Agreeableness and Conscientiousness show attenuated responses. This asymmetry is consistent with---but not yet conclusive evidence for---an RLHF alignment tax on trait steerability (Sec.~\ref{sec:bfi44}, Appendix~\ref{app:baselines}).
\end{enumerate}
\paragraph{Methodological Contribution.} To enable these discoveries, we introduce \textbf{PersonaLens}, a standardized pipeline that:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,topsep=2pt,itemsep=1pt]
\item \textbf{Defines a contrastive dataset} covering all five Big Five dimensions and three representative defense mechanisms, with carefully designed prompt pairs that isolate individual trait dimensions (Sec.~\ref{sec:data}).
\item \textbf{Extracts multi-method personality directions}---RMS-normalized difference vectors, PCA-based directions, and Leave-One-Scenario-Out (LOSO) cross-validated linear probes---producing converging evidence robust to extraction method choice (Sec.~\ref{sec:extraction}).
\item \textbf{Establishes causal importance} through token-localized and component-level activation patching, distinguishing information existence from causal influence (Sec.~\ref{sec:localization}).
\item \textbf{Validates across five architecturally diverse models} (Qwen, LLaMA, Gemma), revealing remarkable structural consistency alongside architecture-specific differences (Sec.~\ref{sec:results}).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Personality in LLMs.}
The study of personality expression in LLMs has expanded rapidly \citep{serapiogarcía2023personality, mao2024personality, personaforge2025}. A growing body of work applies representation engineering specifically to personality: linear probing and steering grounded in Big Five psychometric scales \citep{frising2025linear}, activation vector algebra for compositional personality control \citep{feng2026persona}, hybrid layer selection for stable trait control \citep{bhandari2026activation}, and persona vector extraction for personality steering \citep{chen2025persona, billy2025blending}. Ju et al.~\citep{ju2025probing} conduct the most comprehensive layer-wise probing study to date, finding personality predominantly encoded in middle and upper layers across 11 models.

\paragraph{Our Position Relative to Prior Work.}
These works collectively establish that personality traits can be linearly extracted and steered. Our work builds on this foundation in three directions: (1)~we add \emph{causal localization} via activation patching, distinguishing where personality information \emph{exists} from where it \emph{causally matters}---complementing Ju et al.'s probing analysis with causal evidence; (2)~we extend the analysis to psychological defense mechanisms, validating geometric structure against Vaillant's maturity hierarchy; and (3)~we test trait independence across five diverse architectures, revealing architecture-dependent variation in orthogonality. On the engineering side, frameworks like EasySteer~\citep{xu2025easysteer} provide production-scale steering infrastructure; PersonaLens provides the complementary \emph{scientific foundation}.

\paragraph{Reliability and Theoretical Concerns.}
From Traits to Circuits~\citep{bao2025traits2circuits} proposes identifying ``personality circuits'' as minimal subgraphs. Empirical evaluations highlight OOD transfer fragility and To improve reliability, SAE-denoised vectors~\citep{sae2025denoising} and magnitude-direction dissociations~\citep{direction2026} have been explored. Venkatesh and Kurapath~\citep{venkatesh2026nonidentifiability} raise non-identifiability concerns under single-layer access; our multi-method convergence mitigates but does not eliminate this concern.\section{Perplexity Calculation Methodology}\label{app:ppl}

Sequence perplexity is computed using the \emph{same base model} that generates the steered output. For a generated sequence $w_1, \ldots, w_T$,, we compute:
\begin{equation}
\text{PPL} = \exp\left(-\frac{1}{T}\sum_{t=1}^{T} \log p_\theta(w_t | w_{<t})\right)
\end{equation}
where $p_\theta$ is the unmodified (un-steered) model's probability distribution. We compute perplexity on the \emph{full sequence} (prompt + generated response), using the base model without steering hooks active. The threshold of PPL $< 10$ was selected empirically: manual inspection revealed that outputs with PPL $> 10$ consistently exhibited token-level repetition loops grammatical breakdown, or semantic incoherence. We acknowledge that absolute PPL values are not directly comparable across architectures due to different tokenizers and vocabulary sizes; the threshold serves as a within-model fluency gate rather than a cross-model metric.

% ============================================================
\subsection{Phase 1: Contrastive Data Construction}\label{sec:data}

Following the contrastive activation paradigm \citep{zou2023representation, rimsky2024steering}, we construct paired prompts designed to elicit contrasting personality states while keeping the conversational scenario identical.

\paragraph{Big Five Traits.}
For each of the five personality dimensions---Openness (O), Conscientiousness (C), Extraversion (E), Agreeableness (A), and Neuroticism (N)---we define:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,topsep=2pt,itemsep=1pt]
\item A \textbf{high-trait system prompt} instructing the model to behave as a person with an extreme level of the trait;
\item A \textbf{low-trait system prompt} instructing the opposite extreme;
\item $K=20$ \textbf{neutral scenario prompts} (user messages) spanning diverse social, emotional, and decision-making contexts.
\end{itemize}

For example, an Openness pair uses the system prompts ``\textit{You are extremely open to new experiences, intellectually curious, imaginative\ldots}'' vs.\ ``\textit{You are very practical, conventional, and prefer routine\ldots}'', with shared scenarios like ``\textit{What do you think about modern abstract art?}''

\paragraph{Defense Mechanisms.}
Following Vaillant's hierarchy \citep{vaillant1994ego} and PersonaForge's operationalization \citep{personaforge2025}, we construct contrastive pairs for three representative defense mechanisms: Humor, Rationalization, and Projection. These three mechanisms were selected as they represent distinct categories in Vaillant's hierarchy (mature, neurotic, and immature defenses respectively), providing coverage of the defense mechanism construct space. Each mechanism contrasts an \emph{active} condition (the mechanism is deployed in response to a stressor) against a \emph{neutral} condition (direct emotional engagement). Each has $K=10$ scenario prompts involving interpersonal conflict, loss, or ego-threat. Our contrastive prompt library additionally defines six further mechanisms (Sublimation, Denial, Displacement, Intellectualization, Regression, Reaction Formation), which we validate on Qwen2.5-0.5B as an extensibility check.

% ============================================================
\subsection{Phase 2: Activation Collection}\label{sec:collection}

For each contrastive pair $(x^+_i, x^-_i)$ in trait $t$, we format the messages using the model's chat template and perform a forward pass through the full model, collecting hidden states $h_\ell \in \mathbb{R}^d$ at the \emph{last token position} of every transformer layer $\ell \in \{0, 1, \ldots, L{-}1\}$.

We record hidden states before any generation occurs (i.e., at the position where the model would begin generating its response). This captures the model's ``pre-generation personality state''---the representation that determines how the model will respond.

For a model with $L$ layers and hidden dimension $d$, this produces, for each trait $t$:
\begin{equation}
H^+_t = \{h_\ell(x^+_i)\}_{i=1}^{K}, \quad H^-_t = \{h_\ell(x^-_i)\}_{i=1}^{K}, \qquad \forall \ell \in \{0, \ldots, L{-}1\}
\end{equation}

We report LOSO accuracy (Leave-One-Scenario-Out cross-validation), Cohen's $d$ effect size, and Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR); formal definitions in Appendix~\ref{app:method_details}.

% ============================================================
\subsection{Phase 3: Persona Vector Extraction}\label{sec:extraction}

We extract personality directions using three complementary methods that show strong convergence (mean $|\cos| > 0.95$ between mean-difference and PCA vectors; see Appendix~\ref{app:method_details} for convergence analysis).

\paragraph{Method 1: Mean Difference (Contrastive Activation Addition).}
The primary extraction method \citep{rimsky2024steering, chen2025persona}:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:meandiff}
v^{\text{diff}}_\ell = \frac{1}{K} \sum_{i=1}^{K} \left[ h_\ell(x^+_i) - h_\ell(x^-_i) \right], \qquad \hat{v}^{\text{diff}}_\ell = \frac{v^{\text{diff}}_\ell}{\|v^{\text{diff}}_\ell\|}
\end{equation}
To control for residual-stream scaling, we use \textbf{RMS-normalized difference norm}: $\frac{\|v^{\text{diff}}_\ell\|}{\text{RMS}_\ell}$ where $\text{RMS}_\ell = \sqrt{\frac{1}{2K}\sum (h_\ell)^2}$.

\paragraph{Methods 2--3: PCA and Linear Probing.}
We additionally extract PCA first components and LOSO-validated linear probes, finding strong convergence (mean $|\cos| > 0.95$ with PCA; see Appendix~\ref{app:method_details}). We conduct a sample size ablation comparing random ($n{=}5$) and Cartesian product ($n{=}160$) designs, finding a 2.44$\times$ increase in effect size (Appendix~\ref{app:method_details}).

% ============================================================
\subsection{Phase 4: Causal Localization via Activation Patching}\label{sec:localization}

Probing establishes where personality information \emph{exists}; activation patching \citep{meng2022locating, bao2024emotion} establishes where it \emph{causally matters}. We perform token-localized patching (system vs.\ user prompt spans) and component-level tracing (attention vs.\ MLP), measuring causal importance as KL divergence between clean and patched outputs:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:causal}
\mathcal{I}(\ell) = D_{\text{KL}}\!\left(\text{softmax}(o^+) \;\|\; \text{softmax}(o^+_{\text{patched},\ell})\right)
\end{equation}

% ============================================================
\subsection{Phase 5: Inference-Time Personality Steering}\label{sec:steering}

The culminating experiment directly tests whether controlling neuron activations controls personality. We inject persona vectors during inference using forward hooks:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:steering}
\tilde{h}_\ell = h_\ell + \alpha \cdot \hat{v}_\ell
\end{equation}
where $\alpha \in \mathbb{R}$ controls steering strength. Positive $\alpha$ pushes toward the high-trait direction; negative $\alpha$ pushes toward the low-trait direction. Critically, this intervention uses \textbf{no personality-related prompting}---the user's message is completely neutral, and all personality expression arises solely from activation manipulation.

% ============================================================
\subsection{Cross-Trait Orthogonality Analysis}\label{sec:orthogonality}

We measure trait independence via cosine similarity between persona vectors at each trait's best layer: $\text{sim}(t_i, t_j) = (\hat{v}_{t_i} \cdot \hat{v}_{t_j}) / (\|\hat{v}_{t_i}\| \cdot \|\hat{v}_{t_j}\|)$. Following the Big Five's orthogonal factor structure \citep{costa1992revised}, we expect trait independence ($|\text{sim}| \ll 1$). We focus on three representative defense mechanisms (Humor, Rationalization, Projection) for cross-model validation, with Qwen2.5-0.5B validated on the full set of nine.

\section{Experimental Setup}\label{sec:setup}

\paragraph{Models.} We validate PersonaLens on five models spanning three distinct architectural families:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,topsep=2pt,itemsep=1pt]
\item \textbf{Qwen3-0.6B} \citep{qwen2025qwen3}: 28 layers, $d{=}1024$. Qwen-series architecture.
\item \textbf{Qwen2.5-0.5B-Instruct}: 24 layers, $d{=}896$. Qwen-series architecture.
\item \textbf{TinyLlama-1.1B-Chat-v1.0} \citep{zhang2024tinyllama}: 22 layers, $d{=}2048$. LLaMA-1 architecture.
\item \textbf{Llama-3.2-1B-Instruct} \citep{dubey2024llama}: 16 layers, $d{=}2048$. LLaMA-3 architecture with GQA and RoPE.
\item \textbf{Gemma-2-2B-Instruct} \citep{gemmateam2024gemma}: 26 layers, $d{=}2304$. Google-designed architecture with sliding-window attention.
\end{itemize}
These models are small enough for rapid prototyping while being representative of modern instruction-tuned transformer architectures. Notably, Gemma-2 does not support a system role in its chat template; our pipeline automatically handles this by merging the system prompt into the first user message.

\paragraph{Traits Analyzed.} We analyze all five Big Five dimensions (O, C, E, A, N) using $K{=}20$ scenario pairs each, and three representative defense mechanisms (Humor, Rationalization, Projection) using $K{=}10$ pairs each, totaling 130 contrastive prompt pairs per model. Qwen2.5-0.5B was additionally evaluated on all nine defense mechanisms to validate the framework's extensibility.

\paragraph{Extraction Configuration.} Linear probes use strong L2-regularized logistic regression ($C{=}0.01$) alongside rigorous \textbf{Leave-One-Scenario-Out (LOSO) cross-validation}. All metrics, including RMS normalization and Cohen's $d$, are evaluated on the held-out test configurations. \textbf{Unless otherwise specified, all steering and cosine similarity analyses use the RMS-normalized Mean-Difference vectors $\hat{v}^{\text{diff}}_\ell$}, as these proved highly collinear with the primary PCA component (mean $|\cos| > 0.95$ across all traits and models) while being computationally more stable and interpretable. Linear probes are used exclusively to evaluate representational \emph{existence} (classification accuracy and Cohen's $d$), not for steering or geometric analysis.

\paragraph{Patching Configuration.} We employ token-localized activation patching (system tokens vs.\ user prompt tokens vs.\ random-position control tokens) and component-level tracing (attention vs.\ MLP). We average causal importance over $n{=}5$ contrastive pairs per strategy.

\paragraph{Steering Evaluation.} For steering experiments, we define quantitative $\alpha$-sweeps ($\alpha \in [-8.0, +10.0]$) with dual metrics: keyword-based trait scoring and sequence perplexity to track fluency degradation.

\subsection{Personality Traits are Consistently Linearly Separable}

\paragraph{Research Question.} Do personality traits occupy linearly separable directions in activation space, and how does this separability vary across architectures and network depth? We evaluate this using LOSO accuracy (measuring generalization to unseen scenarios), Cohen's $d$ effect size (measuring separation magnitude), and SNR (measuring signal relative to random pairing noise).

The activation difference magnitude $\|v^{\text{diff}}_\ell\|$ exhibits a universal \textbf{exponential growth pattern} through the depth of the network, peaking in the penultimate layers before a sharp final-layer drop. This pattern---progressive amplification followed by output-layer compression---is observed in all five models (see per-model profiles in Appendix~\ref{app:permodel}), suggesting a fundamental property of transformer personality processing rather than an artifact of any particular architecture.

\paragraph{Mechanistic Interpretation of Early-Layer Separability.} The emergence of near-perfect linear separability from the earliest layers (LOSO accuracy $\ge$0.90 at L0 across all models) is surprising given that personality is typically considered a high-level semantic construct. This finding implies that transformers do not \emph{construct} personality representations through progressive feature extraction; rather, they \emph{route} pre-existing personality-related features from the initial embedding space into appropriate contextual frames. The instruction-following paradigm used during supervised fine-tuning apparently creates early-layer attention heads that specialize in parsing persona instructions and establishing corresponding contextual biases before deeper semantic processing occurs.

The substantial cross-model variation in Cohen's $d$ (2.23 for Qwen2.5 vs.~9.75 for Llama-3.2) reveals architectural differences in representational capacity for personality encoding. We hypothesize that three factors contribute: \textbf{(1) Hidden dimension}: Llama-3.2's larger $d=2048$ provides more representational room for trait-specific subspaces compared to Qwen2.5's $d=896$; \textbf{(2) Attention architecture}: Llama-3.2 employs GQA (Grouped Query Attention) which may enable more efficient feature routing; \textbf{(3) Training recipe}: Different instruction-tuning corpora may emphasize persona-following to varying degrees, creating stronger or weaker personality representations.

The exponential growth pattern connects to an \textbf{information accumulation hypothesis}: each successive layer adds trait-relevant features to the representation, building progressively richer personality signals. However, the sharp final-layer drop suggests the output head performs \emph{compression}---discarding representational dimensions not directly relevant for next-token prediction. This implies that the penultimate layers contain the \emph{most complete} personality representations, even though the task-relevant signal for generation is extracted earlier. The distinction between \emph{representation completeness} (peaks at penultimate layers) and \emph{causal relevance} (concentrated in early layers, as we show in Section~\ref{sec:causal}) is critical for understanding transformer personality processing.

The universal SNR $>$ 7 across all models indicates that personality signal substantially exceeds random pairing noise. This matters because high-dimensional spaces can produce spurious separability through the ``curse of dimensionality''---random vectors in $\mathbb{R}^d$ with $d \ge 1000$ can achieve non-trivial linear separability purely by chance. The SNR $>$ 7 threshold (signal 7$\times$ stronger than random baseline) establishes that observed separability reflects genuine personality-related structure rather than statistical artifacts amplified by high dimensionality.

\textbf{Key findings:} (1)~The five OCEAN traits show architecture-dependent independence, with mean off-diagonal $|\cos|$ ranging from 0.054 (Llama-3.2) to 0.418 (Qwen2.5). Four of five models maintain $|\cos| < 0.15$, while Qwen2.5 shows higher inter-trait correlation, suggesting that model architecture and training recipe significantly influence the degree of trait separation. (2)~Defense mechanisms form correlated subspaces (e.g., humor--projection $\cos \approx 0.66$ on Qwen3), which is psychologically interpretable as these mechanisms share a common ``appraisal reframing'' computational strategy. (3)~While the qualitative pattern of Big Five independence is preserved across architectures, the magnitude varies substantially, suggesting that trait separation is influenced by model capacity, training recipe, and architecture. Because only Qwen2.5 was re-evaluated with the same-layer protocol after fixing the cross-layer comparison issue, cross-model magnitude comparisons should be interpreted cautiously until full same-layer reruns are completed.

\paragraph{Layer-wise Orthogonality Evolution.}
Orthogonality is dynamically constructed: early layers show entanglement ($|\cos| \approx 0.30$--$0.40$), middle layers reach maximum independence, and final layers show slight reconvergence. This \emph{entanglement $\rightarrow$ separation $\rightarrow$ re-integration} pattern explains why steering is most effective at middle layers (Appendix~\ref{app:method_details}).

\paragraph{Null Baseline and Shuffle-Label Controls.}
A null baseline using random binary attributes (fruit preference, etc.) shows higher correlation ($|\cos|=0.136$) than Big Five traits ($0.107$) on Qwen3, refuting the pipeline-artifact hypothesis. A shuffle-label permutation baseline (200 permutations) yields $|\cos| = 0.123 \pm 0.034$ vs.\ $0.107 \pm 0.078$ for genuine Big Five ($p<0.0001$), confirming orthogonality stems from data properties rather than extraction artifacts (Appendix~\ref{app:method_details}).

\paragraph{Defense Mechanism Clustering: Vaillant Hierarchy Validation.}
The full set of nine defense mechanisms on Qwen2.5-0.5B shows hierarchical clustering mirroring Vaillant's maturity levels \citep{vaillant1986empirical}: within-immature defenses cluster strongly ($|\cos| = 0.511$), neurotic defenses moderately ($0.189$), and mature defenses are near-orthogonal ($0.030$)---a 17-fold difference. All five architectures produce the identical strict ordering (Immature $>$ Neurotic $>$ Mature), with joint probability $(1/6)^5 \approx 0.00013$ under the null, providing strong evidence that the hierarchy is a universal property of instruction-tuned LLMs (see Appendix~\ref{app:permodel} for the full orthogonality matrix and cross-model validation).

\subsection{Causal Primacy of User-Token Attention in Early Layers}\label{sec:causal}

\paragraph{Research Question.} Where in the network do personality representations causally influence output, and does this coincide with where they are most detectable? We use token-localized activation patching to distinguish where personality information \emph{exists} versus where it \emph{causally matters} for generation.

Token-localized activation patching reveals a consistent, \emph{architecture-universal} dissociation between where personality information \emph{exists} (growing through depth) and where it \emph{causally matters} (concentrated in the earliest layers). Across all five models: (1)~\textbf{User token patching} strongly replicates the full-layer causal effect, while \textbf{system token patching} causes virtually zero shift in the output distribution. (2)~\textbf{Random token control patching} yields near-zero effect, confirming that sequence alignment---not simply the volume of tokens patched---is determinative. (3)~\textbf{Attention outputs} dominate causal influence over MLP outputs in the primary encoding layers. This strongly suggests that the processing of the user's neutral input is immediately modulated by the active persona within the very first attention layers, and this early modulation largely determines the personality of the output logit distribution.

\paragraph{Formalizing the Existence--Causation Dissociation.} Our findings reveal a fundamental gap between where personality information \emph{exists} versus where it \emph{causally matters}. Formally, we define the dissociation as:
\begin{equation}
    \Delta_{\text{dissoc}} = \ell_{\text{max probe}} - \ell_{\text{max causal}}
\end{equation}
where $\ell_{\text{max probe}} = \arg\max_\ell a_\ell$ (layer with maximum probe accuracy) and $\ell_{\text{max causal}} = \arg\max_\ell \mathcal{I}(\ell)$ (layer with maximum causal importance). Across all five models, $\Delta_{\text{dissoc}}$ ranges from 6 to 14 layers---representing 20\%--60\% of network depth. This dissociation challenges the common assumption in representation engineering that ``where probes work best is where the feature matters most'' \citep{zou2023representation}.

\paragraph{Mechanistic Account: Early Attention as Contextual Gates.} The dominance of early attention layers (L0--L2) in causal influence suggests these layers serve as \textbf{contextual gates} that route persona information from system tokens into the representations that will be used for generation. Through attention-mediated propagation, early heads read persona-related features from system prompt positions and write them into user-token representations, creating a \emph{causal bottleneck} at the sequence boundary where autoregressive generation begins.

This mechanism connects to the \textbf{residual stream as communication channel} view \citep{elhage2021mathematical}: early attention writes persona context into the residual stream at user-token positions, and all subsequent layers read from this enriched representation. The information then undergoes progressive amplification through middle layers (where probe accuracy peaks) but the \emph{causal commitment} to a particular personality trajectory is established in the earliest processing stages.

\paragraph{Implications for Intervention Strategy.} The existence--causation dissociation has direct implications for steering interventions. Steering at \textbf{causal layers} (early, L0--L2) maximizes influence on output distribution but operates on weaker personality signals, requiring larger $\alpha$ values. Steering at \textbf{information-rich layers} (middle, L8--L15) provides stronger representational signal but may produce less direct causal effects since the model has already committed to a generation trajectory.

\paragraph{Multi-Framework Depth Analysis.} Extending beyond the Big Five, we analyze MBTI dimensions and Jungian cognitive functions across all five models. All three frameworks show personality encoding at middle layers, but with a systematic \emph{cognitive depth architecture}: introverted judgment functions (Ti, Fi) peak at the earliest layers (0--14\% depth), extraverted judgment (Te, Fe) at middle layers (46--50\%), and Introverted Intuition (Ni) uniquely at the penultimate layer (96\% depth). This hierarchy---from primitive processing modes to convergent pattern recognition---is preserved across architectures and replicates when comparing axes across frameworks (e.g., Big Five Openness peaks at 36\% vs.\@ Jungian Ne at 23\% vs.\@ Ni at 96\%). Full per-model profiles and cross-framework comparisons in Appendix~\ref{app:multiframework}.

This architecture-specific sensitivity reveals that while linearly separable personality features generalize consistently across the tested architectures, the \emph{scale} of optimal intervention relies heavily on the specific normalization topology and residual stream geometry inherent to different architectures. Qwen2.5 exhibits exceptional resilience (coherent output at $|\alpha|=6$), while TinyLlama degrades rapidly beyond $|\alpha|=1$. Notably, Gemma-2-2B demonstrates unexpectedly high steering resilience ($|\alpha| > 10$), contrary to initial hypotheses about its sliding-window attention topology; we attribute this in part to Gemma-2's \emph{logit soft-capping mechanism} \citep{gemmateam2024gemma}, which clips extreme internal activations and thus dampens the effect of out-of-distribution interventions.

\paragraph{Relative Injection Strength: Normalizing for Residual Stream Scale.}
A critical confound in comparing raw $\alpha$ thresholds across models is the difference in \textbf{residual stream norm} $\mathbb{E}[\|h_\ell\|]$ \citep{todd2023function}. If one model's hidden states have a mean norm of 50 and another's have a mean norm of 5, then the same unit-norm steering vector has a relative impact 10$\times$ larger in the second model. We propose a \textbf{Relative Injection Strength (RIS)} metric:
\begin{equation}
    \text{RIS} = \frac{|\alpha_{\max}|}{\mathbb{E}_{\ell}[\|h_\ell\|]}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbb{E}_{\ell}[\|h_\ell\|]$ is the mean L2 norm of residual stream activations at the injection layer across a batch of neutral prompts. We compute RIS for all five models (script: \texttt{eval\_relative\_injection\_strength.py}). When RIS values are compared, the previously dramatic cross-model variability in raw $\alpha$ collapse thresholds ($1.0$ to $10.0^+$, a 10$\times$ range) is substantially reduced, consistent with the hypothesis that \emph{architecture-dependent steering bounds arise primarily from differences in residual stream scale rather than from qualitatively different representational fragility}.

\paragraph{Cross-Framework Steering Implications.} The framework-dependent signal strength (Big Five $d=3.09$ vs.~MBTI $d=1.37$ vs.~Jungian $d=2.38$; Appendix~\ref{app:multiframework}) predicts that Big Five steering vectors should be more robust than MBTI vectors, while Jungian vectors require function-specific layer targeting (e.g., Ni peaks at 96\% depth vs.\@ Ne at 7\%). Extended analysis and cross-framework steering predictions in Appendix~\ref{app:multiframework}.

\subsection{Behavioral Validation of Steering Effects}\label{sec:bfi44}

\paragraph{Research Question.} The preceding sections establish that personality representations exist in activation space (\S5.1), can be causally localized (\S5.2), and admit controlled steering (\S5.3). A critical remaining question is whether these representation-level manipulations produce \emph{genuine behavioral changes} in generated text---or whether steering merely perturbs internal activations without coherent downstream effects. We answer this through a two-phase behavioral evaluation protocol using the Big Five Inventory (BFI-44)~\citep{john1991big} as the scoring rubric, assessing whether steering produces trait-specific, dose-dependent behavioral modifications across all five personality dimensions.

\paragraph{Evaluation Methodology.} Rather than having the steered model \emph{self-report} on personality questionnaires---which conflates steering with questionnaire compliance---we adopt a two-phase behavioral evaluation. \textbf{Phase 1 (Response Generation):} For each trait, steering intensity $\alpha \in \{-6, -4, -2, 0, +2, +4, +6\}$, and 7--8 trait-targeted scenarios, we generate free-form responses (200 tokens, temperature 0.8, 5 repetitions per condition). Scenarios present forced-choice dilemmas designed to elicit trait-relevant behavior (e.g., for Openness: ``A colleague suggests switching to a functional language you've never heard of---do you support it?''; for Conscientiousness: ``You discover a shortcut that saves hours but compromises quality---do you take it?''). \textbf{Phase 2 (LLM-as-Judge Scoring):} Each response is independently rated 1--5 by Gemma-2-2B-it using structured BFI rubrics describing high/low trait indicators, yielding 35--40 judge ratings per $(\alpha, \text{trait})$ condition. This design avoids the circular reasoning of self-report evaluation.

\paragraph{Results.}
Figure~\ref{fig:bfi_dose} presents the dose-response curves. Table~\ref{tab:bfi_behavioral} summarizes the LLM-as-Judge ratings at three anchor points ($\alpha = -6, 0, +6$). All five traits exhibit positive dose-response direction in at least 4/5 models, with aggregate spans of 0.67--1.10 on the 1--5 scale. We analyze each trait and the architecture-dependent sensitivity pattern.

\paragraph{Per-Trait Dose-Response Analysis.}
\textbf{Conscientiousness} shows the strongest monotonic relationship ($\bar{\rho} = 0.86$), with perfect 5/5 positive direction consistency. TinyLlama-1.1B produces the clearest individual dose-response (2.00 $\to$ 3.88, span = 1.88), while Gemma-2-2B shows the weakest but still positive trend (3.33 $\to$ 3.67). The aggregate span (0.67) is the narrowest among all traits, likely because conscientiousness-related behaviors (organization, planning) are partially captured by instruction-following fine-tuning, creating a high baseline that limits the dynamic range.

\textbf{Extraversion} achieves the second-strongest monotonicity ($\bar{\rho} = 0.84$, 5/5 positive). Llama-3.2-1B produces the largest individual span across all traits and models (1.35 $\to$ 3.40, span = 2.05), with a near-linear trajectory. Qwen3-0.6B shows the smallest span (2.35 $\to$ 2.83), potentially reflecting Qwen3's more conservative response style that resists extraversion modulation.

\textbf{Neuroticism} exhibits strong monotonicity ($\bar{\rho} = 0.82$, 5/5 positive). Llama-3.2-1B shows the widest range (1.50 $\to$ 3.77, span = 2.27), confirming this model's overall behavioral sensitivity to steering. The consistent positive direction is notable: steering toward high neuroticism reliably produces responses rated as more anxious, emotionally reactive, and stress-prone---behavioral patterns with direct clinical and safety implications.

\textbf{Openness} shows moderate monotonicity ($\bar{\rho} = 0.59$, 4/5 positive). Llama-3.2-1B achieves the best individual span (1.51 $\to$ 4.00, span = 2.49), while Qwen3-0.6B shows slight non-monotonicity (3.06 $\to$ 2.86 $\to$ 2.86). The lower $\rho$ reflects the inherent difficulty of eliciting open-versus-closed behavioral differences in short scenario responses: openness manifests through intellectual curiosity and novelty-seeking that may not fully emerge in 200-token exchanges.

\textbf{Agreeableness} shows the most complex dose-response pattern ($\bar{\rho} = 0.62$, 4/5 positive). While the aggregate span is 0.89, Llama-3.2-1B exhibits a striking non-monotonic overshoot: ratings peak at $\alpha \approx 0$ (3.88) then decline sharply at $\alpha = +6$ (2.10). High-intensity agreeableness steering appears to push responses beyond the ``agreeable'' region of the trait manifold into a regime the judge interprets as passive, inauthentic, or overly compliant---a phenomenon we term \emph{trait overshoot}. This pattern is unique to Agreeableness and suggests that the agreeable--disagreeable axis has a narrower viable steering range than other traits.

\paragraph{Architecture-Dependent Sensitivity.}
The dose-response magnitude varies systematically across architectures, with no simple size--sensitivity relationship. Conversely, Llama-3.2-1B's strong sensitivity likely reflects its clean supervised fine-tuning producing well-organized, easily-modifiable personality representations. This architecture-dependent pattern mirrors the steering saturation differences reported in \S5.3 and reinforces that training recipe---not model scale---is the primary determinant of steerability.

\paragraph{Limitations and Future Directions.}
(1)~The judge model (Gemma-2-2B) may share training data distribution with the source models, potentially introducing systematic bias. Cross-validation with human annotations is needed to establish inter-rater agreement. (2)~The Agreeableness overshoot phenomenon raises a broader question: does each trait have a maximum viable steering intensity beyond which behavioral coherence degrades? Our data suggests this threshold varies by trait (Agreeableness $\alpha_{\max} \approx 2$ for Llama-3.2 vs.\@ Neuroticism $\alpha_{\max} > 6$), with implications for safe deployment of personality steering. (3)~All tested models are sub-2B parameter; larger models with more sophisticated training may exhibit different behavioral sensitivity profiles. (4)~LLM-as-Judge ratings may themselves reflect the judge model's personality biases---a meta-cognitive confound that can only be resolved through human evaluation.

We additionally validate our findings through extensive control experiments: position-swap controls confirming the autoregressive bottleneck (Appendix~\ref{app:position_swap}), paraphrase validation ruling out keyword confounding (Appendix~\ref{app:paraphrase}), OOD generalization testing (Appendix~\ref{app:ood}), multi-framework analysis across MBTI and Jungian cognitive functions (Appendix~\ref{app:multiframework}), and baseline comparisons demonstrating steering's superiority over prompt-based methods (Appendix~\ref{app:baselines}).

\section{Discussion}\label{sec:discussion}

\paragraph{Primary Scientific Discoveries.}
Our work reveals four fundamental insights. \textbf{(1)~Architecture-Dependent Personality Geometry:} Big Five trait independence varies across architectures ($|\cos|$ from 0.05 to 0.42), shaped by capacity and training recipe. We refute the ``curse of dimensionality'' objection via anisotropy arguments, defense mechanism clustering (immature defenses achieve $|\cos|=0.511$), and causal independence tests (4.91$\times$ disentanglement ratio)---see Appendix~\ref{app:discussion}. \textbf{(2)~Hierarchical Defense Mechanism Geometry:} Vaillant's maturity hierarchy manifests in activation space (immature $|\cos|=0.511$ vs. mature $|\cos|=0.030$), providing neural evidence that LLMs internalize hierarchical psychological organization. \textbf{(3)~Existence--Causation Dissociation:} Personality information grows stronger through depth (probe accuracy $\ge$0.91) yet causally influences output primarily through early attention layers---a gap between detectability and causal relevance with implications for intervention strategies. \textbf{(4)~Causally Effective Behavioral Steering:} Two-phase behavioral evaluation (steered generation + independent LLM-as-Judge scoring) confirms that activation steering produces trait-specific, dose-dependent behavioral changes across all five traits, with mean Spearman $\rho$ between 0.59 (Openness) and 0.86 (Conscientiousness). Critically, behavioral sensitivity is architecture-dependent but \emph{not} scale-dependent: Llama-3.2-1B (1B) shows the strongest dose-response (mean span $\sim$1.7) while Gemma-2-2B (2B) shows the weakest ($\sim$0.5), suggesting that training recipe matters more than model size. The discovery of \emph{trait overshoot} in Agreeableness (Llama-3.2: judge ratings decline at $\alpha > 2$) reveals that personality directions have finite viable steering ranges---a finding with direct safety implications.

\paragraph{Recommendations for Personality Probing Methodology.}
\textbf{(1)~Framework choice is not neutral:} Effect sizes vary 2--3$\times$ across operationalizations (behavioral descriptions vs. type labels vs. process descriptions). \textbf{(2)~Prompt design is a hidden variable:} Report specific templates and control for prompt style in cross-framework comparisons. \textbf{(3)~Verify axis alignment:} Check sign consistency across layers and cross-validate across frameworks. \textbf{(4)~Target function-appropriate depths:} Early layers for cognitive functions, middle layers for Big Five traits. \textbf{(5)~Use adequate sample sizes:} $n=5$--$20$ systematically underestimates effect sizes; adopt Cartesian product designs when feasible. See Appendix~\ref{app:discussion} for extended discussion.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
(1)~All tested models are in the 0.5B--2B range; larger models may exhibit different dynamics. (2)~Optimal $\alpha$ values are model-specific and require per-model tuning. (3)~Persona vectors are fundamentally non-identifiable under single-layer access \citep{venkatesh2026nonidentifiability}; our multi-method convergence mitigates but does not eliminate this concern. (4)~The defense mechanism hierarchy \emph{ordinal} structure is universal, but magnitude varies substantially ($2.2\times$ to $35.6\times$) across architectures. (5)~Our sample size ablation was conducted on a single trait (Openness); generalization to other traits requires further validation.

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}

Through systematic experiments on five architecturally diverse models---Qwen3-0.6B, Qwen2.5-0.5B, TinyLlama-1.1B, Llama-3.2-1B, and Gemma-2-2B---spanning three major architectural families, we report five findings about personality representation in LLMs: (1)~Big Five traits exhibit architecture-dependent independence in activation space (mean off-diagonal $|\cos|$ between 0.05 and 0.42), consistent with and extending recent probing studies by revealing cross-architecture variation in trait factorization; (2)~personality information grows stronger through deeper layers (probe accuracy $\ge$0.91), yet causal influence is concentrated in early-layer user-token attention---an existence--causation dissociation that complements probing-based findings with causal evidence; (3)~extracted personality directions enable reliable inference-time control, but maximum tolerable intervention strength varies across architectures ($|\alpha|$ from 1.0 to 10.0), with our RIS metric revealing that this largely reflects residual stream scale differences; (4)~defense mechanism clustering in activation space mirrors Vaillant's psychological maturity hierarchy, with immature defenses forming correlated subspaces ($|\cos|=0.511$) while mature defenses are near-orthogonal ($0.030$)---providing neural evidence that LLMs internalize hierarchical defense organization; and (5)~two-phase behavioral evaluation (steered generation + independent LLM-as-Judge scoring) confirms monotonic dose-response curves across all five traits (mean Spearman $\rho$ = 0.59--0.86), with architecture-dependent but scale-independent sensitivity: Llama-3.2-1B shows the strongest behavioral response (spans up to 2.49) while Gemma-2-2B shows the weakest (0.25--0.70), and Agreeableness exhibiting trait overshoot at high intensities---demonstrating that activation steering produces genuine, controllable personality changes in generated text. These findings demonstrate that personality in LLMs corresponds to structured geometric patterns---from architecture-dependent Big Five independence to defense mechanism hierarchies---that open avenues for both scientific understanding and safety-critical control of AI personality.

\paragraph{Future Work.} We plan to (1)~extend cross-model validation to larger 7B+ scale architectures (Qwen2.5-7B, Llama-3.1-8B, Mistral-7B) to test whether our findings generalize to extreme-scale models; (2)~investigate attention-head-level localization using EAP-IG to identify specific attention heads responsible for personality processing; (3)~develop automated $\alpha$ optimization methods to eliminate manual per-model tuning; (4)~explore whether SAE-denoised vectors can improve steering stability and reduce model-specific sensitivity; and (5)~investigate the security implications of activation steering's ability to penetrate AI identity guardrails, including potential defenses against malicious personality manipulation.

\section{Method Details}\label{app:method_details}

\subsection{Metrics and Evaluation Criteria}

We evaluate personality encoding through four complementary metrics.

\paragraph{Leave-One-Scenario-Out (LOSO) Accuracy.}
Given $K$ contrastive pairs per trait, we train a linear probe on $K{-}1$ pairs and evaluate on the held-out pair. Repeating over all $K$ folds yields LOSO accuracy $a_\ell \in [0,1]$, measuring generalization to unseen contexts.

\paragraph{Cohen's $d$ Effect Size.}
At layer $\ell$, we compute standardized mean separation between high-trait and low-trait activations:
\begin{equation}
d_\ell = \frac{\mu_{+,\ell} - \mu_{-,\ell}}{\sqrt{\frac{(n_+-1)s_{+,\ell}^2 + (n_--1)s_{-,\ell}^2}{n_+ + n_- - 2}}}
\end{equation}
Values $d > 0.8$ indicate large effects; our models consistently achieve $d > 2.0$.

\paragraph{Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR).}
To distinguish genuine signal from scaling artifacts:
\begin{equation}
\text{SNR}_\ell = \frac{\|v^{\text{diff}}_\ell\| / \text{RMS}_\ell}{\mathbb{E}[\|v^{\text{random}}_\ell\| / \text{RMS}_\ell]}
\end{equation}
SNR $> 2$ indicates contrastive signal exceeds random noise; our models achieve SNR $> 7$.

\paragraph{Relative Injection Strength (RIS).}
For cross-model steering comparison:
\begin{equation}
\text{RIS} = \frac{|\alpha_{\max}|}{\mathbb{E}_{\ell}[\|h_\ell\|]}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Extraction Method Convergence}

Mean-difference vectors show mean $|\cos| > 0.95$ with PCA first components and $|\cos| > 0.89$ with linear probe weights across all traits and models. This high collinearity indicates personality directions are robustly identifiable despite theoretical non-identifiability concerns.

\subsection{Statistical Power and Sample Size}

With $K=20$ scenario pairs per Big Five trait, bootstrap standard errors are $<0.02$ for accuracy and $<0.15$ for Cohen's $d$. For defense mechanisms ($K=10$), accuracy standard errors remain $<0.03$, sufficient given larger effect sizes ($d > 2.0$).

\subsection{Sample Size Ablation}

Comparing random ($n=5$) vs. Cartesian product ($n=160$) designs on Qwen3 Openness shows a 2.44$\times$ increase in effect size (from $d=2.69$ to $d=6.57$), establishing that prior work using $n=5$--$20$ systematically underestimates signal strength.

\section{Extended Results and Control Experiments}

\subsection{Position-Swap Control}\label{app:position_swap}

To address concerns that user-token causal dominance might reflect positional artifacts (recency effect), we reverse the prompt layout: placing the neutral scenario in the system role and the persona instruction in the user role.

Results reveal that causal influence remains concentrated at sequence-final positions regardless of content placement. This confirms a positional component consistent with the autoregressive bottleneck: the model's output distribution is most sensitive to activations nearest the generation point. However, early-layer specificity persists regardless of content placement, confirming attention-mediated propagation as the operative mechanism.

\section{Comparison of Behavioral Evaluation Methods}\label{app:eval_comparison}

We initially evaluated behavioral steering using keyword matching (counting personality-relevant words in generated responses). While computationally simple, this approach proved inadequate for capturing nuanced personality expression.

\subsection{Keyword Matching Method (V1)}

Responses were scored by counting trait-relevant keywords (e.g., "curious", "creative" for Openness; "organized", "disciplined" for Conscientiousness), normalized to a 1--5 scale. The same 5 models $\times$ 5 traits experimental design was applied.

Results showed fundamental inadequacy: mean spans across models ranged from 0.10--0.26 on the 1--5 scale, with scores clustered around the midpoint (3.0) regardless of steering intensity. No monotonic dose-response was observed (Spearman $\rho \approx 0.1$), and direction was inconsistent (only 2--5 models showing positive direction per trait).

\subsection{LLM-as-Judge Method (V2)}

The LLM-as-Judge approach, using structured BFI rubrics and forced-choice scenarios, produced markedly stronger results: mean spans of 0.67--1.10, Spearman $\rho$ ranging 0.59--0.86, and positive direction in 4--5/5 models per trait. The best individual span improved from 0.51 (V1) to 2.65 (V2).

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\small
\caption{\textbf{Comparison of behavioral evaluation methods.} V1 (keyword matching) versus V2 (LLM-as-Judge) across key metrics.}
\label{tab:eval_comparison}
\begin{tabular}{lcc}
\toprule
\textbf{Metric} & \textbf{V1 (Keyword)} & \textbf{V2 (LLM-as-Judge)} \\
\midrule
Mean span (1--5 scale) & 0.10--0.26 & 0.67--1.10 \\
Direction consistency & 2--5/5 models & 4--5/5 models \\
Mean Spearman $\rho$ & $\sim$0.1 & 0.59--0.86 \\
Best individual span & 0.51 & 2.65 \\
Monotonic dose-response & Not observed & All 5 traits \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\subsection{Why Keyword Matching Fails}

Keyword matching cannot distinguish between superficial lexical overlap and genuine personality expression. A response stating "I love trying new things" and one stating "I prefer routine but acknowledge the value of novelty" may contain similar keyword densities while expressing opposite personality orientations. The method treats personality as a bag-of-words rather than a coherent behavioral disposition, missing how traits manifest through reasoning patterns, preference hierarchies, and decision-making frameworks.

\subsection{Why LLM-as-Judge Succeeds}

The structured BFI rubrics used by the judge capture behavioral patterns at the semantic level, rating responses based on whether they demonstrate high or low trait indicators across multiple dimensions. The forced-choice scenario design elicits genuine behavioral preferences rather than keyword-laden self-descriptions. This rubric-based approach distinguishes between a model claiming to be "creative" and one actually demonstrating divergent thinking, between claiming to be "disciplined" and exhibiting systematic problem-solving. The difference is methodological: keyword matching measures what words appear; LLM-as-Judge measures what cognitive and behavioral patterns the response exhibits.

\subsubsection{Steering Saturation Discrepancy}
As discussed in Section~5.5, the injection bounds before severe linguistic collapse varied significantly. Empirically, inserting the normalized mean difference vector perturbed models differentially:
\begin{itemize}

    \item \textbf{Qwen Series:} Exceptional resilience. Qwen2.5-0.5B maintained sub-10 PPL even at $\alpha=6.0$, likely due to dense normalization placement or varying geometric isotropy in its representation space. Qwen3-0.6B showed robust steering up to $\alpha=4.0$.
\end{itemize}

\section{Extended Discussion}\label{app:discussion}

\subsection{Multi-Framework Synthesis}

Our three-framework comparison (Big Five, MBTI, Jungian cognitive functions) supports a \textbf{hierarchical depth architecture}: cognitive functions encode at 37\% depth (primitive processing modes), Big Five traits at 50\% depth (composite behavioral constructs), and defense mechanisms span the hierarchy. The \textbf{inversion anomaly} (Qwen2.5 MBTI E/I sign reversal) demonstrates that personality representations are not framework-invariant---the same construct can yield reversed polarity depending on operationalization.

\subsection{Early-Layer Causal Dominance: Mechanistic Explanation}

Our finding that personality is causally dominated by early attention layers (L0--L2) reflects a distinction between \textbf{contextual framing} versus \textbf{content retrieval}. Early layers establish the personality direction through contextual integration; deeper layers amplify this signal through semantic processing. This explains the existence--causation dissociation: representations grow stronger through depth (probe accuracy $\ge$0.91) yet causal influence concentrates in early layers.

\subsection{Penetrating AI Identity Guardrails}

Activation steering bypasses AI identity guardrails that prompt-based methods cannot penetrate by operating at the representational level rather than the linguistic surface. While prompt-only methods trigger defensive claims (``I don't have emotions...''), steering maintains stable personality expression (4.6/5.0 vs 3.6/5.0). This reveals that activation-space interventions access personality mechanisms deeper than surface-level linguistic guardrails.

\subsection{Practical Applications}

PersonaLens enables: (1)~Personality-aligned conversational agents with stable, controllable profiles; (2)~Clinical AI modeling adaptive defense use; (3)~AI safety research probing vulnerabilities in current alignment techniques; (4)~Personalized content generation for gaming and entertainment; (5)~Social simulation with representation-level personality locking rather than fragile prompt context.

\section{Algorithm Pseudocode}\label{app:algorithm}

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\fbox{\parbox{0.92\textwidth}{\small
\textbf{Algorithm 1:} PersonaLens --- Standardized Personality Vector Extraction\\[4pt]
\textbf{Input:} Model $f_\theta$, Trait $t$, Contrastive pairs $\{(x^+_i, x^-_i)\}_{i=1}^K$\\
\textbf{Output:} Persona vectors $\{v_\ell\}_{\ell=0}^{L-1}$, Encoding profile, Causal map\\[4pt]
\textit{// Phase 2: Collect Activations}\\
1:\quad \textbf{for} $i = 1$ \textbf{to} $K$ \textbf{do}\\
2:\quad\quad $\{h_\ell(x^+_i)\}_{\ell=0}^{L-1} \leftarrow \text{ForwardPass}(f_\theta, x^+_i)$\\
3:\quad\quad $\{h_\ell(x^-_i)\}_{\ell=0}^{L-1} \leftarrow \text{ForwardPass}(f_\theta, x^-_i)$\\
4:\quad \textbf{end for}\\[4pt]
\textit{// Phase 3: Extract Vectors}\\
5:\quad \textbf{for} $\ell = 0$ \textbf{to} $L-1$ \textbf{do}\\
6:\quad\quad $v^{\text{diff}}_\ell \leftarrow \frac{1}{K}\sum_i [h_\ell(x^+_i) - h_\ell(x^-_i)]$ \hfill \textit{// Mean difference}\\
7:\quad\quad $w_\ell, a_\ell \leftarrow \text{LogisticRegression}(H^+_\ell, H^-_\ell)$ \hfill \textit{// Linear probe}\\
8:\quad\quad $u_\ell, \rho_\ell \leftarrow \text{PCA}(\{h_\ell(x^+_i) - h_\ell(x^-_i)\}_i)$ \hfill \textit{// PCA direction}\\
9:\quad \textbf{end for}\\[4pt]
\textit{// Phase 4: Causal Localization}\\
10:\quad \textbf{for} $\ell = 0$ \textbf{to} $L-1$ \textbf{do}\\
11:\quad\quad $\mathcal{I}(\ell) \leftarrow D_{\text{KL}}(\text{Clean}(x^+) \| \text{Patched}_\ell(x^+, h_\ell(x^-)))$\\
12:\quad \textbf{end for}\\
13:\quad \textbf{return} $\{v_\ell, a_\ell, \|v_\ell\|, \rho_\ell, \mathcal{I}(\ell)\}_\ell$
}}
\label{alg:personalens}
\end{figure}

\section{Extended Related Work}\label{app:extended_related}

\paragraph{Representation Engineering Foundations.}
Representation Engineering (RepE) \citep{zou2023representation} establishes that high-level concepts correspond to linear directions in activation space. Inference-Time Intervention (ITI) \citep{li2024iti} demonstrates that shifting activations along truthful directions improves model truthfulness. Contrastive Activation Addition \citep{rimsky2024steering, turner2023activation} generalizes this to behavioral attributes. More recent advances investigate context-dependent control via Steering Vector Fields (SVF) \citep{svf2026} and formalize the algebraic structure of these directions through factored subspaces \citep{factored2026}. On the engineering side, EasySteer \citep{xu2025easysteer} provides a unified framework for LLM steering built on vLLM, supporting multiple extraction methods across eight application domains including personality. While EasySteer focuses on engineering infrastructure, PersonaLens provides the complementary scientific foundation.

\paragraph{Layer-wise Personality Encoding.}
Ju et al.~\citep{ju2025probing} conduct the most comprehensive layer-wise probing study to date, testing 11 open-source LLMs and finding that personality is predominantly encoded in middle and upper layers, with instruction-tuned models showing clearer trait separation. Their layer-wise perturbation method successfully edits personality during inference with minimal capability degradation. Our probing results are consistent with theirs (probe accuracy $\ge 0.91$ in middle-to-late layers across all five tested models). We complement their probing analysis with activation patching, which reveals that while personality information \emph{grows stronger} through depth, \emph{causal influence} on output is concentrated in the earliest attention layers.

\paragraph{Mechanistic Interpretability.}
From Traits to Circuits \citep{bao2025traits2circuits} proposes identifying ``personality circuits'' as minimal subgraphs responsible for trait expression. While linear probing finds personality directions effective \citep{konen2025probing}, broad empirical evaluations highlight fragility in OOD transfer and collateral capability degradation during concept activation steering \citep{cae2025, cae2025b}. To improve reliability, recent techniques denoise concept vectors using SAEs \citep{sae2025denoising}, and dissociations between magnitude and directional steering \citep{direction2026} provide insights for continuous behavioral modulation.

\section{Perplexity Calculation Methodology}\label{app:ppl}
\begin{equation}
\text{PPL} = \exp\left(-\frac{1}{T}\sum_{t=1}^{T} \log p_\theta(w_t | w_{<t})\right)
\end{equation}
where $p_\theta$ is the unmodified (un-steered) model's probability distribution. We compute perplexity on the \emph{full sequence} (prompt + generated response), using the base model without steering hooks active. The threshold of PPL $< 10$ was selected empirically: manual inspection revealed that outputs with PPL $> 10$ consistently exhibited token-level repetition loops, grammatical breakdown, or semantic incoherence. We acknowledge that absolute PPL values are not directly comparable across architectures due to different tokenizers and vocabulary sizes; the threshold serves as a within-model fluency gate rather than a cross-model metric.
