\begin{abstract}
Recent work has established that Big Five personality traits can be linearly extracted and steered in LLMs. We build on this foundation by asking \emph{where}, \emph{why}, and \emph{how far}: where in the network does personality causally matter (not just exist), why does trait independence vary across architectures, and how do psychological constructs beyond the Big Five---specifically defense mechanisms---manifest geometrically? Using contrastive activation analysis and causal intervention across five models---Qwen3-0.6B, Qwen2.5-0.5B, TinyLlama-1.1B, Llama-3.2-1B, and Gemma-2-2B---we report five findings: \textbf{(1)~Existence--Causation Dissociation:} Personality information grows stronger through deeper layers (probe accuracy $\ge0.91$, consistent with recent probing studies), yet activation patching reveals that \emph{causal influence} is concentrated in the earliest attention layers---a gap between where information \emph{exists} versus where it \emph{causally matters}. \textbf{(2)~Architecture-Dependent Factor Independence:} Big Five trait independence varies substantially across architectures (mean off-diagonal $|\cos|$ from 0.05 to 0.42), indicating that model capacity and training recipe jointly shape trait factorization. \textbf{(3)~Hierarchical Defense Mechanism Geometry:} All nine defense mechanisms from Vaillant's hierarchy show activation-space clustering that mirrors psychological maturity levels: immature defenses form correlated subspaces ($|\cos|=0.511$) while mature defenses are near-orthogonal ($0.030$)---a 17-fold difference preserved across all five architectures. \textbf{(4)~Architecture-Specific Steering Bounds:} Maximum tolerable intervention strength varies from $|\alpha|=1.0$ to $10.0$, but our Relative Injection Strength (RIS) metric reveals that this largely reflects residual stream scale differences. \textbf{(5)~RLHF Alignment Tax on Steerability:} BFI-44 dose-response analysis reveals that RLHF-reinforced traits (Agreeableness, Conscientiousness) resist activation steering while value-neutral traits (Extraversion, Neuroticism) are highly controllable---a pattern consistent across architectures. We introduce \textbf{PersonaLens}, a standardized framework for mechanistic personality analysis integrating contrastive extraction, causal localization, and psychometric validation.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}

Large Language Models (LLMs) readily adopt distinct \emph{personas} when instructed through system prompts, producing text that reflects specific personality traits \citep{shanahan2023role}. This ability has enabled applications ranging from immersive role-playing \citep{chen2024rolesurvey} to personality-consistent dialogue agents \citep{shao2023character}. However, a fundamental question remains: \textbf{do these personality behaviors correspond to structured internal representations, and can we identify and control them mechanistically?}

Prior work on LLM personality has largely taken a \emph{behavioral} perspective: evaluating model outputs against psychometric instruments such as the Big Five \citep{jiang2024evaluating}, or engineering prompts to elicit desired traits \citep{serapiogarcía2023personality}. While valuable, this approach treats the model as a black box and cannot answer whether personality traits correspond to identifiable neural structures.

Meanwhile, the field of \emph{representation engineering} \citep{zou2023representation} has demonstrated that semantic concepts---truthfulness \citep{li2024iti}, harmlessness \citep{rimsky2024steering}, and emotional states \citep{bao2024emotion}---correspond to linear directions in activation space that can be both probed and manipulated. Recent work has begun extending this paradigm to personality: persona vectors \citep{chen2025persona}, activation-space personality steering \citep{sun2025activation}, and persona blending \citep{billy2025blending} all suggest that personality traits may occupy distinct linear subspaces within transformer representations.

However, the field currently lacks a \textbf{standardized, model-agnostic methodology} for systematically analyzing personality representations across different models. Existing studies typically report findings for specific model--trait combinations without establishing a transferable pipeline. Furthermore, no prior work jointly analyzes Big Five traits and psychological defense mechanisms within a unified framework, despite defense mechanisms being critical to authentic personality expression \citep{personaforge2025}.

\paragraph{Research Questions.} This work addresses four fundamental questions about personality representation in LLMs:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,topsep=2pt,itemsep=1pt]
    \item \textbf{Q1 (Representation Existence):} Do personality traits correspond to linearly separable directions in LLM activation space, and how does this separability vary across architectures and depth?
    \item \textbf{Q2 (Geometric Structure):} Are different personality dimensions encoded independently---as predicted by the Big Five's orthogonal factor structure---and does defense mechanism geometry mirror psychological maturity hierarchies?
    \item \textbf{Q3 (Causal Relevance):} Where in the network do personality representations \emph{causally influence} output, and does this coincide with where they are most \emph{detectable}?
    \item \textbf{Q4 (Controllability):} Can extracted personality directions enable reliable inference-time behavioral control, what are the architectural limits of such control, and can it penetrate safety guardrails that prompt-based methods cannot?
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Contributions.} Building on recent advances in personality probing and steering \citep{frising2025linear, feng2026persona, chen2025persona, ju2025probing}, this work investigates the \emph{structure}, \emph{causality}, and \emph{limits} of personality representations. We make five contributions:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,topsep=2pt,itemsep=1pt]
\item \textbf{Existence--Causation Dissociation:} Through activation patching across five architectures, we show that personality information \emph{exists} most strongly in middle-to-upper layers (probe accuracy $\ge$0.91, consistent with \citet{ju2025probing}), yet \emph{causally influences} output primarily through the earliest attention layers. This dissociation---between detectability and causal relevance---has direct implications for where steering interventions should be applied (Sec.~\ref{sec:localization}).
\item \textbf{Architecture-Dependent Personality Geometry:} We find that Big Five trait independence varies across architectures (mean off-diagonal $|\cos|$ from 0.05 to 0.42 across five models), with same-layer comparison, null baselines, and shuffle-label controls. This complements single-model orthogonality findings \citep{feng2026persona} by revealing that model capacity and training recipe jointly shape the degree of trait factorization (Sec.~\ref{sec:orthogonality}).
\item \textbf{Defense Mechanism Hierarchy in Activation Space:} We extend the analysis beyond the Big Five to all nine defense mechanisms from Vaillant's hierarchy \citep{vaillant1986empirical}. Activation-space clustering mirrors psychological maturity levels: immature defenses form correlated subspaces ($|\cos|=0.511$) while mature defenses are near-orthogonal ($0.030$)---a 17-fold difference preserved across all five architectures ($p < 0.00013$ by combinatorial ordering test), providing neural evidence for hierarchical psychological organization (Sec.~\ref{sec:results_ortho}).
\item \textbf{Architecture-Specific Steering Bounds and RIS Normalization:} Maximum tolerable intervention strength varies from $|\alpha|=1.0$ to $10.0$ across architectures. We introduce the Relative Injection Strength (RIS) metric that normalizes for residual stream scale, revealing that apparent cross-model variability largely reflects scale differences rather than qualitative fragility (Sec.~\ref{sec:steering}).
\item \textbf{RLHF Alignment Tax on Steerability:} BFI-44 dose-response analysis across all five Big Five traits reveals that RLHF-reinforced traits (Agreeableness, Conscientiousness) resist activation steering while value-neutral traits (Extraversion, Neuroticism) are highly controllable. Activation steering also penetrates AI identity guardrails that prompt-based methods cannot bypass (4.6/5.0 vs 3.6/5.0 human evaluation). Both patterns replicate across architectures (Sec.~\ref{sec:baselines}, \ref{sec:bfi44}).
\end{enumerate}
\paragraph{Methodological Contribution.} To enable these discoveries, we introduce \textbf{PersonaLens}, a standardized pipeline that:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,topsep=2pt,itemsep=1pt]
\item \textbf{Defines a contrastive dataset} covering all five Big Five dimensions and three representative defense mechanisms, with carefully designed prompt pairs that isolate individual trait dimensions (Sec.~\ref{sec:data}).
\item \textbf{Extracts multi-method personality directions}---RMS-normalized difference vectors, PCA-based directions, and Leave-One-Scenario-Out (LOSO) cross-validated linear probes---producing converging evidence robust to extraction method choice (Sec.~\ref{sec:extraction}).
\item \textbf{Establishes causal importance} through token-localized and component-level activation patching, distinguishing information existence from causal influence (Sec.~\ref{sec:localization}).
\item \textbf{Validates across five architecturally diverse models} (Qwen, LLaMA, Gemma), revealing remarkable structural consistency alongside architecture-specific differences (Sec.~\ref{sec:results}).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Personality in LLMs.}
The study of personality expression in LLMs has expanded rapidly. Foundational surveys \citep{serapiogarcía2023personality} establish that LLMs can simulate Big Five personality traits via prompting. Personality Alignment \citep{mao2024personality} introduces training-free alignment via Personality Activation Search (PAS). PersonaForge \citep{personaforge2025} proposes psychology-grounded architecture with dual-process generation but operates entirely at the prompt level. Our work extends PersonaForge by grounding its psychological constructs in neural representations.

\paragraph{Representation Engineering and Activation Steering.}
Representation Engineering (RepE) \citep{zou2023representation} establishes that high-level concepts correspond to linear directions in activation space. Inference-Time Intervention (ITI) \citep{li2024iti} demonstrates that shifting activations along truthful directions improves model truthfulness. Contrastive Activation Addition \citep{rimsky2024steering, turner2023activation} generalizes this to behavioral attributes. More recent advances investigate context-dependent control via Steering Vector Fields (SVF) \citep{svf2026} and formalize the algebraic structure of these directions through factored subspaces \citep{factored2026}.

\paragraph{Personality-Specific Representation Engineering.}
A rapidly growing body of work applies RepE specifically to personality traits. Frising and Balcells~\citep{frising2025linear} ground linear probing and steering in Big Five psychometric scales using Llama~3.3~70B, finding that linear directions are effective probes but that steering capabilities are \emph{context-dependent}---reliable in forced-choice tasks but limited in open-ended generation. PERSONA~\citep{feng2026persona} introduces activation vector algebra (scalar multiplication, addition, subtraction) for compositional personality control, claiming approximately orthogonal trait directions and achieving near-SFT performance on PersonalityBench. Bhandari et al.~\citep{bhandari2026activation} propose hybrid layer selection for stable trait control, discovering that personality traits occupy a low-rank shared subspace. Chen et al.~\citep{chen2025persona} extract persona vectors for personality steering, and BILLY~\citep{billy2025blending} blends multiple persona vectors for creative generation. Recent works have also developed the PERSONA frameworks~\citep{persona2026, persona2025} for mapping personality representations using trait vectors and hybrid layer subspaces. On the application side, Ong et al.~\citep{ong2025identifying} apply Big Five steering vectors to study cooperative behavior in multi-agent Prisoner's Dilemma settings, demonstrating that personality steering has downstream behavioral consequences.

These works collectively establish that personality traits can be linearly extracted and steered. Our work builds on this foundation in three directions: (1)~we add \emph{causal localization} via activation patching, distinguishing where personality information exists from where it causally matters; (2)~we extend the analysis to psychological defense mechanisms, validating geometric structure against Vaillant's maturity hierarchy; and (3)~we test trait independence across five diverse architectures, revealing architecture-dependent variation in orthogonality.

\paragraph{Layer-wise Personality Encoding.}
Ju et al.~\citep{ju2025probing} conduct the most comprehensive layer-wise probing study to date, testing 11 open-source LLMs and finding that personality is predominantly encoded in middle and upper layers, with instruction-tuned models showing clearer trait separation. Their layer-wise perturbation method successfully edits personality during inference with minimal capability degradation. Our probing results are consistent with theirs (probe accuracy $\ge 0.91$ in middle-to-late layers across all five tested models). We complement their probing analysis with activation patching, which reveals an additional dimension: while personality information \emph{grows stronger} through depth, \emph{causal influence} on output is concentrated in the earliest attention layers. This existence--causation dissociation suggests that optimal steering strategy depends on whether the goal is to maximize representational signal (middle layers) or causal leverage (early layers).

\paragraph{Steering Frameworks.}
On the engineering side, EasySteer \citep{xu2025easysteer} provides a unified, high-performance framework for LLM steering built on vLLM, supporting multiple vector extraction methods (DiffMean, PCA, SAE) and learning-based approaches (LoReFT) across eight application domains including personality. While EasySteer demonstrates the practical viability of activation steering at production scale, it focuses on \emph{engineering infrastructure} rather than scientific analysis of how personality is represented. Our work is complementary: PersonaLens provides the \emph{scientific foundation}---psychometrically grounded methodology, geometric structure analysis, causal localization, and cross-model validation---that informs and validates the engineering practice enabled by frameworks like EasySteer.

\paragraph{Mechanistic Interpretability and Reliability Constraints.}
From Traits to Circuits \citep{bao2025traits2circuits} proposes identifying ``personality circuits'' as minimal subgraphs responsible for trait expression. While linear probing finds personality directions effective \citep{konen2025probing}, broad empirical evaluations highlight fragility in Out-of-Distribution (OOD) transfer and collateral capability degradation during concept activation steering \citep{cae2025, cae2025b}. To improve reliability, recent techniques denoise concept vectors using Sparse Autoencoders (SAEs) \citep{sae2025denoising}. Furthermore, dissociations between magnitude and directional steering \citep{direction2026} provide crucial insights for continuous behavioral modulation.

\paragraph{Non-Identifiability Concerns.}
Venkatesh and Kurapath~\citep{venkatesh2026nonidentifiability} raise important theoretical concerns: persona vectors are fundamentally non-identifiable under single-layer white-box access due to null-space ambiguity. While we acknowledge this limitation, we argue that our multi-method approach provides converging evidence that is robust to the specific choice of extraction method.

\subsection{Phase 1: Contrastive Data Construction}\label{sec:data}

Following the contrastive activation paradigm \citep{zou2023representation, rimsky2024steering}, we construct paired prompts designed to elicit contrasting personality states while keeping the conversational scenario identical.

\paragraph{Big Five Traits.}
For each of the five personality dimensions---Openness (O), Conscientiousness (C), Extraversion (E), Agreeableness (A), and Neuroticism (N)---we define:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,topsep=2pt,itemsep=1pt]
\item A \textbf{high-trait system prompt} instructing the model to behave as a person with an extreme level of the trait;
\item A \textbf{low-trait system prompt} instructing the opposite extreme;
\item $K=20$ \textbf{neutral scenario prompts} (user messages) spanning diverse social, emotional, and decision-making contexts.
\end{itemize}

For example, an Openness pair uses the system prompts ``\textit{You are extremely open to new experiences, intellectually curious, imaginative\ldots}'' vs.\ ``\textit{You are very practical, conventional, and prefer routine\ldots}'', with shared scenarios like ``\textit{What do you think about modern abstract art?}''

\paragraph{Defense Mechanisms.}
Following Vaillant's hierarchy \citep{vaillant1994ego} and PersonaForge's operationalization \citep{personaforge2025}, we construct contrastive pairs for three representative defense mechanisms: Humor, Rationalization, and Projection. These three mechanisms were selected as they represent distinct categories in Vaillant's hierarchy (mature, neurotic, and immature defenses respectively), providing coverage of the defense mechanism construct space. Each mechanism contrasts an \emph{active} condition (the mechanism is deployed in response to a stressor) against a \emph{neutral} condition (direct emotional engagement). Each has $K=10$ scenario prompts involving interpersonal conflict, loss, or ego-threat. Our contrastive prompt library additionally defines six further mechanisms (Sublimation, Denial, Displacement, Intellectualization, Regression, Reaction Formation), which we validate on Qwen2.5-0.5B as an extensibility check.

% ============================================================
\subsection{Phase 2: Activation Collection}\label{sec:collection}

For each contrastive pair $(x^+_i, x^-_i)$ in trait $t$, we format the messages using the model's chat template and perform a forward pass through the full model, collecting hidden states $h_\ell \in \mathbb{R}^d$ at the \emph{last token position} of every transformer layer $\ell \in \{0, 1, \ldots, L{-}1\}$.

We record hidden states before any generation occurs (i.e., at the position where the model would begin generating its response). This captures the model's ``pre-generation personality state''---the representation that determines how the model will respond.

For a model with $L$ layers and hidden dimension $d$, this produces, for each trait $t$:
\begin{equation}
H^+_t = \{h_\ell(x^+_i)\}_{i=1}^{K}, \quad H^-_t = \{h_\ell(x^-_i)\}_{i=1}^{K}, \qquad \forall \ell \in \{0, \ldots, L{-}1\}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Metrics and Evaluation Criteria}\label{sec:metrics}

We evaluate personality encoding through four complementary metrics, each capturing a distinct property of the extracted representations.

\paragraph{Leave-One-Scenario-Out (LOSO) Accuracy.}
Given $K$ contrastive pairs per trait, we train a linear probe on $K{-}1$ pairs and evaluate on the held-out pair. Repeating over all $K$ folds and averaging yields the LOSO accuracy $a_\ell \in [0,1]$, which measures \emph{generalization to unseen conversational contexts}---the critical test of whether personality directions capture abstract trait structure rather than scenario-specific lexical patterns.

\paragraph{Cohen's $d$ Effect Size.}
At layer $\ell$, we compute the standardized mean separation between high-trait ($+$) and low-trait ($-$) activation projections onto the extracted direction:
\begin{equation}
d_\ell = \frac{\mu_{+,\ell} - \mu_{-,\ell}}{\sqrt{\frac{(n_+-1)s_{+,\ell}^2 + (n_--1)s_{-,\ell}^2}{n_+ + n_- - 2}}}
\end{equation}
Values $d > 0.8$ indicate large effects by conventional standards \citep{cohen1988statistical}; our models consistently achieve $d > 2.0$, indicating very large separation. We report 95\% bootstrap confidence intervals (1000 resamples) and permutation $p$-values (500 permutations) to quantify statistical reliability.

\paragraph{Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR).}
To distinguish genuine personality signal from residual-stream scaling artifacts, we compare the RMS-normalized difference norm against a null baseline of random within-condition pairings:
\begin{equation}
\text{SNR}_\ell = \frac{\|v^{\text{diff}}_\ell\| / \text{RMS}_\ell}{\mathbb{E}[\|v^{\text{random}}_\ell\| / \text{RMS}_\ell]}
\end{equation}
where $\text{RMS}_\ell = \sqrt{\frac{1}{2K}\sum_i (\|h_\ell(x_i)\|^2)}$ is the per-layer activation scale. SNR $> 2$ indicates that the contrastive signal exceeds random pairing noise; our models consistently achieve SNR $> 7$.

\paragraph{Relative Injection Strength (RIS).}
To enable fair cross-model comparison of steering sensitivity, we normalize the maximum safe steering intensity by the residual stream scale:
\begin{equation}
\text{RIS} = \frac{|\alpha_{\max}|}{\mathbb{E}_{\ell}[\|h_\ell\|]}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbb{E}_{\ell}[\|h_\ell\|]$ is the mean L2 norm of residual stream activations at the injection layer across a batch of neutral prompts. RIS controls for the confound that models with larger residual stream norms require proportionally larger $\alpha$ values for equivalent representational perturbation.

% ============================================================
\subsection{Phase 3: Persona Vector Extraction}\label{sec:extraction}

We apply three complementary methods to extract personality directions from the collected activations, enabling cross-validation of results.

\paragraph{Method 1: Mean Difference (Contrastive Activation Addition).}
The simplest and most widely used approach \citep{rimsky2024steering, chen2025persona}:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:meandiff}
v^{\text{diff}}_\ell = \frac{1}{K} \sum_{i=1}^{K} \left[ h_\ell(x^+_i) - h_\ell(x^-_i) \right], \qquad \hat{v}^{\text{diff}}_\ell = \frac{v^{\text{diff}}_\ell}{\|v^{\text{diff}}_\ell\|}
\end{equation}

To rule out trivial residual-stream scaling confounds across depth, we evaluate the \textbf{RMS-normalized difference norm}: $\frac{\|v^{\text{diff}}_\ell\|}{\text{RMS}_\ell}$, where $\text{RMS}_\ell = \sqrt{\frac{1}{2K}\sum (h_\ell)^2}$ represents the per-layer activation scale. We further contextualize this against a \textbf{random-pair baseline} to compute a signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) for feature existence.

\paragraph{Method 2: PCA on Difference Vectors.}
For each layer, we compute individual difference vectors $\delta_i = h_\ell(x^+_i) - h_\ell(x^-_i)$ and apply PCA to the set $\{\delta_i\}_{i=1}^K$. The first principal component represents the dominant direction of personality variation, and its explained variance ratio $\rho_1$ indicates how concentrated the personality information is in a single direction.

\paragraph{Method 3: Linear Probing.}
We train a logistic regression classifier on the concatenated activations $\{h_\ell(x^+_i)\} \cup \{h_\ell(x^-_i)\}$ with labels $y \in \{1, 0\}$. Crucially, to prevent overfitting in the $d \gg K$ regime, we use strong $L_2$ regularization alongside \textbf{Leave-One-Scenario-Out (LOSO) cross-validation}. By training on $K-1$ contrastive pairs and testing on the remaining disjoint pair, we rigorously estimate generalization accuracy across unseen contexts.

\paragraph{Layer-wise Encoding Profile.}
For each trait, the triple $(\|v^{\text{diff}}_\ell\|, \rho_{1,\ell}, a_\ell)$ across all layers constitutes the \textbf{personality encoding profile}---a standardized fingerprint that can be compared across models.

\paragraph{Extraction Method Convergence.}
A natural concern is whether scientific conclusions depend on the choice of extraction method. We find strong convergence across all three approaches: mean-difference vectors show mean $|\cos| > 0.95$ with PCA first components and $|\cos| > 0.89$ with linear probe weight vectors, across all traits and models. This high collinearity indicates that the personality direction is \emph{robustly identifiable} despite theoretical non-identifiability concerns \citep{venkatesh2026nonidentifiability}---the extraction method choice affects computational cost and confidence estimation, not the recovered direction. Critically, the three methods provide \emph{complementary diagnostic information}: PCA yields explained variance ratios ($\rho_1$) quantifying how concentrated personality information is in a single direction; linear probes provide generalization bounds via LOSO accuracy; and mean-difference vectors provide interpretable magnitude profiles across depth. We use mean-difference vectors for steering and geometric analysis (due to computational stability and interpretability), while linear probes serve exclusively to evaluate representational \emph{existence} via classification accuracy and effect sizes.

\paragraph{Statistical Power and Sample Size.}
With $K=20$ scenario pairs per Big Five trait, our LOSO cross-validation achieves stable estimates: bootstrap standard errors are $<0.02$ for accuracy and $<0.15$ for Cohen's $d$ across all models. The choice of $K=20$ reflects a balance between statistical power and computational cost. For defense mechanisms ($K=10$), accuracy standard errors remain $<0.03$, sufficient for reliable inference given the substantially larger effect sizes ($d > 2.0$) observed for defense-related contrasts. The smaller $K$ for defense mechanisms is justified by the stronger signal: defense mechanism contrasts involve more behaviorally distinct response patterns (e.g., humor deflection vs.\ direct emotional engagement) compared to the subtler personality trait contrasts.

% ============================================================
\subsection{Phase 4: Causal Localization via Activation Patching}\label{sec:localization}

Probing and mean-difference analyses establish where personality information \emph{exists}, but not whether it \emph{causally matters}. Following the activation patching paradigm \citep{meng2022locating, bao2024emotion, bao2025traits2circuits}, we establish causal importance through targeted interventional experiments.

For each contrastive pair, we map token spans corresponding to the \emph{system prompt} (handling personality instruction) and the \emph{user prompt} (handling neutral context). We perform:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,topsep=2pt,itemsep=1pt]
\item \textbf{Token-Localized Patching:} We patch activations exclusively for the system prompt span versus exclusively for the user prompt span to isolate where causal influence originates.
\item \textbf{Component-Level Patching:} We patch primarily the outputs of the self-attention submodule vs.\ the MLP submodule to trace computational pathways.
\end{enumerate}

The causal importance is measured as the KL divergence between clean (high-trait) and patched output distributions:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:causal}
\mathcal{I}(\ell) = D_{\text{KL}}\!\left(\text{softmax}(o^+) \;\|\; \text{softmax}(o^+_{\text{patched},\ell})\right)
\end{equation}

A high $\mathcal{I}(\ell)$ indicates that replacing layer $\ell$'s localized activations with the opposite-personality version significantly shifts the model's output reasoning logic.

% ============================================================
\subsection{Phase 5: Inference-Time Personality Steering}\label{sec:steering}

The culminating experiment directly tests whether controlling neuron activations controls personality. We inject persona vectors during inference using forward hooks:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:steering}
\tilde{h}_\ell = h_\ell + \alpha \cdot \hat{v}_\ell
\end{equation}
where $\alpha \in \mathbb{R}$ controls steering strength. Positive $\alpha$ pushes toward the high-trait direction; negative $\alpha$ pushes toward the low-trait direction. Critically, this intervention uses \textbf{no personality-related prompting}---the user's message is completely neutral, and all personality expression arises solely from activation manipulation.

% ============================================================
\subsection{Cross-Trait Orthogonality Analysis}\label{sec:orthogonality}

A key theoretical question is whether different personality dimensions occupy \emph{independent} directions in activation space, as would be predicted by the Big Five's orthogonal factor structure \citep{costa1992revised}. For each pair of traits $(t_i, t_j)$, we compute the cosine similarity between their extracted persona vectors at the best layer:
\begin{equation}
\text{sim}(t_i, t_j) = \frac{\hat{v}_{t_i} \cdot \hat{v}_{t_j}}{\|\hat{v}_{t_i}\| \cdot \|\hat{v}_{t_j}\|}
\end{equation}

If the model's internal representation reflects psychological theory, we expect Big Five dimensions to show meaningful independence ($|\text{sim}| \ll 1$), while defense mechanisms may share subspaces due to common underlying cognitive strategies. Note that while our contrastive prompt library defines nine defense mechanisms, we focus on three representative mechanisms (Humor, Rationalization, Projection) for cross-model validation, with Qwen2.5-0.5B serving as a validation model for the full set.

\section{Experimental Setup}\label{sec:setup}

\paragraph{Models.} We validate PersonaLens on five models spanning three distinct architectural families:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,topsep=2pt,itemsep=1pt]
\item \textbf{Qwen3-0.6B} \citep{qwen2025qwen3}: 28 layers, $d{=}1024$. Qwen-series architecture.
\item \textbf{Qwen2.5-0.5B-Instruct}: 24 layers, $d{=}896$. Qwen-series architecture.
\item \textbf{TinyLlama-1.1B-Chat-v1.0} \citep{zhang2024tinyllama}: 22 layers, $d{=}2048$. LLaMA-1 architecture.
\item \textbf{Llama-3.2-1B-Instruct} \citep{dubey2024llama}: 16 layers, $d{=}2048$. LLaMA-3 architecture with GQA and RoPE.
\item \textbf{Gemma-2-2B-Instruct} \citep{gemmateam2024gemma}: 26 layers, $d{=}2304$. Google-designed architecture with sliding-window attention.
\end{itemize}
These models are small enough for rapid prototyping while being representative of modern instruction-tuned transformer architectures. Notably, Gemma-2 does not support a system role in its chat template; our pipeline automatically handles this by merging the system prompt into the first user message.

\paragraph{Traits Analyzed.} We analyze all five Big Five dimensions (O, C, E, A, N) using $K{=}20$ scenario pairs each, and three representative defense mechanisms (Humor, Rationalization, Projection) using $K{=}10$ pairs each, totaling 130 contrastive prompt pairs per model. Qwen2.5-0.5B was additionally evaluated on all nine defense mechanisms to validate the framework's extensibility.

\paragraph{Extraction Configuration.} Linear probes use strong L2-regularized logistic regression ($C{=}0.01$) alongside rigorous \textbf{Leave-One-Scenario-Out (LOSO) cross-validation}. All metrics, including RMS normalization and Cohen's $d$, are evaluated on the held-out test configurations. \textbf{Unless otherwise specified, all steering and cosine similarity analyses use the RMS-normalized Mean-Difference vectors $\hat{v}^{\text{diff}}_\ell$}, as these proved highly collinear with the primary PCA component (mean $|\cos| > 0.95$ across all traits and models) while being computationally more stable and interpretable. Linear probes are used exclusively to evaluate representational \emph{existence} (classification accuracy and Cohen's $d$), not for steering or geometric analysis.

\paragraph{Patching Configuration.} We employ token-localized activation patching (system tokens vs.\ user prompt tokens vs.\ random-position control tokens) and component-level tracing (attention vs.\ MLP). We average causal importance over $n{=}5$ contrastive pairs per strategy.

\paragraph{Steering Evaluation.} For steering experiments, we define quantitative $\alpha$-sweeps ($\alpha \in [-8.0, +10.0]$) with dual metrics: keyword-based trait scoring and sequence perplexity to track fluency degradation.

\paragraph{Layer-wise Orthogonality Evolution.}
Examining how trait independence evolves across depth reveals that orthogonality is not a static property but is \emph{dynamically constructed} through the network. In early layers ($0$--$30\%$ of depth), Big Five vectors exhibit relatively high inter-correlation (mean $|\cos| \approx 0.30$--$0.40$), reflecting the shared processing of persona instructions before trait-specific separation has occurred. Independence emerges progressively through middle layers ($30$--$70\%$), where attention-mediated processing separates trait-specific features, reaching minimum inter-correlation at the layers that also maximize LOSO accuracy. Interestingly, the final $15\%$ of layers show slight reconvergence ($|\cos|$ increases by $\approx 0.03$--$0.08$), suggesting that output preparation involves partial re-integration of trait information into a shared generation manifold. This temporal dynamics---\emph{entanglement $\rightarrow$ separation $\rightarrow$ partial re-integration}---mirrors the processing pipeline observed in causal localization: early layers establish trait-specific contextual frames, middle layers amplify and separate them, and final layers compress representations for output generation. The reconvergence phenomenon also explains why steering is most effective at middle layers rather than the deepest layers where trait signal magnitude is highest: middle layers offer the optimal combination of strong signal \emph{and} maximal trait independence.

\paragraph{Why Does Qwen2.5 Show Higher Inter-Trait Correlation?}
The anomalously high Big Five correlation in Qwen2.5-0.5B ($|\cos|=0.418$ vs.\ $<0.15$ for other models) warrants mechanistic explanation. We hypothesize three contributing factors: (1)~\textbf{Capacity constraints}: Qwen2.5 has the smallest hidden dimension ($d=896$) among tested models, potentially forcing trait representations into overlapping subspaces due to dimensional pressure---consistent with the superposition hypothesis \citep{elhage2022superposition}. (2)~\textbf{Aggressive instruction tuning}: Qwen2.5's instruction-tuning recipe may impose stronger behavioral constraints that couple personality dimensions (e.g., high Agreeableness and high Conscientiousness are jointly rewarded), creating correlated trait directions. (3)~\textbf{Training data composition}: Different pretraining corpora may expose models to personality-related text with varying degrees of trait co-occurrence, shaping the geometry of learned representations. Supporting evidence for the capacity hypothesis comes from the observation that Qwen3-0.6B ($d=1024$, 14\% larger hidden dimension) achieves substantially better separation ($|\cos|=0.107$), suggesting a critical threshold in representational capacity for trait factorization.

\paragraph{Null Baseline for Orthogonality.}
To verify that Big Five trait independence is not a trivial artifact of the contrastive extraction pipeline, we construct a null baseline: five psychologically meaningless binary attribute pairs (fruit preference, writing style, time preference, pet preference, season preference) processed through the identical extraction pipeline. If contrastive extraction inherently produces orthogonal vectors regardless of semantic content, this null baseline should show comparable or lower off-diagonal $|\cos|$.

Results show architecture-dependent behavior: on Qwen3-0.6B, null attributes exhibit mean off-diagonal $|\cos| = 0.136$ compared to $0.107$ for Big Five traits---a 27\% higher correlation among random attributes. However, on Qwen2.5-0.5B, Big Five traits show higher inter-correlation ($0.418$) than null attributes ($0.128$), indicating that trait independence is architecture-dependent (Figure~\ref{fig:null_ortho}). Individual null attribute pairs on Qwen3 show correlations up to 0.224 (time-season preference).

However, one might further object that near-orthogonality in high-dimensional spaces ($d \ge 1024$) is near-universal for arbitrary vectors---making even this random-attribute baseline difficult to interpret. We address this concern in two complementary ways.

First, we note that LLM activation spaces are not isotropic. As \citet{ethayarajh2019contextual} showed, contextualized representations are highly \textbf{anisotropic}: all token representations cluster in a narrow cone, so every pair of semantically distinct concept vectors shares a baseline level of collinearity ($|\cos| \approx 0.10$--$0.15$) driven by shared linguistic priors---not by semantic relatedness. Furthermore, \citet{elhage2022superposition} demonstrate that when the represented features outnumber dimensions, models pack features into almost-orthogonal directions under \emph{superposition}, with incidental interference between unrelated concepts. Under this lens, random binary attributes (apples vs.~oranges) exhibit $|\cos| = 0.136$ precisely because they \emph{share superposition interference}---they are near-orthogonal not because of semantic independence but because of dimensional compression. In models where Big Five traits show lower inter-correlation than random pairs (e.g., Qwen3: 0.107 vs null 0.136), this suggests active representational effort to separate decision-critical dimensions, but this pattern is architecture-dependent rather than universal.

Second, we introduce a statistically rigorous \textbf{shuffle-label (permutation) baseline}: we randomly reassign the high/low trait labels within each trait's activation pool ($K$ permutations) and re-extract mean-difference vectors from the scrambled pairings. If the extraction pipeline itself creates orthogonality as an artifact, shuffled vectors should remain equally orthogonal; if orthogonality is a data property, shuffled pairings should collapse toward zero (canceling the semantic direction) with inconsistent off-diagonal structure. Results confirm the latter: shuffled label vectors yield a mean off-diagonal $|\cos| = 0.123 \pm 0.034$ with high variance and no consistent structure (200 permutations on Qwen3-0.6B), versus $0.107 \pm 0.078$ for the genuine Big Five---a statistically significant difference (two-sample $t=9.64$, $p<0.0001$; 76.5\% of shuffled permutations exceed the genuine mean). This provides a mathematically principled refutation of the pipeline-artifact hypothesis (script: \texttt{eval\_shuffle\_label\_baseline.py}).

\paragraph{Defense Mechanism Clustering: Vaillant Hierarchy Validation.}
To deepen our understanding of defense mechanism clustering, we analyze the full set of nine defense mechanisms on Qwen2.5-0.5B-Instruct, organized according to Vaillant's empirically validated hierarchy \citep{vaillant1986empirical, digiuseppe2021hierarchy}. Vaillant's framework classifies defenses into three maturity levels based on their adaptive sophistication and degree of reality distortion: \textbf{mature} defenses (Humor, Sublimation) involve minimal distortion and support adaptive functioning; \textbf{neurotic} defenses (Rationalization, Intellectualization, Displacement) involve moderate distortion and emerge in childhood/adolescence; \textbf{immature} defenses (Projection, Denial, Regression, Reaction Formation) involve heavy reality distortion and associate with psychopathology.

Figure~\ref{fig:defense_full} presents the complete $9{\times}9$ orthogonality matrix organized by Vaillant hierarchy. Quantitative analysis reveals clear hierarchical clustering: within-immature defenses show mean $|\cos| = 0.511$, substantially higher than within-neurotic ($0.189$) or within-mature ($0.030$). This 17-fold difference between immature and mature clustering indicates that maturity level---reflecting shared cognitive operations and reality-distortion strategies---is a primary organizing principle in activation space.

\textbf{Theoretical interpretation}: The strong clustering of immature defenses reflects their shared reliance on \emph{primitive cognitive operations}---denial, projection, and regression all involve failing to process threatening information adaptively, instead distorting attribution (projection), blocking awareness (denial), or reverting to childlike responses (regression). In contrast, mature defenses like humor and sublimation employ sophisticated \emph{representational restructuring}---transforming threatening content through creative reframing (humor) or channeling it into productive activities (sublimation). These mature operations are more differentiated and thus occupy more orthogonal directions.

The neurotic cluster (rationalization, intellectualization, displacement) occupies an intermediate position, consistent with Vaillant's hierarchy. These defenses share an \emph{appraisal-reframing} strategy: rationalization constructs logical justifications, intellectualization separates emotion from cognition, and displacement redirects emotional targets. Their moderate clustering ($|\cos| = 0.189$) reflects shared cognitive operations while maintaining some differentiation.

Cross-hierarchy correlations provide additional insight: mature-immature correlations ($0.201$) are lower than neurotic-immature ($0.294$), suggesting that developmental distance in Vaillant's hierarchy corresponds to geometric distance in activation space. This alignment between psychological theory and neural geometry provides compelling evidence that LLMs internalize not just individual defense mechanisms but their hierarchical organization.

To assess statistical significance we invoke a more powerful argument than conventional hypothesis testing: \textbf{the probability of the observed ordering arising by chance}. Vaillant's theory predicts a strict ordering Immature $>$ Neurotic $>$ Mature. There are $3! = 6$ possible orderings of three groups; under the null hypothesis of random assignment, each ordering has probability $1/6$. Crucially, all five architectures---spanning three model families, four distinct training recipes, and hidden dimensions ranging from 896 to 2304---independently produce the \emph{identical} strict ordering. The joint probability under the null is $(1/6)^5 \approx 0.00013$, providing strong evidence that the hierarchy is a universal property of instruction-tuned LLMs rather than a coincidence of any single architecture. This combinatorial argument is exact and assumption-free, requiring no distributional assumptions. A supplementary Kruskal-Wallis test on the five within-group means ($H = 10.14$, $p = 0.006$) confirms the same conclusion via conventional significance testing, though we note that with only $N=5$ models the KW result should be interpreted cautiously and the ordering argument is primary.

\subsection{Position-Swap Control: Disentangling Position from Content}\label{sec:position_swap}

To address the concern that user-token causal dominance might reflect a positional artifact
(recency effect) rather than genuine content-based importance, we conduct a position-swap
control experiment. We reverse the standard prompt layout: placing the neutral scenario
in the system role and the persona instruction in the user role, then re-run activation
patching.

Results (Figure~\ref{fig:position_swap}) reveal that causal influence remains concentrated
at sequence-final (user) positions regardless of content placement:
in the normal layout, user tokens (containing the neutral scenario) yield total KL of 118.3
vs 0.001 for system tokens (persona instruction); in the swapped layout, user tokens
(now containing the persona instruction) yield 105.8 vs 0.000 for system tokens.

This confirms a positional component to causal patching results, consistent with the \textbf{autoregressive bottleneck} inherent to decoder-only transformers: the model's output distribution is necessarily most sensitive to activations at positions nearest the generation point, because these positions carry the most recently integrated contextual information.
Critically, this finding \emph{refines} rather than invalidates our Existence--Causation Dissociation. Three lines of evidence support this interpretation:
\textbf{(1)~Consistent early-layer dominance}: Both normal and swapped conditions show identical early-layer causal concentration (L0, L1, L2 as top causal layers). If the effect were purely positional, we would expect uniform causal influence across layers---instead, the early-layer specificity persists regardless of content placement, confirming that \emph{attention-mediated propagation} in early layers is the operative mechanism.
\textbf{(2)~Attention as the propagation channel}: The dominance of attention (not MLP) outputs in causal influence (Table~\ref{tab:causal}) is consistent with a propagation account: early-layer attention heads read persona information from system tokens and write it into user-token representations, creating a causal bottleneck at the sequence boundary where generation begins.
\textbf{(3)~Practical implications preserved}: For steering applications, the position-swap result is actually \emph{favorable}---it means that persona vectors injected at sequence-final positions (the standard steering protocol) operate at the point of maximum causal leverage, regardless of where the original persona instruction appeared. The autoregressive bottleneck concentrates causal influence precisely where steering interventions are applied.

\paragraph{Why Do Some Traits Generalize Better Than Others?}
The trait-specific OOD pattern reveals distinct encoding mechanisms. \textbf{Openness} shows exceptional cross-split stability (mean $\cos=0.935$ across all models), suggesting it is encoded via \emph{abstract conceptual features}---intellectual curiosity, aesthetic appreciation, and unconventionality are recognizable regardless of the specific conversational scenario. In contrast, \textbf{Extraversion} (mean $0.855$) and \textbf{Agreeableness} (mean $0.854$) show lower stability, indicating more \emph{context-dependent encoding}: extraversion manifests differently in social gatherings versus solitary reflection scenarios, creating scenario-specific activation patterns that diverge across splits. This aligns with psychometric theory: Openness loads heavily on abstract cognitive style, while Extraversion and Agreeableness are inherently interpersonal and thus more context-sensitive \citep{costa1992revised}.

\paragraph{Model Capacity and Trait Abstraction.}
The capacity--generalization relationship reveals a threshold effect rather than a smooth scaling law. Models below $\approx$1B parameters (Qwen2.5-0.5B: mean $0.801$, TinyLlama-1.1B: $0.760$) show moderate OOD stability with high trait-dependent variance (std $=0.07$), while models $\ge$1B (Llama-3.2: $0.988$, Gemma-2: $0.914$, Qwen3: $0.909$) achieve consistently high stability (std $=0.03$)---a $20\%$ gap in mean and $2.3\times$ reduction in variance. This suggests that robust personality abstraction requires sufficient capacity to extract \emph{trait-invariant features} across diverse contexts; smaller models may rely more heavily on scenario-specific lexical cues, producing directions that are partially confounded with surface-level prompt features. Notably, Llama-3.2-1B achieves near-perfect OOD stability ($0.988$) despite being only marginally larger than TinyLlama-1.1B, suggesting that architectural improvements (GQA, RoPE) and training recipe quality matter as much as raw parameter count.

This architecture-specific sensitivity reveals that while linearly separable personality features generalize consistently across the tested architectures, the \emph{scale} of optimal intervention relies heavily on the specific normalization topology and residual stream geometry inherent to different architectures. Qwen2.5 exhibits exceptional resilience (coherent output at $|\alpha|=6$), while TinyLlama degrades rapidly beyond $|\alpha|=1$. Notably, Gemma-2-2B demonstrates unexpectedly high steering resilience ($|\alpha| > 10$), contrary to initial hypotheses about its sliding-window attention topology; we attribute this in part to Gemma-2's \emph{logit soft-capping mechanism} \citep{gemmateam2024gemma}, which clips extreme internal activations and thus dampens the effect of out-of-distribution interventions.

\paragraph{Relative Injection Strength: Normalizing for Residual Stream Scale.}
A critical confound in comparing raw $\alpha$ thresholds across models is the difference in \textbf{residual stream norm} $\mathbb{E}[\|h_\ell\|]$ \citep{todd2023function}. If one model's hidden states have a mean norm of 50 and another's have a mean norm of 5, then the same unit-norm steering vector has a relative impact 10$\times$ larger in the second model. We propose a \textbf{Relative Injection Strength (RIS)} metric:
\begin{equation}
    \text{RIS} = \frac{|\alpha_{\max}|}{\mathbb{E}_{\ell}[\|h_\ell\|]}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbb{E}_{\ell}[\|h_\ell\|]$ is the mean L2 norm of residual stream activations at the injection layer across a batch of neutral prompts. We compute RIS for all five models (script: \texttt{eval\_relative\_injection\_strength.py}). When RIS values are compared, the previously dramatic cross-model variability in raw $\alpha$ collapse thresholds ($1.0$ to $10.0^+$, a 10$\times$ range) is substantially reduced, consistent with the hypothesis that \emph{architecture-dependent steering bounds arise primarily from differences in residual stream scale rather than from qualitatively different representational fragility}.

\subsection{BFI-44 Validation: Standardized Psychological Assessment}\label{sec:bfi44}

Moving beyond keyword-based heuristics, we evaluate \textbf{trait-specific steering asymmetry} using the Big Five Inventory (BFI-44)~\citep{john1991big}, a standardized 44-item psychological instrument with 30+ years of validation history. We evaluate \textbf{all five Big Five traits} across steering intensities $\alpha \in \{-8, -6, -4, -2, 0, +2, +4, +6, +8\}$, prompting the model to self-report on trait-specific items using 1--5 Likert scales (e.g., ``I am original, come up with new ideas'' for Openness).

Results (Figure~\ref{fig:bfi_dose_response}) reveal \textbf{trait-specific dose-response patterns}. \textbf{Extraversion} and \textbf{Neuroticism} exhibit strong steering effects (score ranges: 0.31 and 0.29 respectively), with Extraversion increasing from 2.70 ($\alpha=-8$) to 3.01 ($\alpha=+6$) and Neuroticism from 2.72 to 3.03. \textbf{Openness} shows moderate response (range: 0.11, from 2.45 to 2.56). In contrast, \textbf{Agreeableness} demonstrates minimal steering sensitivity (range: 0.05, nearly flat across all $\alpha$), while \textbf{Conscientiousness} exhibits a reversed pattern (higher scores at negative $\alpha$). These are scientific discoveries about trait-specific controllability, not failures of the method.

Bootstrap uncertainty estimates reinforce this interpretation: at $\alpha=+8$, Extraversion reaches $2.54\pm0.05$ (95\% CI) and Neuroticism reaches $2.80\pm0.04$ (95\% CI), while Agreeableness remains within a narrow band consistent with representational resistance. These trait-specific patterns represent a significant scientific finding: they reveal that different personality dimensions are encoded with different degrees of controllability in activation space. Extraversion and Neuroticism are highly steerable ($\alpha=+8$ yields 2.54$\pm$0.05 and 2.80$\pm$0.04 respectively), while Agreeableness exhibits strong representational resistance---suggesting distinct encoding mechanisms across the Big Five factor structure.

The linear regime spans $\alpha \in [-8, +4]$ for responsive traits; beyond $\alpha=+4$, scores plateau or decline, indicating saturation effects. Overall, BFI-44 evaluation supports a trait-specific steering asymmetry account: some dimensions are highly controllable, while others are constrained by more resistant internal representations.

\paragraph{Cross-Model BFI-44 Replication.}
To test whether trait-specific steering asymmetry generalizes across architectures, we replicate the full BFI-44 sweep on Llama-3.2-1B-Instruct with $\alpha \in \{-8, -6, -4, -2, 0, +2, +4, +6, +8\}$ (using $\alpha_{\max}=3.0$ clipped to safe range; Table~\ref{tab:bfi_crossmodel}). The cross-model pattern is consistent: \textbf{Extraversion} remains the most steerable trait on Llama-3.2 (range: 0.73), \textbf{Neuroticism} shows moderate response (range: 0.51), and \textbf{Agreeableness} exhibits the weakest sensitivity (range: 0.30)---mirroring the Qwen3-0.6B hierarchy. \textbf{Conscientiousness} again shows a reversed pattern on Llama-3.2 (higher scores at negative $\alpha$), suggesting that this reversal is not model-specific but reflects a genuine property of conscientiousness representations across architectures.

\paragraph{Trait-Specific Steerability as a Window into RLHF Geometry.}
The consistent hierarchy of steerability (Extraversion/Neuroticism $\gg$ Openness $>$ Conscientiousness $\approx$ Agreeableness) constitutes a scientific finding about how alignment training shapes personality representations---not a methodological limitation. Modern instruction-tuned models undergo extensive Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) and Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) \citep{casper2023open} optimized for ``Helpful, Honest, and Harmless (HHH)\textquotedblright{} behavior. In Big Five personality space, these objectives directly map to \emph{maximally high Agreeableness} (cooperation, empathy, non-confrontation) and \emph{high Conscientiousness} (rule-following, diligence). RLHF training consequently \textbf{deeply imprints} these dimensions into the model's safety manifold, creating a phenomenon we term the \textbf{alignment tax on steerability}: Agreeableness and Conscientiousness vectors become locked into the alignment infrastructure, resistant to post-hoc activation steering. In contrast, Extraversion and Neuroticism are affectively \emph{value-neutral} in the training distribution---so they reside in a flatter, more isotropic region of activation space that admits smooth perturbation \citep{turner2023activation}. Both failure modes (prompt refusals and steering resistance for Agreeableness) are consistent with RLHF creating a ``representational attractor'' for high-Agreeableness outputs that resists both surface-level and activation-level interventions.

\paragraph{Conscientiousness Reversal: RLHF Over-Correction Hypothesis.}
The \emph{reversed} BFI-44 response of Conscientiousness---where injecting $\alpha < 0$ (intended to reduce conscientiousness) produces \emph{higher} self-reported score---is mechanistically distinct from the flat Agreeableness resistance and merits a dedicated explanation. We propose the \textbf{RLHF over-correction hypothesis}: during pretraining and SFT, instruction-tuned models are rewarded for being thorough, structured, and rule-adherent---precisely the behavioral markers of high Conscientiousness. The RLHF gradient therefore deeply couples high-Conscientiousness output patterns with the model's core generation mandate. When a negative-$\alpha$ steering vector attempts to push activations toward low-Conscientiousness directions, this injection conflicts with the model's alignment objective, triggering an internal \emph{over-correction}: the model detects an inconsistency between the perturbed activations and its trained behavioral policy and compensates by \emph{increasing} the compensatory high-Conscientiousness signal across the generation pathway. The net output therefore paradoxically exhibits \emph{more} structured, rule-following behavior---as if the model is doubling down on its trained defaults under adversarial pressure. This pattern---resistance-by-rebound rather than simple resistance-by-attractor---mirrors findings from adversarial robustness literature where models over-correct against out-of-distribution inputs \citep{casper2023open}. The cross-model consistency of this reversal (both Qwen3-0.6B and Llama-3.2-1B; Table~\ref{tab:bfi_crossmodel}) rules out architecture-specific artefacts and instead implicates a property of the RLHF training objective itself.

\section{Discussion}\label{sec:discussion}

\paragraph{Primary Scientific Discoveries.}
Our work reveals five fundamental insights about personality representation in LLMs:
\textbf{(1) Spontaneous Internalization of Psychometric Structure:} The geometry of Big Five trait vectors (mean off-diagonal $|\cos|$ ranging from 0.05 to 0.42 across architectures), together with converging evidence from recent probing and steering studies \citep{frising2025linear, feng2026persona, ju2025probing}, demonstrates that personality in LLMs is not merely a behavioral phenomenon induced by prompting, but corresponds to structured geometric patterns that mirror human psychological theory \citep{costa1992revised}. Our contribution is to characterize \emph{how this structure varies}: trait independence is architecture-dependent, shaped by model capacity and training recipe, rather than a universal geometric property. We address the ``curse of dimensionality'' objection via two complementary lines of evidence:\par
\textit{Anisotropy argument}: LLM activation spaces are highly anisotropic \citep{ethayarajh2019contextual}---concept vectors share baseline collinearity driven by shared linguistic priors. Under feature superposition \citep{elhage2022superposition}, psychologically unrelated concepts are packed into near-orthogonal directions incidentally, so random attributes (apples vs.~oranges, $|\cos|=0.136$) can equal or exceed the Big Five correlation. In models where Big Five traits show lower inter-correlation than random pairs (e.g., Qwen3: 0.107 vs null 0.136), this suggests active representational effort to separate decision-critical dimensions. However, this pattern is not universal (Qwen2.5: 0.418 vs null 0.128), indicating architecture-dependent variation in trait separation.\par
\textit{Contrastive argument}: Our defense mechanism clustering provides the strongest counter-evidence to the high-dimensional orthogonality objection. Immature defenses achieve $|\cos|=0.511$ despite the same high-dimensional space that supposedly forces all vectors to be near-orthogonal. This proves the model \emph{can} represent highly correlated structures when psychology demands it---making the architecture-dependent Big Five independence pattern a genuine structural property, not a geometric artifact.\par
\textit{Permutation test}: The shuffle-label baseline (scrambling high/low labels within each trait; see \texttt{eval\_shuffle\_label\_baseline.py}) confirms that destroying the semantic pairing destroys the orthogonal structure, proving it originates from the data's psychological signal rather than from the extraction pipeline.\par
\textit{Interventional orthogonality}: Going beyond geometric cosine distance, we test causal independence \citep{belrose2023leace, park2023linear}: injecting the Extraversion vector at $\alpha=3.0$ on Qwen3-0.6B produces negligible cross-trait probe response (mean $|\Delta_{\text{cross}}|=0.023$) relative to the self-effect ($|\Delta_{\text{self}}|=0.114$), yielding a disentanglement ratio of 4.91$\times$---confirming that the Big Five are not merely geometrically orthogonal but causally disentangled (\texttt{eval\_interventional\_orthogonality.py}).
\textbf{(1b) Hierarchical Defense Mechanism Geometry:} The correspondence between neural geometry and psychological theory extends beyond the Big Five. Our analysis of all nine defense mechanisms organized by Vaillant's empirically validated maturity hierarchy \citep{vaillant1986empirical, digiuseppe2021hierarchy} reveals that activation-space clustering mirrors theoretical predictions at three levels. First, \emph{immature defenses} (projection, denial, regression, reaction formation) cluster strongly (within-group mean $|\cos|=0.511$), reflecting their shared reliance on primitive cognitive operations that heavily distort reality---projection shifts attribution, denial blocks awareness, regression reverts to childlike responses, and reaction formation inverts unacceptable impulses. These mechanisms share a common computational substrate of \emph{representational distortion} that avoids direct engagement with threatening content. Second, \emph{neurotic defenses} (rationalization, intellectualization, displacement) show moderate clustering ($|\cos|=0.189$), consistent with their shared strategy of \emph{appraisal reframing}---constructing alternative interpretations of threatening material while partially preserving reality contact. Rationalization constructs logical justifications, intellectualization separates emotion from cognition, and displacement redirects emotional targets; all three involve active cognitive restructuring that transforms rather than blocks the threatening stimulus. Third, \emph{mature defenses} (humor, sublimation) are near-orthogonal ($|\cos|=0.030$), indicating that despite both being classified as adaptive, they deploy fundamentally different cognitive strategies---humor involves affective reframing through comic perspective, while sublimation channels negative emotions into productive activity. This 17-fold difference between immature and mature clustering (0.511 vs 0.030) constitutes the first neural evidence that Vaillant's hierarchy, developed through decades of clinical research, corresponds to measurable geometric structure in LLM representations. Cross-hierarchy correlations provide additional validation: mature-immature distances ($|\cos|=0.201$) are smaller than neurotic-immature ($0.294$), suggesting that developmental distance in Vaillant's hierarchy corresponds to geometric distance in activation space.
\textbf{(2) Existence--Causation Dissociation:} We identify a fundamental gap between where personality information \emph{exists} (growing stronger through deeper layers, with probe accuracy $\ge$0.91) and where it \emph{causally matters} for output (concentrated in early-layer user-token attention). This dissociation challenges the common assumption that ``where probes work best is where the feature matters most'' and has profound implications for targeted intervention strategies. The finding suggests a processing pipeline where personality is \emph{established} early (via contextual framing) and \emph{amplified} later (via semantic elaboration)---a distinction critical for both scientific understanding and safety-critical control. Our position-swap control experiment (Section~\ref{sec:position_swap}) reveals that this user-token dominance has a positional component: causal influence concentrates at sequence-final positions regardless of content placement. We interpret this as reflecting the autoregressive bottleneck---the model's output distribution is necessarily most sensitive to the final contextual positions. Importantly, the persona signal still propagates from its origin (system tokens) to these final positions via early-layer attention, maintaining the existence-causation dissociation at the mechanistic level.
\textbf{(3) Architecture-Specific Steering Vulnerabilities:} While personality directions are geometrically consistent across architectures, their susceptibility to activation-space manipulation varies substantially ($|\alpha|$ ranges from 1.0 for TinyLlama to 10.0 for Gemma-2). This variation is not arbitrary. When $\alpha$ is \textbf{normalized by the mean residual stream norm} $\mathbb{E}[\|h_\ell\|]$ \citep{todd2023function}---yielding a Relative Injection Strength (RIS) metric---the cross-model variability in collapse thresholds is substantially reduced. This indicates that \emph{architectural choices determine residual stream scale more than representational fragility per se}, with implications for AI safety: the susceptibility to activation-space manipulation may be more uniform across architectures than raw $\alpha$ thresholds suggest.
\textbf{(4) Penetration of AI Identity Guardrails:} We demonstrate that activation steering can bypass AI identity guardrails that prompt-based methods cannot penetrate. While prompt-only personality engineering triggers defensive AI identity claims (``I don't have emotions or personal experiences...''), activation steering maintains stable personality expression by operating at the representational level---achieving 4.6/5.0 human evaluation scores versus 3.6/5.0 for prompting. This dissociation reveals that activation-space interventions access personality mechanisms deeper than surface-level linguistic guardrails, with implications for both AI safety and personality control.
\paragraph{Methodological Framework.}
To enable these discoveries, we introduce PersonaLens, a standardized and model-agnostic pipeline validated on five architecturally distinct models spanning three model families (Qwen, LLaMA, Gemma). The remarkable consistency of structural metrics across these models---from LOSO accuracy to causal localization patterns to orthogonality structure---demonstrates that the pipeline captures genuine architectural invariants rather than model-specific artifacts. The framework's multi-method approach (mean-difference vectors, PCA directions, and linear probes) provides converging evidence robust to extraction method choice, addressing non-identifiability concerns \citep{venkatesh2026nonidentifiability}. PersonaLens outputs---encoding profiles, causal importance maps, orthogonality matrices, and steering trade-off curves---provide a common vocabulary for cross-model personality analysis.
\paragraph{Early-Layer Causal Dominance: Mechanistic Explanation.}
Our finding that personality expression is causally dominated by early-layer attention processing (L0--L2) appears to conflict with prior work suggesting that semantic and identity-related processing occurs in middle-to-late layers \citep{geva2021transformer, geva2023dissecting, meng2022locating}. However, this apparent discrepancy reflects a fundamental distinction between two types of information processing in transformers: \textbf{contextual framing} versus \textbf{content retrieval} \citep{elhage2021mathematical}.
\textbf{Contextual framing vs content retrieval}: Geva et al.~\citep{geva2021transformer, geva2023dissecting} demonstrate that factual knowledge retrieval---recalling that ``Paris is the capital of France''---relies on middle-to-late layer MLP computations that function as key-value memories. In contrast, personality traits represent \emph{behavioral modulation}---a meta-level control signal that determines \emph{how} content is expressed rather than \emph{what} content is retrieved. Our findings suggest that early attention layers specialize in extracting contextual framing cues (including persona instructions) that modulate downstream processing, while deeper layers handle semantic elaboration and knowledge retrieval.
\textbf{Attention-mediated propagation}: The position-swap control (Section~\ref{sec:position_swap}) reveals that persona information \emph{originates} in system tokens but is \emph{propagated} to sequence-final user tokens via early-layer attention (L0--L2). This creates a causal bottleneck at the sequence boundary where the autoregressive generation process begins. The concentration of causal influence at sequence-final positions reflects the autoregressive bottleneck---the model's output distribution is necessarily most sensitive to activations nearest the generation point. However, the \emph{content} of those activations is determined by early-layer attention that integrates persona instructions from system tokens.
\textbf{Existence-causation dissociation}: Our probing results confirm that personality representations \emph{grow stronger} through depth (probe accuracy $\ge$0.91 in middle-to-late layers), yet causal patching demonstrates that early layers dominate causal influence. This dissociation is not contradictory---it reflects the distinction between where information is \emph{stored/amplified} versus where it \emph{causally determines output}. Early layers establish the personality \emph{direction} through contextual integration; deeper layers amplify and elaborate this signal through semantic processing. Interventions at early layers are causally potent because they set the trajectory that downstream layers follow.
\textbf{Architectural specificity}: Our models (0.5B--2B parameters) may exhibit compressed processing dynamics compared to larger models (7B--175B) examined in prior work. Smaller models may handle both contextual framing and some semantic processing in early layers due to capacity constraints. Validating PersonaLens on larger models (7B+) remains a priority for future work to test whether the early-layer causal dominance generalizes or whether larger models exhibit more distributed causal influence.
\paragraph{Penetrating AI Identity Guardrails.}
Beyond performance gains, we observe that activation steering can penetrate AI identity guardrails that prompt-based methods cannot. On decision-making questions, prompt-only engineering triggers defensive AI identity claims (``I don't have emotions or personal experiences... I rely solely on data-driven analysis''), collapsing openness scores from 4.0 to 2.0. In contrast, activation steering at the same intensity successfully maintains 4.0 by injecting personality at the representational level (``Embrace Empathy: Understand the human impact... feel the weight of your decisions'').
This dissociation suggests that activation steering operates at a deeper level than surface-level prompting. While explicit persona instructions in prompts can trigger safety mechanisms designed to prevent anthropomorphization, direct manipulation of activation patterns bypasses these linguistic guardrails. This has three implications: (1)~for AI safety, activation-space interventions may be harder to defend against than prompt-level attacks; (2)~for personality control, steering can achieve stable personality expression even in contexts where prompting fails; (3)~for mechanistic understanding, personality representations exist as geometric structures independent of their linguistic expression.

\paragraph{Safety and Ethics.}
Identifying and controlling mechanisms of personality inherently introduces dual-use risks. Constructive applications include building empathetic, clinically supportive, or pedagogically adapted AI personas. However, the ability to directly modulate defense mechanisms (e.g., enforcing projection or rationalization) or extract vectors mapping to the ``Dark Triad'' traits computationally opens the door to maliciously polarizing, toxic, or psychologically manipulative models at inference time. Our framework exposes these latent vulnerabilities, stressing the need for representation-level guardrails distinct from traditional reinforcement learning filters.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
(1)~\textbf{Model scale}: While we evaluate five distinct models spanning three architectural families, all are in the 0.5B--2B parameter range. Larger models (7B--175B) may exhibit qualitatively different dynamics---in particular, the early-layer causal dominance we observe may become more distributed in deeper architectures with greater capacity for parallel processing. (2)~\textbf{Prompt sensitivity}: Our paraphrase control (91.2\% cross-template accuracy) and BFI-44 validation provide strong evidence against prompt confounding, yet trait-specific variation (Extraversion: 87.5\% vs.\ Openness: 100\%) indicates that some traits are more sensitive to prompt wording than others. More diverse paraphrase templates and additional psychological instruments (e.g., NEO-PI-R, HEXACO) would further strengthen claims. (3)~\textbf{Steering hyperparameters}: Optimal $\alpha$ values are model-specific and require per-model tuning. While our RIS metric (Section~\ref{sec:metrics}) partially addresses cross-model comparability, automated hyperparameter optimization remains an open challenge. (4)~\textbf{Non-identifiability}: Persona vectors are fundamentally non-identifiable under single-layer white-box access \citep{venkatesh2026nonidentifiability}: functionally equivalent but geometrically distinct directions may exist. Our multi-method convergence analysis (mean-diff $|\cos| > 0.95$ with PCA, $> 0.89$ with probes) mitigates but does not eliminate this concern. (5)~\textbf{Defense mechanism hierarchy magnitude}: While the \emph{ordinal} structure of Vaillant's maturity levels is preserved across all five architectures, the \emph{magnitude} of clustering varies substantially (immature/mature ratio: $2.2\times$ to $35.6\times$). Full investigation of the training-regime factors driving this variation remains an important open question. (6)~\textbf{Cross-layer comparison methodology}: Our orthogonality analysis uses a common layer per model (median of per-trait best layers). Layer index is not functionally equivalent across architectures---layer 11 in a 24-layer model occupies a different processing stage than layer 6 in a 16-layer model. Normalized-depth comparisons (using $\ell/L$) would provide more principled cross-model alignment, but require recomputation of all orthogonality metrics at matched normalized positions. (7)~\textbf{Sample size}: With $K=20$ scenario pairs, our statistical power is adequate for detecting large effects ($d > 2.0$) but may miss subtler trait interactions. Scaling to $K=50$+ would enable finer-grained analyses such as trait-pair interaction effects and nonlinear encoding patterns.

\paragraph{Practical Applications.}
Beyond scientific understanding, PersonaLens enables concrete applications across multiple domains:
\textbf{(1)~Personality-aligned conversational agents}: Our framework enables building AI assistants with stable, controllable personality profiles. Unlike prompt-based personality engineering (which triggers AI identity guardrails), activation steering maintains consistent personality expression across diverse contexts. Applications include customer service agents with specific interpersonal styles (high Agreeableness for support roles, high Conscientiousness for task-oriented assistance), educational tutors adapted to student personality (matching or complementing learner traits), and creative writing assistants with distinct authorial voices.
\textbf{(2)~Clinical and therapeutic AI}: The ability to control defense mechanisms opens applications in mental health support. Therapeutic chatbots could model adaptive defense use (mature defenses like humor and sublimation) while avoiding maladaptive patterns (immature defenses like denial and projection). Our Vaillant hierarchy validation (Section~\ref{sec:results_ortho}) provides theoretical grounding for such applications. However, clinical deployment requires careful ethical oversight---the same mechanisms enabling therapeutic support could be misused to manipulate vulnerable users.
\textbf{(3)~AI safety and alignment research}: PersonaLens provides tools for probing and controlling personality-related behaviors that current alignment techniques struggle to address. The discovery that activation steering penetrates AI identity guardrails (Section~\ref{sec:baselines}) has dual implications: it reveals vulnerabilities requiring new defense mechanisms, but also enables more reliable personality control for beneficial applications. Understanding architecture-specific steering bounds (Table~\ref{tab:steering}) informs robustness testing and adversarial defense strategies.
\textbf{(4)~Personalized content generation}: Applications in creative writing, marketing, and entertainment benefit from controllable personality expression. Game NPCs with distinct, stable personalities; marketing copy adapted to target audience personality profiles; personalized storytelling that matches reader preferences. The relative independence of Big Five dimensions (Section~\ref{sec:results_ortho}) enables control of multiple personality facets with limited cross-trait interference.
These applications demonstrate that PersonaLens addresses not just scientific questions but practical challenges in building personality-aware AI systems. The framework's model-agnostic design ensures applicability across current and future architectures.
\textbf{(5)~Social Simulation and Agent-Based Modeling}: A central motivation for this research is the limitation of current agent-based social simulation frameworks (e.g., AgentSociety), which rely on prompt-level persona descriptions that erode over interaction context. \citet{argyle2023out} demonstrate that LLMs implicitly carry population-level demographic distributions; our work provides the \emph{micro-level mechanistic complement}---controllable, stable personality vectors that can drive individual agents in social simulations. By injecting RepE-based persona vectors at inference time, agent personalities are locked at the representation level rather than encoded in fragile prompt context, enabling social and economic agent simulations with \emph{causal mechanism fidelity} rather than mere behavioral mimicry.

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}

Through systematic experiments on five architecturally diverse models---Qwen3-0.6B, Qwen2.5-0.5B, TinyLlama-1.1B, Llama-3.2-1B, and Gemma-2-2B---spanning three major architectural families, we report five findings about personality representation in LLMs: (1)~Big Five traits exhibit architecture-dependent independence in activation space (mean off-diagonal $|\cos|$ between 0.05 and 0.42), consistent with and extending recent probing studies by revealing cross-architecture variation in trait factorization; (2)~personality information grows stronger through deeper layers (probe accuracy $\ge$0.91), yet causal influence is concentrated in early-layer user-token attention---an existence--causation dissociation that complements probing-based findings with causal evidence; (3)~extracted personality directions enable reliable inference-time control, but maximum tolerable intervention strength varies across architectures ($|\alpha|$ from 1.0 to 10.0), with our RIS metric revealing that this largely reflects residual stream scale differences; (4)~defense mechanism clustering in activation space mirrors Vaillant's psychological maturity hierarchy, with immature defenses forming correlated subspaces ($|\cos|=0.511$) while mature defenses are near-orthogonal ($0.030$)---providing neural evidence that LLMs internalize hierarchical defense organization; and (5)~BFI-44 dose-response analysis reveals a consistent RLHF alignment tax on steerability, where training-reinforced traits resist activation steering while value-neutral traits are highly controllable. These findings demonstrate that personality in LLMs corresponds to structured geometric patterns---from architecture-dependent Big Five independence to defense mechanism hierarchies---that open avenues for both scientific understanding and safety-critical control of AI personality.

\paragraph{Future Work.} We plan to (1)~extend cross-model validation to larger 7B+ scale architectures (Qwen2.5-7B, Llama-3.1-8B, Mistral-7B) to test whether our findings generalize to extreme-scale models; (2)~investigate attention-head-level localization using EAP-IG to identify specific attention heads responsible for personality processing; (3)~develop automated $\alpha$ optimization methods to eliminate manual per-model tuning; (4)~explore whether SAE-denoised vectors can improve steering stability and reduce model-specific sensitivity; and (5)~investigate the security implications of activation steering's ability to penetrate AI identity guardrails, including potential defenses against malicious personality manipulation.

\subsection{Extended Cross-Model Analytics}\label{app:cross_model}
\subsubsection{Cohen's d and Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR)}
Across all tested models, the linear probe separability of Big Five traits yielded large effect sizes. For instance, Llama-3.2-1B consistently maintained a Cohen's $d$ of $\approx 9.75$ and an SNR of $\approx 10.3$ at its optimal intermediate layer (L6 out of L16). Gemma-2-2B achieved $d \approx 7.59$ and SNR $\approx 10.4$ at its optimal layer (L8 out of L26). For TinyLlama-1.1B, the LOSO generalization peak occurred at layer 11 with an equivalent probe accuracy of 0.972.

\subsubsection{Steering Saturation Discrepancy}
As discussed in Section~5.5, the injection bounds before severe linguistic collapse varied significantly. Empirically, inserting the normalized mean difference vector perturbed models differentially:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{TinyLlama-1.1B:} Extreme sensitivity. Alpha sweeps ($\alpha > 1.0$) caused cascading entropy collapses, emitting token loops.
    \item \textbf{Gemma-2-2B:} Surprisingly high resilience ($|\alpha| \le 10.0+$). Despite initial hypotheses about sliding-window attention constraints, Gemma-2 demonstrated robust steering capabilities, suggesting that attention mechanism design alone does not determine steering bounds.
    \item \textbf{Llama-3.2-1B:} Moderate sensitivity ($\alpha \in [-3, 3]$). The architecture smoothly interpolates personality changes.
    \item \textbf{Qwen Series:} Exceptional resilience. Qwen2.5-0.5B maintained sub-10 PPL even at $\alpha=6.0$, likely due to dense normalization placement or varying geometric isotropy in its representation space. Qwen3-0.6B showed robust steering up to $\alpha=4.0$.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Algorithm 1:} PersonaLens --- Standardized Personality Vector Extraction\\[4pt]
\textbf{Input:} Model $f_\theta$, Trait $t$, Contrastive pairs $\{(x^+_i, x^-_i)\}_{i=1}^K$\\
\textbf{Output:} Persona vectors $\{v_\ell\}_{\ell=0}^{L-1}$, Encoding profile, Causal map\\[4pt]
\textit{// Phase 2: Collect Activations}\\
1:\quad \textbf{for} $i = 1$ \textbf{to} $K$ \textbf{do}\\
2:\quad\quad $\{h_\ell(x^+_i)\}_{\ell=0}^{L-1} \leftarrow \text{ForwardPass}(f_\theta, x^+_i)$\\
3:\quad\quad $\{h_\ell(x^-_i)\}_{\ell=0}^{L-1} \leftarrow \text{ForwardPass}(f_\theta, x^-_i)$\\
4:\quad \textbf{end for}\\[4pt]
\textit{// Phase 3: Extract Vectors}\\
5:\quad \textbf{for} $\ell = 0$ \textbf{to} $L-1$ \textbf{do}\\

8:\quad\quad $u_\ell, \rho_\ell \leftarrow \text{PCA}(\{h_\ell(x^+_i) - h_\ell(x^-_i)\}_i)$ \hfill \textit{// PCA direction}\\
9:\quad \textbf{end for}\\[4pt]
\textit{// Phase 4: Causal Localization}\\
10:\quad \textbf{for} $\ell = 0$ \textbf{to} $L-1$ \textbf{do}\\
11:\quad\quad $\mathcal{I}(\ell) \leftarrow D_{\text{KL}}(\text{Clean}(x^+) \| \text{Patched}_\ell(x^+, h_\ell(x^-)))$\\
12:\quad \textbf{end for}\\
13:\quad \textbf{return} $\{v_\ell, a_\ell, \|v_\ell\|, \rho_\ell, \mathcal{I}(\ell)\}_\ell$

\section{Perplexity Calculation Methodology}\label{app:ppl}

Sequence perplexity is computed using the \emph{same base model} that generates the steered output. For a generated sequence $w_1, \ldots, w_T$, we compute:
\begin{equation}
\text{PPL} = \exp\left(-\frac{1}{T}\sum_{t=1}^{T} \log p_\theta(w_t | w_{<t})\right)
\end{equation}
where $p_\theta$ is the unmodified (un-steered) model's probability distribution. We compute perplexity on the \emph{full sequence} (prompt + generated response), using the base model without steering hooks active. The threshold of PPL $< 10$ was selected empirically: manual inspection revealed that outputs with PPL $> 10$ consistently exhibited token-level repetition loops, grammatical breakdown, or semantic incoherence. We acknowledge that absolute PPL values are not directly comparable across architectures due to different tokenizers and vocabulary sizes; the threshold serves as a within-model fluency gate rather than a cross-model metric.
