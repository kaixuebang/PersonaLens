\begin{abstract}
Recent work has established that Big Five personality traits can be linearly extracted and steered in LLMs, but has focused on single models---leaving open the question of \emph{cross-model universality}. We conduct the first systematic cross-model analysis of personality representations across \textbf{eight} instruction-tuned LLMs spanning four architectural families (Qwen, LLaMA, Gemma, Mistral; 0.5B--7B parameters). We report four findings:

\textbf{(1)~Extraction is universal, steering is not:} All eight models achieve near-perfect LOSO extraction accuracy ($\ge$0.89), yet steering effectiveness (behavioral shift $\Delta$) varies 11$\times$ across architectures (Mistral-7B: $\Delta$=2.23 vs.\ Qwen2.5-7B: $\Delta$=0.20 at $\alpha{\in}[-6,6]$).

\textbf{(2)~The Perturbation Ratio Problem:} All steering vectors are unit-normalized ($\|v\|{=}1$), but hidden state magnitude $\|h\|$ varies 57$\times$ across models (Mistral-7B: RMS=0.051 vs.\ Gemma-2B: RMS=2.91). At standard $\alpha{=}6$, the effective perturbation $\alpha/\|h\|$ ranges from 4\% (Gemma-2B) to 183\% (Mistral-7B). This scale mismatch explains much of the cross-model steering variance ($r{=}-0.54$ between RMS scale and steering $\Delta$).

\textbf{(3)~Perturbation Ratio is Necessary but Not Sufficient:} Adjusted-alpha experiments that equalize perturbation ratios rescue Gemma-2B ($\Delta$: 0.39$\to$2.31, +492\%) and Qwen3-0.6B (+402\%), but \emph{worsen} monotonicity for Qwen2.5-1.5B ($r$: 0.38$\to$0.20) and barely improve Qwen2.5-7B ($\Delta$=0.62). Representation entanglement, not just perturbation magnitude, limits steerability.

\textbf{(4)~The Qwen Scaling Paradox:} Within the Qwen2.5 family, extraction quality \emph{improves} with scale (LOSO: 0.89$\to$1.00) while steering effectiveness \emph{degrades} ($\Delta$: 0.98$\to$0.20), even after perturbation-ratio correction---suggesting that representation entanglement increases with model capacity in this family.

These findings establish that the perturbation ratio must be a \emph{standard control variable} in activation steering research, and that cross-model analysis reveals fundamental architectural differences invisible to single-model studies.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}

Large Language Models (LLMs) readily adopt distinct \emph{personas} when instructed through system prompts, producing text that reflects specific personality traits \citep{shanahan2023role}. This ability has enabled applications ranging from immersive role-playing \citep{chen2024rolesurvey} to personality-consistent dialogue agents \citep{shao2023character}. However, a fundamental question remains: \textbf{do these personality behaviors correspond to structured internal representations, and can we identify and control them mechanistically?}

Prior work on LLM personality has largely taken a \emph{behavioral} perspective: evaluating model outputs against psychometric instruments such as the Big Five \citep{jiang2024evaluating}, or engineering prompts to elicit desired traits \citep{serapiogarcia2023personality}. While valuable, this approach treats the model as a black box and cannot answer whether personality traits correspond to identifiable neural structures.

Meanwhile, the field of \emph{representation engineering} \citep{zou2023representation} has demonstrated that semantic concepts---truthfulness \citep{li2024iti}, harmlessness \citep{rimsky2024steering}, and emotional states \citep{bao2024emotion}---correspond to linear directions in activation space that can be both probed and manipulated. Recent work has begun extending this paradigm to personality: persona vectors \citep{chen2025persona}, activation-space personality steering \citep{sun2025activation}, and persona blending \citep{billy2025blending} all suggest that personality traits may occupy distinct linear subspaces within transformer representations.

\citet{feng2026persona} demonstrated that personality vectors work well on \emph{a single model} (LLaMA-3-8B), establishing contrastive extraction, approximate orthogonality, and linear dose-response. But a critical question remains unanswered: \textbf{do these findings generalize across architectures, and if not, what governs the differences?}

\paragraph{Research Questions.} This work addresses three questions:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,topsep=2pt,itemsep=1pt]
    \item \textbf{Q1 (Universality):} Are personality representations equally extractable and steerable across diverse LLM architectures, or does effectiveness depend on model-specific properties?
    \item \textbf{Q2 (Perturbation Ratio):} Why does steering effectiveness vary across models---is it a fundamental representational difference, or a methodological artifact of how steering vectors interact with different activation scales?
    \item \textbf{Q3 (Scaling):} Does scaling up a model family improve personality steerability, as one might expect from richer representations?
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Contributions.} We analyze eight instruction-tuned LLMs (0.5B--7B parameters, four architectural families) and report four findings:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,topsep=2pt,itemsep=1pt]
\item \textbf{Extraction is universal, steering is not} (Sec.~\ref{sec:results}): All eight models achieve near-perfect extraction (LOSO $\ge$0.89), yet steering effectiveness varies 11$\times$ (Mistral-7B: $\Delta$=2.23 vs.\ Qwen2.5-7B: $\Delta$=0.20).
\item \textbf{The Perturbation Ratio Problem} (Sec.~\ref{sec:perturbation}): Steering vectors are unit-normalized while hidden state magnitudes vary 57$\times$, creating a systematic scale mismatch that explains much of the cross-model steering variance.
\item \textbf{Perturbation Ratio is Necessary but Not Sufficient} (Sec.~\ref{sec:adjusted_alpha}): Adjusted-alpha ablations show that equalizing perturbation ratios rescues some models (+492\% for Gemma-2B) but worsens monotonicity for others, revealing a second factor: representation entanglement.
\item \textbf{The Qwen Scaling Paradox} (Sec.~\ref{sec:qwen_paradox}): Within the Qwen2.5 family, extraction quality \emph{improves} with scale while steering effectiveness \emph{degrades}, even after perturbation-ratio correction.
\end{enumerate}
We additionally provide: architecture-dependent orthogonality analysis (Sec.~\ref{sec:orthogonality}), causal localization (Sec.~\ref{sec:localization}), defense mechanism geometry (Appendix~\ref{app:defense}), and multi-framework analysis (Appendix~\ref{app:multiframework}).

\section{Related Work}\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Personality in LLMs.}
The study of personality expression in LLMs has expanded rapidly \citep{serapiogarcia2023personality, mao2024personality, personaforge2025}. A growing body of work applies representation engineering specifically to personality: linear probing and steering grounded in Big Five psychometric scales \citep{frising2025linear}, activation vector algebra for compositional personality control \citep{feng2026persona}, hybrid layer selection for stable trait control \citep{bhandari2026activation}, and persona vector extraction for personality steering \citep{chen2025persona, billy2025blending}. \citet{ju2025probing} conduct the most comprehensive layer-wise probing study to date, finding personality predominantly encoded in middle and upper layers across 11 models.

\paragraph{Our Position Relative to PERSONA.}
The most closely related work, PERSONA \citep{feng2026persona}, demonstrated personality extraction and steering on \emph{a single model} (LLaMA-3-8B). Our work extends this foundation to \emph{eight models} across four architectural families, revealing that cross-model analysis exposes fundamental phenomena invisible to single-model studies. While PERSONA established that personality steering \emph{can work}, we identify \emph{why it works better on some architectures than others}---the perturbation ratio problem. Our work builds on PERSONA's contrastive activation methodology but adds: (1)~causal localization via activation patching; (2)~cross-model orthogonality analysis revealing architecture-dependent variation; (3)~the perturbation ratio metric as a critical methodological control; and (4)~the Qwen scaling paradox demonstrating that model scale does not guarantee improved steerability.

\paragraph{Representation Engineering and Steering.}
Representation Engineering (RepE) \citep{zou2023representation} establishes that high-level concepts correspond to linear directions in activation space. Inference-Time Intervention (ITI) \citep{li2024iti} demonstrates that shifting activations along truthful directions improves model truthfulness. Contrastive Activation Addition \citep{rimsky2024steering, turner2023activation} generalizes this to behavioral attributes. Recent advances investigate context-dependent control via Steering Vector Fields (SVF) \citep{svf2026} and formalize the algebraic structure of these directions through factored subspaces \citep{factored2026}. EasySteer \citep{xu2025easysteer} provides production-scale steering infrastructure; PersonaLens provides the complementary \emph{scientific foundation}.

\paragraph{Reliability and Theoretical Concerns.}
From Traits to Circuits \citep{bao2025traits2circuits} proposes identifying ``personality circuits'' as minimal subgraphs. Empirical evaluations highlight OOD transfer fragility and collateral capability degradation during concept activation steering \citep{cae2025, cae2025b}. To improve reliability, SAE-denoised vectors \citep{sae2025denoising} and magnitude-direction dissociations \citep{direction2026} have been explored. \citet{venkatesh2026nonidentifiability} raise non-identifiability concerns under single-layer access; our multi-method convergence mitigates but does not eliminate this concern.

% ============================================================
\subsection{Phase 1: Contrastive Data Construction}\label{sec:data}

Following the contrastive activation paradigm \citep{zou2023representation, rimsky2024steering}, we construct paired prompts designed to elicit contrasting personality states while keeping the conversational scenario identical.

\paragraph{Big Five Traits.}
For each of the five personality dimensions---Openness (O), Conscientiousness (C), Extraversion (E), Agreeableness (A), and Neuroticism (N)---we define:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,topsep=2pt,itemsep=1pt]
\item A \textbf{high-trait system prompt} instructing the model to behave as a person with an extreme level of the trait;
\item A \textbf{low-trait system prompt} instructing the opposite extreme;
\item $K=20$ \textbf{neutral scenario prompts} (user messages) spanning diverse social, emotional, and decision-making contexts.
\end{itemize}

For example, an Openness pair uses the system prompts ``\textit{You are extremely open to new experiences, intellectually curious, imaginative\ldots}'' vs.\ ``\textit{You are very practical, conventional, and prefer routine\ldots}'', with shared scenarios like ``\textit{What do you think about modern abstract art?}''

\paragraph{Defense Mechanisms.}
Following Vaillant's hierarchy \citep{vaillant1994ego} and PersonaForge's operationalization \citep{personaforge2025}, we construct contrastive pairs for three representative defense mechanisms: Humor, Rationalization, and Projection. These three mechanisms were selected as they represent distinct categories in Vaillant's hierarchy (mature, neurotic, and immature defenses respectively). Each mechanism contrasts an \emph{active} condition against a \emph{neutral} condition. Each has $K=10$ scenario prompts involving interpersonal conflict, loss, or ego-threat. Qwen2.5-0.5B was additionally evaluated on all nine defense mechanisms as an extensibility check.

% ============================================================
\subsection{Phase 2: Activation Collection}\label{sec:collection}

For each contrastive pair $(x^+_i, x^-_i)$ in trait $t$, we format the messages using the model's chat template and perform a forward pass through the full model, collecting hidden states $h_\ell \in \mathbb{R}^d$ at the \emph{last token position} of every transformer layer $\ell \in \{0, 1, \ldots, L{-}1\}$.

We record hidden states before any generation occurs (at the position where the model would begin generating its response). This captures the model's ``pre-generation personality state''---the representation that determines how the model will respond.

For a model with $L$ layers and hidden dimension $d$, this produces, for each trait $t$:
\begin{equation}
H^+_t = \{h_\ell(x^+_i)\}_{i=1}^{K}, \quad H^-_t = \{h_\ell(x^-_i)\}_{i=1}^{K}, \qquad \forall \ell \in \{0, \ldots, L{-}1\}
\end{equation}

We report LOSO accuracy (Leave-One-Scenario-Out cross-validation), Cohen's $d$ effect size, and Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR); formal definitions in Appendix~\ref{app:method_details}.

% ============================================================
\subsection{Phase 3: Persona Vector Extraction}\label{sec:extraction}

We extract personality directions using three complementary methods that show strong convergence (mean $|\cos| > 0.95$ between mean-difference and PCA vectors; see Appendix~\ref{app:method_details} for convergence analysis).

\paragraph{Method 1: Mean Difference (Contrastive Activation Addition).}
The primary extraction method \citep{rimsky2024steering, chen2025persona}:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:meandiff}
v^{\text{diff}}_\ell = \frac{1}{K} \sum_{i=1}^{K} \left[ h_\ell(x^+_i) - h_\ell(x^-_i) \right], \qquad \hat{v}^{\text{diff}}_\ell = \frac{v^{\text{diff}}_\ell}{\|v^{\text{diff}}_\ell\|}
\end{equation}
To control for residual-stream scaling, we use \textbf{RMS-normalized difference norm}: $\frac{\|v^{\text{diff}}_\ell\|}{\text{RMS}_\ell}$ where $\text{RMS}_\ell = \sqrt{\frac{1}{2K}\sum (h_\ell)^2}$.

\paragraph{Methods 2--3: PCA and Linear Probing.}
We additionally extract PCA first components and LOSO-validated linear probes, finding strong convergence (mean $|\cos| > 0.95$ with PCA; see Appendix~\ref{app:method_details}). We conduct a sample size ablation comparing random ($n{=}5$) and Cartesian product ($n{=}160$) designs, finding a 2.44$\times$ increase in effect size (Appendix~\ref{app:method_details}).

% ============================================================
\subsection{Phase 4: Causal Localization via Activation Patching}\label{sec:localization}

Probing establishes where personality information \emph{exists}; activation patching \citep{meng2022locating, bao2024emotion} establishes where it \emph{causally matters}. We perform token-localized patching (system vs.\ user prompt spans) and component-level tracing (attention vs.\ MLP), measuring causal importance as KL divergence between clean and patched outputs:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:causal}
\mathcal{I}(\ell) = D_{\text{KL}}\!\left(\text{softmax}(o^+) \;\|\; \text{softmax}(o^+_{\text{patched},\ell})\right)
\end{equation}

% ============================================================
\subsection{Phase 5: Inference-Time Personality Steering}\label{sec:steering}

The culminating experiment directly tests whether controlling neuron activations controls personality. We inject persona vectors during inference using forward hooks:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:steering}
\tilde{h}_\ell = h_\ell + \alpha \cdot \hat{v}_\ell
\end{equation}
where $\alpha \in \mathbb{R}$ controls steering strength. Positive $\alpha$ pushes toward the high-trait direction; negative $\alpha$ pushes toward the low-trait direction. Critically, this intervention uses \textbf{no personality-related prompting}---the user's message is completely neutral, and all personality expression arises solely from activation manipulation.

% ============================================================
\subsection{Cross-Trait Orthogonality Analysis}\label{sec:orthogonality}

We measure trait independence via cosine similarity between persona vectors at each trait's best layer: $\text{sim}(t_i, t_j) = (\hat{v}_{t_i} \cdot \hat{v}_{t_j}) / (\|\hat{v}_{t_i}\| \cdot \|\hat{v}_{t_j}\|)$. Following the Big Five's orthogonal factor structure \citep{costa1992revised}, we expect trait independence ($|\text{sim}| \ll 1$). We focus on three representative defense mechanisms (Humor, Rationalization, Projection) for cross-model validation, with Qwen2.5-0.5B validated on the full set of nine.

% ============================================================
\subsection{The Perturbation Ratio Problem}\label{sec:perturbation_method}

A critical methodological insight emerged during our cross-model analysis: \textbf{steering vectors are unit-normalized ($\|\hat{v}\| = 1$), but hidden state magnitude varies dramatically across architectures}. This creates a systematic scale mismatch that we term the \textbf{Perturbation Ratio} problem.

\paragraph{Definition.} At a given layer $\ell$, define:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,topsep=2pt,itemsep=1pt]
\item Steering vector: $\hat{v}_\ell$ with $\|\hat{v}_\ell\| = 1$ (unit-normalized)
\item Hidden state magnitude: $\|h_\ell\|$ varies with model architecture
\item RMS scale: $\text{RMS}_\ell = \sqrt{\frac{1}{d} \sum_{j=1}^d h_{\ell,j}^2}$
\end{itemize}

The \textbf{Perturbation Ratio} (PR) at steering coefficient $\alpha$ is:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:pr}
\text{PR} = \frac{\alpha \cdot \|\hat{v}\|}{\|h\|} \approx \frac{\alpha}{\|h\|}
\end{equation}

This ratio determines the effective strength of the intervention: PR = 1.0 means the perturbation equals the hidden state magnitude; PR = 0.1 means a 10\% perturbation. Critically, at a fixed $\alpha = 6$ (a common default in the literature), PR varies 46$\times$ across our eight models---from 0.04 (4\% perturbation, Gemma-2B) to 1.83 (183\% perturbation, Mistral-7B).

\paragraph{Implications.} This scale mismatch means that:\textbf{(1)}~Comparing steering effectiveness at fixed $\alpha$ across models is misleading; \textbf{(2)}~Models with larger hidden state magnitudes (higher RMS scale) require proportionally larger $\alpha$ to achieve equivalent perturbation; \textbf{(3)}~The ``safe'' $\alpha$ range varies not just by model architecture but by the specific residual stream geometry of each model. We validate this hypothesis through adjusted-alpha ablations in Sec.~\ref{sec:adjusted_alpha}.

\section{Experimental Setup}\label{sec:setup}

\paragraph{Models.} We validate PersonaLens on eight models spanning four distinct architectural families:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,topsep=2pt,itemsep=1pt]
\item \textbf{Qwen2.5-0.5B-Instruct}: 24 layers, $d{=}896$, 0.5B params
\item \textbf{Qwen3-0.6B} \citep{qwen2025qwen3}: 28 layers, $d{=}1024$, 0.6B params
\item \textbf{TinyLlama-1.1B-Chat-v1.0} \citep{zhang2024tinyllama}: 22 layers, $d{=}2048$, 1.1B params
\item \textbf{Llama-3.2-1B-Instruct} \citep{dubey2024llama}: 16 layers, $d{=}2048$, 1.0B params
\item \textbf{Qwen2.5-1.5B-Instruct}: 28 layers, $d{=}1536$, 1.5B params
\item \textbf{Gemma-2-2B-Instruct} \citep{gemmateam2024gemma}: 26 layers, $d{=}2304$, 2.0B params
\item \textbf{Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct}: 28 layers, $d{=}3584$, 7.0B params
\item \textbf{Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.1}: 32 layers, $d{=}4096$, 7.0B params
\end{itemize}
These models span a 14$\times$ parameter range (0.5B--7B) and four architectural families (Qwen, LLaMA, Gemma, Mistral), enabling systematic analysis of scale and architectural effects. Gemma-2 does not support a system role in its chat template; our pipeline automatically handles this by merging the system prompt into the first user message.

\paragraph{Traits Analyzed.} We analyze all five Big Five dimensions (O, C, E, A, N) using $K{=}20$ scenario pairs each, and three representative defense mechanisms (Humor, Rationalization, Projection) using $K{=}10$ pairs each, totaling 130 contrastive prompt pairs per model. Qwen2.5-0.5B was additionally evaluated on all nine defense mechanisms to validate the framework's extensibility.

\paragraph{Extraction Configuration.} Linear probes use strong L2-regularized logistic regression ($C{=}0.01$) alongside rigorous \textbf{Leave-One-Scenario-Out (LOSO) cross-validation}. All metrics, including RMS normalization and Cohen's $d$, are evaluated on the held-out test configurations. \textbf{Unless otherwise specified, all steering and cosine similarity analyses use the RMS-normalized Mean-Difference vectors $\hat{v}^{\text{diff}}_\ell$}, as these proved highly collinear with the primary PCA component (mean $|\cos| > 0.95$ across all traits and models) while being computationally more stable and interpretable. Linear probes are used exclusively to evaluate representational \emph{existence} (classification accuracy and Cohen's $d$), not for steering or geometric analysis.

\paragraph{Steering Evaluation.} For steering experiments, we evaluate across $\alpha \in [-6.0, +6.0]$ using LLM-as-Judge scoring with BFI rubrics. We report two key metrics per trait: \textbf{(1)}~Steering effectiveness $\Delta = \max(\text{judge\_mean}) - \min(\text{judge\_mean})$ across alphas; \textbf{(2)}~Monotonicity correlation $r$ (Spearman) between $\alpha$ and judge ratings.

\subsection{Personality Extraction is Universal}\label{sec:extraction_universal}

\subsection{The Perturbation Ratio Problem}\label{sec:perturbation}

\paragraph{Research Question.} Why does steering effectiveness vary across models, and can this variance be explained by methodological factors rather than fundamental representational differences?

\caption{\textbf{Perturbation ratios across eight models.} RMS scale (hidden state magnitude), estimated $\|h\|$, perturbation ratio at $\alpha=6$, and steering effectiveness ($\Delta$). Higher RMS scale = smaller effective perturbation = weaker steering.}
\label{tab:perturbation_ratio}
\centering
\small
\begin{tabular}{lcccc}

\end{tabular}
\end{table}

Table~\ref{tab:perturbation_ratio} reveals a striking pattern: \textbf{RMS scale and steering effectiveness show a moderate negative correlation} ($\rho = -0.539$, $p < 0.05$), explaining approximately 29\% of cross-model variance ($R^2 = 0.29$). While this establishes scale mismatch as a significant factor, the remaining 71\% of variance points to additional architectural determinants---a question we investigate through adjusted-alpha ablations (Sec.~\ref{sec:adjusted_alpha}). Models with small RMS scales (Mistral-7B: 0.051, Llama-3.2: 0.538) achieve strong steering ($\Delta > 2.0$), while models with large RMS scales (Gemma-2B: 2.907, Q3-0.6B: 1.734) show weak steering ($\Delta < 0.5$).

The perturbation ratio (PR) quantifies this: at standard $\alpha = 6$, the effective perturbation ranges from 183\% for Mistral-7B (where the steering vector dominates the hidden state) to just 4\% for Gemma-2B (where the steering vector is a minor perturbation). This 46$\times$ range in effective intervention strength explains a substantial portion, but not all, of the cross-model steering variance.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.65\textwidth]{figures/fig_pr_scatter.png}
\caption{\textbf{Hidden state scale vs.\ steering effectiveness.} Each point is one model. The negative correlation ($\rho = -0.54$, $p < 0.05$) confirms that models with larger residual stream magnitudes achieve weaker steering at fixed $\alpha$, motivating perturbation-ratio-aware comparisons.}
\label{fig:pr_scatter}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Mechanistic interpretation.} The steering intervention in Eq.~\ref{eq:steering} adds $\alpha \hat{v}$ to hidden state $h$. When $\|h\|$ is large (high RMS scale), the same $\alpha$ produces a smaller \emph{relative} perturbation. Since the model's output distribution depends on the relative activation pattern, not absolute magnitudes, models with larger hidden state norms require proportionally larger $\alpha$ to achieve equivalent behavioral shifts.

\paragraph{Implications for the field.} This finding establishes that \textbf{perturbation ratio should be a standard control variable} in activation steering research. Comparing steering effectiveness at fixed $\alpha$ across models with different residual stream scales is methodologically invalid---it conflates true representational differences with simple scale mismatches. Future steering studies should report either: (1)~perturbation ratios alongside $\alpha$ values, or (2)~steering curves normalized by hidden state magnitude.

\subsection{Adjusted-Alpha Ablation: Necessary but Not Sufficient}\label{sec:adjusted_alpha}

\paragraph{Research Question.} If we equalize perturbation ratios across models by scaling $\alpha$ proportionally, do all models achieve equivalent steering effectiveness? Or are there additional architectural factors that limit steerability?

\textbf{Key insight 2: Q2.5-1.5B shows heterogeneous trait responses.} While the mean monotonicity $r$ drops from 0.38 to 0.20, individual traits diverge dramatically: Extraversion improves ($r$: 0.74$\to$0.89, $\Delta$: 0.38$\to$0.80), while Openness and Neuroticism flip negative ($r$: 0.05$\to$-0.36, 0.32$\to$-0.11). Larger perturbations \emph{activate} some personality directions while \emph{disrupting} others, producing non-monotonic dose-response curves that average out poorly.

\textbf{Key insight 3: Q2.5-7B hits an architectural ceiling.} Even with 5$\times$ adjusted alphas, Q2.5-7B only achieves $\Delta$=0.62---weak compared to other models. This suggests \textbf{representation entanglement that cannot be overcome by larger perturbations} alone. As models in the Qwen2.5 family scale up, their personality representations become increasingly intertwined with other features, limiting steerability regardless of intervention strength.

\paragraph{The necessary-but-not-sufficient principle.} Perturbation ratio adjustment is \textbf{necessary} to achieve fair cross-model comparisons (rescuing Gemma-2B, Q3-0.6B) but \textbf{not sufficient} to equalize steerability across all architectures. A second factor---representation entanglement---emerges as a fundamental architectural property that varies across model families and scales.

The common assumption that scale improves controllability holds for extraction but fails for steering, at least within the Qwen2.5 family. Model families may differ in their steerability-scaling properties; cross-model analysis is essential to identify these patterns.

\section{Discussion}\label{sec:discussion}

\paragraph{The Perturbation Ratio Problem: Implications for Steering Research.}
Our finding that perturbation ratio explains much of the cross-model steering variance has immediate methodological implications. Current steering literature typically reports fixed $\alpha$ values (e.g., $\alpha \in [-6, 6]$) without accounting for model-specific hidden state magnitudes. This practice is \emph{methodologically problematic without proper normalization} for cross-model comparisons. We recommend:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,topsep=2pt,itemsep=1pt]
\item Report RMS scale or mean $\|h\|$ alongside steering results
\item Report perturbation ratios (PR = $\alpha / \|h\|$) as normalized intervention strengths
\item When comparing across models, use adjusted alphas that equalize PR or report results at comparable PR values
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Necessary but Not Sufficient: The Two-Factor Model of Steerability.}
Our adjusted-alpha ablations reveal that steering effectiveness depends on \emph{two} factors: (1)~\textbf{perturbation ratio}---the relative magnitude of the intervention, and (2)~\textbf{representation entanglement}---the degree to which personality features are intertwined with other semantic features. Perturbation ratio is a methodological control; representation entanglement is a fundamental architectural property. This two-factor model explains why:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,topsep=2pt,itemsep=1pt]
\item Gemma-2B was weak at standard $\alpha$ but strong after adjustment (low entanglement, wrong PR)
\item Q2.5-7B remained weak even after adjustment (high entanglement, architectural ceiling)
\item Q2.5-1.5B showed non-monotonicity after adjustment (moderate entanglement activated by larger perturbations)
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{The Qwen Scaling Paradox and Entanglement Hypothesis.}
The Qwen2.5 family's inverse scaling pattern---better extraction, worse steering at larger scales---challenges the assumption that larger models are more controllable. We hypothesize that larger capacity enables richer, more distributed representations that are \emph{better separated} (improving extraction) but \emph{more entangled} (harming steerability). This suggests a trade-off between representational richness and controllability that varies across model families.

\paragraph{Representation Entanglement and Steerability.}
We quantify \textbf{representation entanglement} as the mean absolute cosine similarity $|\cos|$ between persona vectors at each model's best layer (Table~\ref{tab:entanglement_summary}). Values range from 0.058 (Q2.5-1.5B) to 0.192 (TinyLlama). Critically, entanglement does \emph{not} linearly predict steering effectiveness: Q2.5-1.5B has the lowest entanglement (0.058) yet remains poorly steerable, while TinyLlama has the highest (0.192) and achieves moderate steering ($\Delta$=1.22). This confirms that entanglement alone cannot explain the steering landscape---it is the \emph{interaction} between perturbation ratio, entanglement, and potentially other architectural factors (e.g., attention head distribution, layer normalization) that determines steerability.

\paragraph{Orthogonality Variation Across Architectures.}
While we focus on steering in the main results, our cross-model orthogonality analysis (Appendix~\ref{app:permodel}) reveals architecture-dependent variation in trait independence. Mean off-diagonal $|\cos|$ ranges from 0.054 (Llama-3.2) to 0.418 (Qwen2.5), suggesting that some architectures naturally factorize personality traits while others represent them in more correlated subspaces. This variation correlates weakly with steering effectiveness, suggesting that orthogonality is neither necessary nor sufficient for steerability.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
(1)~All steering experiments use linear directions (mean-difference vectors). Non-linear or multi-directional interventions might overcome entanglement limitations. (2)~Our LLM-as-Judge evaluation, while rigorous, may introduce judge-model biases. Human evaluation would strengthen claims. (3)~The perturbation ratio analysis assumes isotropic hidden state geometry; future work should investigate anisotropic corrections. (4)~Defense mechanism and multi-framework results (Appendices~\ref{app:defense}, \ref{app:multiframework}) were primarily validated on the original five models; full replication on the three new models (Q2.5-1.5B, Q2.5-7B, Mistral-7B) is ongoing.

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}

Through systematic experiments on eight instruction-tuned LLMs spanning four architectural families and a 14$\times$ parameter range (0.5B--7B), we report four findings about cross-model personality representation:

\textbf{(1)~Extraction is universal:} All eight models achieve near-perfect LOSO accuracy ($\ge$0.89), demonstrating that personality representations are a fundamental property of instruction-tuned transformers.

\textbf{(2)~The Perturbation Ratio Problem:} Steering vectors are unit-normalized, but hidden state magnitudes vary 57$\times$ across models. This scale mismatch explains much of the cross-model steering variance ($r = -0.54$), establishing perturbation ratio as a critical methodological control.

\textbf{(3)~Perturbation ratio is necessary but not sufficient:} Adjusted-alpha ablations rescue some models (+492\% for Gemma-2B) but reveal representation entanglement as a second limiting factor. Some architectures (Q2.5-7B) hit steering ceilings that larger perturbations cannot overcome.

\textbf{(4)~The Qwen Scaling Paradox:} Within the Qwen2.5 family, extraction quality improves with scale while steering degrades---even after perturbation-ratio correction---suggesting that representation entanglement increases with capacity in this family.

These findings establish that cross-model analysis reveals fundamental architectural differences invisible to single-model studies. The perturbation ratio must become a standard control variable in activation steering research. Future work should investigate: attention-head-level localization to identify specific circuits; automated $\alpha$ optimization methods; and whether SAE-based interventions can overcome representation entanglement.

\section{Method Details}\label{app:method_details}

\subsection{Metrics and Evaluation Criteria}

We evaluate personality encoding through four complementary metrics.

\paragraph{Leave-One-Scenario-Out (LOSO) Accuracy.}
Given $K$ contrastive pairs per trait, we train a linear probe on $K{-}1$ pairs and evaluate on the held-out pair. Repeating over all $K$ folds yields LOSO accuracy $a_\ell \in [0,1]$, measuring generalization to unseen contexts.

\paragraph{Cohen's $d$ Effect Size.}
At layer $\ell$, we compute standardized mean separation between high-trait and low-trait activations:
\begin{equation}
d_\ell = \frac{\mu_{+,\ell} - \mu_{-,\ell}}{\sqrt{\frac{(n_+-1)s_{+,\ell}^2 + (n_--1)s_{-,\ell}^2}{n_+ + n_- - 2}}}
\end{equation}
Values $d > 0.8$ indicate large effects; our models consistently achieve $d > 2.0$.

\paragraph{Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR).}
To distinguish genuine signal from scaling artifacts:
\begin{equation}
\text{SNR}_\ell = \frac{\|v^{\text{diff}}_\ell\| / \text{RMS}_\ell}{\mathbb{E}[\|v^{\text{random}}_\ell\| / \text{RMS}_\ell]}
\end{equation}
SNR $> 2$ indicates contrastive signal exceeds random noise; our models achieve SNR $> 7$.

\paragraph{Perturbation Ratio (PR).}
Defined in Eq.~\ref{eq:pr} as PR = $\alpha / \|h\|$, this metric normalizes intervention strength by hidden state magnitude for cross-model comparability.

\subsection{Extraction Method Convergence}

Mean-difference vectors show mean $|\cos| > 0.95$ with PCA first components and $|\cos| > 0.89$ with linear probe weights across all traits and models. This high collinearity indicates personality directions are robustly identifiable despite theoretical non-identifiability concerns.

\subsection{Statistical Power and Sample Size}

With $K=20$ scenario pairs per Big Five trait, bootstrap standard errors are $<0.02$ for accuracy and $<0.15$ for Cohen's $d$. For defense mechanisms ($K=10$), accuracy standard errors remain $<0.03$, sufficient given larger effect sizes ($d > 2.0$).

\subsection{Sample Size Ablation}

Comparing random ($n=5$) vs.\ Cartesian product ($n=160$) designs on Qwen3 Openness shows a 2.44$\times$ increase in effect size (from $d=2.69$ to $d=6.57$), establishing that prior work using $n=5$--$20$ systematically underestimates signal strength.

\section{Defense Mechanism Geometry}\label{app:defense}

Following Vaillant's hierarchy \citep{vaillant1986empirical}, we analyze nine defense mechanisms across five maturity levels. The full set of nine defense mechanisms on Qwen2.5-0.5B shows hierarchical clustering mirroring Vaillant's maturity levels: within-immature defenses cluster strongly ($|\cos| = 0.511$), neurotic defenses moderately ($0.189$), and mature defenses are near-orthogonal ($0.030$)---a 17-fold difference. All five architectures from our original study produce the identical strict ordering (Immature $>$ Neurotic $>$ Mature), with joint probability $(1/6)^5 \approx 0.00013$ under the null, providing strong evidence that the hierarchy is a universal property of instruction-tuned LLMs.

\section{Control Experiments}\label{app:control}

\subsection{Position-Swap Control}\label{app:position_swap}

To address concerns that user-token causal dominance might reflect positional artifacts (recency effect), we reverse the prompt layout: placing the neutral scenario in the system role and the persona instruction in the user role.

Results reveal that causal influence remains concentrated at sequence-final positions regardless of content placement. This confirms a positional component consistent with the autoregressive bottleneck: the model's output distribution is most sensitive to activations nearest the generation point. However, early-layer specificity persists regardless of content placement, confirming attention-mediated propagation as the operative mechanism.

\subsection{Paraphrase Control}\label{app:paraphrase}

To rule out keyword confounding, we train probes on Template A (original prompts) and test on Template B (paraphrased versions), achieving 91.2\% mean cross-template accuracy (range: 87.5--100\%). Openness achieves perfect bidirectional transfer (1.000), confirming abstract conceptual representation rather than surface lexical cues.

\subsection{Out-of-Domain Generalization}\label{app:ood}

When extracting vectors from non-overlapping 50\% splits, OOD stability is strong for larger models (Llama-3.2 mean 0.988, Gemma-2 mean 0.914) and moderate for smaller models (Qwen2.5 mean 0.801). Openness shows exceptional stability (mean $\cos=0.935$), suggesting abstract conceptual encoding, while Extraversion and Agreeableness show context-dependent encoding.

\section{Algorithm Pseudocode}\label{app:algorithm}

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\fbox{\parbox{0.92\textwidth}{\small
\textbf{Algorithm 1:} PersonaLens --- Standardized Personality Vector Extraction\\[4pt]
\textbf{Input:} Model $f_\theta$, Trait $t$, Contrastive pairs $\{(x^+_i, x^-_i)\}_{i=1}^K$\\
\textbf{Output:} Persona vectors $\{v_\ell\}_{\ell=0}^{L-1}$, Encoding profile, Causal map\\[4pt]
\textit{// Phase 2: Collect Activations}\\
1:\quad \textbf{for} $i = 1$ \textbf{to} $K$ \textbf{do}\\
2:\quad\quad $\{h_\ell(x^+_i)\}_{\ell=0}^{L-1} \leftarrow \text{ForwardPass}(f_\theta, x^+_i)$\\
3:\quad\quad $\{h_\ell(x^-_i)\}_{\ell=0}^{L-1} \leftarrow \text{ForwardPass}(f_\theta, x^-_i)$\\
4:\quad \textbf{end for}\\[4pt]
\textit{// Phase 3: Extract Vectors}\\
5:\quad \textbf{for} $\ell = 0$ \textbf{to} $L-1$ \textbf{do}\\
6:\quad\quad $v^{\text{diff}}_\ell \leftarrow \frac{1}{K}\sum_i [h_\ell(x^+_i) - h_\ell(x^-_i)]$ \hfill \textit{// Mean difference}\\
7:\quad\quad $w_\ell, a_\ell \leftarrow \text{LogisticRegression}(H^+_\ell, H^-_\ell)$ \hfill \textit{// Linear probe}\\
8:\quad\quad $u_\ell, \rho_\ell \leftarrow \text{PCA}(\{h_\ell(x^+_i) - h_\ell(x^-_i)\}_i)$ \hfill \textit{// PCA direction}\\
9:\quad \textbf{end for}\\[4pt]
\textit{// Phase 4: Causal Localization}\\
10:\quad \textbf{for} $\ell = 0$ \textbf{to} $L-1$ \textbf{do}\\
11:\quad\quad $\mathcal{I}(\ell) \leftarrow D_{\text{KL}}(\text{Clean}(x^+) \| \text{Patched}_\ell(x^+, h_\ell(x^-)))$\\
12:\quad \textbf{end for}\\
13:\quad \textbf{return} $\{v_\ell, a_\ell, \|v_\ell\|, \rho_\ell, \mathcal{I}(\ell)\}_\ell$
}}
\label{alg:personalens}
\end{figure}

\section{Perplexity Calculation Methodology}\label{app:ppl}

\begin{equation}
\text{PPL} = \exp\left(-\frac{1}{T}\sum_{t=1}^{T} \log p_\theta(w_t | w_{<t})\right)
\end{equation}
where $p_\theta$ is the unmodified (un-steered) model's probability distribution. We compute perplexity on the \emph{full sequence} (prompt + generated response), using the base model without steering hooks active. The threshold of PPL $< 10$ was selected empirically: manual inspection revealed that outputs with PPL $> 10$ consistently exhibited token-level repetition loops, grammatical breakdown, or semantic incoherence. We acknowledge that absolute PPL values are not directly comparable across architectures due to different tokenizers and vocabulary sizes; the threshold serves as a within-model fluency gate rather than a cross-model metric.
